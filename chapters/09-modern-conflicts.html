Chapter 9

Modern Conflicts

1950 -- 2000

The second half of the twentieth century transformed the Middle East into the world's most contested region. Wars, revolutions, peace processes, and assassinations played out against a backdrop of Cold War rivalries, oil politics, and competing nationalisms. This chapter traces the arc from pan-Arab idealism through devastating wars to the agonizingly close -- and ultimately failed -- attempts at Israeli-Palestinian peace.

Early Wars & Pan-Arabism (1950--1967)

The aftermath of Israel's 1948 War of Independence left the region in a state of shock and unresolved tension. Hundreds of thousands of Palestinian refugees languished in camps, Arab governments seethed with humiliation, and the new Israeli state worked feverishly to absorb immigrants and build a viable nation. Into this volatile mix stepped a charismatic Egyptian officer named Gamal Abdel Nasser, whose vision of pan-Arab unity would reshape the political landscape and set the stage for the wars to come.

Section at a Glance

  • Over 700,000 Palestinian refugees languish in camps across the region; UNRWA is created in 1949 to provide services, but the camps become permanent -- entire generations born and raised without seeing their ancestral homes
  • Nasser nationalizes the Suez Canal (1956), provoking a joint British-French-Israeli invasion that is forced to withdraw by both the US and USSR -- marking the end of European colonial power in the Middle East
  • The United Arab Republic (Egypt-Syria merger, 1958-1961) collapses after three years, revealing that pan-Arab unity is easier as a slogan than as governance
  • The PLO is founded in 1964 as an Arab League puppet; by 1969, Yasser Arafat takes over and transforms it into the primary vehicle for Palestinian national aspirations

What is the Right of Return?

Right of return is the claim — central to Palestinian national identity — that Palestinian refugees displaced in 1948 (and their descendants) have the right to return to the homes and lands they lost. Palestinians cite UN General Assembly Resolution 194 (1948) as the legal basis. For Israel, accepting a full right of return would fundamentally change the country's Jewish demographic majority, which is why it remains one of the most explosive issues in peace negotiations.

1948--1950s

Palestinian Refugees: A Crisis That Never Ended

The 1948 war created one of the modern world's most enduring refugee crises. More than 700,000 Palestinians fled or were expelled from their homes during the conflict, scattering into refugee camps across the Gaza Strip, the West Bank, Jordan, Syria, and Lebanon. These camps -- hastily constructed settlements of tents and cinderblock shelters -- were meant to be temporary. The refugees themselves, and the Arab governments that hosted them, assumed they would return home within weeks or months once the political situation was resolved.

That return never came. In 1949, the United Nations established UNRWA (the United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees in the Near East) to provide education, healthcare, and basic social services to the displaced population. UNRWA built schools, clinics, and distribution centers in the camps, becoming one of the largest UN agencies in the world. But the camps themselves became permanent fixtures -- entire generations were born, raised, and died in them without ever seeing the villages their grandparents had left behind.

The refugee issue became a core grievance in Palestinian identity. The keys to their old homes became a powerful symbol of the "right of return" -- the demand that Palestinians be allowed to go back to the lands they had lost. For Israel, absorbing these refugees would mean the end of the Jewish state's demographic majority. This fundamental impasse -- Palestinian insistence on return versus Israeli insistence on maintaining a Jewish state -- would remain one of the most intractable issues in all subsequent peace negotiations.

1956

The Suez Crisis

In July 1956, Egyptian President Gamal Abdel Nasser nationalized the Suez Canal, a vital waterway connecting the Mediterranean Sea to the Red Sea that had been controlled by the Anglo-French Suez Canal Company since its construction in the nineteenth century. Nasser's bold move was partly driven by the Western withdrawal of funding for the Aswan High Dam project, and partly by his desire to assert Egyptian sovereignty and challenge the remnants of European colonialism in the Middle East.

Britain and France, furious at losing control of the canal, secretly colluded with Israel to launch a joint military operation. On October 29, 1956, Israeli forces invaded the Sinai Peninsula, quickly advancing toward the canal. Britain and France then issued an ultimatum demanding that both Egypt and Israel withdraw from the canal zone -- a demand they knew Egypt would reject -- and used Nasser's refusal as a pretext to launch their own military intervention, bombing Egyptian airfields and deploying paratroopers.

The military operation was a success, but the political fallout was catastrophic for Britain and France. In a remarkable Cold War alignment, both the United States and the Soviet Union condemned the invasion. President Eisenhower, furious that he had not been consulted and fearful of pushing Arab states toward the Soviet Union, threatened economic sanctions against Britain. Under enormous international pressure, British, French, and Israeli forces withdrew. A UN Emergency Force was deployed to the Sinai to keep the peace.

The crisis was a pivotal moment. For Britain and France, it marked the definitive end of their ability to project imperial power in the Middle East. For Nasser, it was a triumphant vindication -- even though Egypt had lost militarily, Nasser emerged as the undisputed hero of the Arab world, the leader who had stood up to the old colonial powers and won. For Israel, the withdrawal came with a significant gain: guaranteed access to the Straits of Tiran, a crucial shipping lane connecting the Red Sea to the Israeli port of Eilat.

1958--1961

Gamal Abdel Nasser & the Dream of Pan-Arabism

The Suez Crisis catapulted Nasser to the status of the most important leader in the Arab world. His vision of pan-Arabism -- the idea that all Arabic-speaking peoples shared a common identity and should unite into a single political entity -- electrified populations from Morocco to Iraq. Nasser argued that the artificial borders drawn by European colonial powers had divided a natural nation, and that only through Arab unity could the region confront Israel, resist Western imperialism, and achieve modernization.

In 1958, this dream appeared to become reality when Egypt and Syria merged to form the United Arab Republic (UAR), with Nasser as president. The union was greeted with euphoria across the Arab world. But the reality proved far less inspiring than the ideal. Syrian political and military leaders chafed under Egyptian dominance. Cairo's bureaucrats ran the union with a heavy hand, marginalizing Syrian interests. The Syrian economy suffered under Nasser's socialist policies. In September 1961, a military coup in Damascus dissolved the union after just three years.

The failure of the UAR was a serious blow to the pan-Arab dream, but Nasser's personal charisma and the appeal of his ideas remained powerful throughout the 1960s. His radio broadcasts from Cairo's Voice of the Arabs station reached millions, stirring nationalist sentiment and undermining pro-Western monarchies across the region. Pan-Arabism shaped the political discourse of an entire generation, and its eventual failure would leave a vacuum that political Islam would later fill.

1961--1962

The Eichmann Trial: Confronting the Holocaust

On May 11, 1960, agents of the Israeli intelligence service Mossad carried out one of the most dramatic operations in espionage history. In a suburb of Buenos Aires, Argentina, they captured Adolf Eichmann, a former SS officer who had been one of the principal architects of the Holocaust. Eichmann had been responsible for organizing the logistics of the "Final Solution" -- the systematic deportation and murder of six million Jews. After the war, he had escaped to Argentina using a forged Red Cross passport and had been living quietly under a false identity for over a decade.

Eichmann was smuggled out of Argentina and brought to Jerusalem, where he was put on trial in April 1961. The trial was a watershed moment -- it was one of the first major trials to be televised internationally, and it forced the world to confront the full horror of the Holocaust in a way that the earlier Nuremberg Trials had not. Over the course of months, more than 100 Holocaust survivors took the stand and testified about their experiences. Their testimony was devastating, bringing audiences around the world to tears and searing the reality of the genocide into global consciousness.

Eichmann, sitting in a bulletproof glass booth, presented himself as a mere bureaucrat following orders -- famously prompting the philosopher Hannah Arendt, who covered the trial for The New Yorker, to coin the phrase "the banality of evil." Eichmann was found guilty and hanged on May 31, 1962 -- the only person ever executed under Israeli law. The trial had a profound impact on Israeli society, where many Holocaust survivors had previously been reluctant to speak about their experiences. It became a defining moment in the formation of Israeli collective identity, reinforcing the conviction that Jewish survival depended on the existence of a strong Jewish state.

What is the PLO?

The Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) is the political body recognized internationally as the representative of the Palestinian people. Founded in 1964 by the Arab League, it was initially a coalition of Palestinian factions. Under Yasser Arafat's leadership from 1969, it became the main vehicle for Palestinian nationalism. The PLO includes several factions, the largest being Fatah. It formally recognized Israel in 1993 as part of the Oslo Accords.

1964

The PLO Founded

In 1964, the Arab League created the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) at a summit in Cairo. The organization was initially established under the control of Arab governments -- particularly Egypt -- as a way to channel Palestinian aspirations without allowing them to develop into an independent political force that might destabilize existing Arab states. The PLO's founding charter called for the "liberation of Palestine" and the establishment of an Arab state in all of the territory of the former British Mandate, implicitly calling for the destruction of Israel.

In its early years, the PLO was led by Ahmad Shuqeiri, a diplomat who served more as a figurehead for Arab governments than as a genuine leader of Palestinian resistance. The organization had little independent military capability and functioned primarily as a political tool of Nasser's Egypt. But the Palestinian grassroots were already organizing on their own. Various guerrilla factions -- most notably Fatah, founded in the late 1950s by a young engineer named Yasser Arafat -- were conducting cross-border raids into Israel from Jordan, Syria, and Lebanon, establishing an armed resistance movement that would soon transform Palestinian politics.

1969

Yasser Arafat: The Face of Palestinian Resistance

After the devastating Arab defeat in the 1967 Six-Day War (covered in the next section), the established Arab regimes were discredited and the Palestinians took their struggle into their own hands. Yasser Arafat, leader of the Fatah guerrilla movement, emerged as the dominant figure. In 1969, he took over as chairman of the PLO, transforming it from a puppet of Arab governments into the primary vehicle for Palestinian national aspirations.

Under Arafat's leadership, the PLO pursued a strategy that combined guerrilla warfare with international publicity. Palestinian factions carried out a wave of dramatic attacks designed to draw global attention to their cause -- most notoriously, a series of airplane hijackings in the late 1960s and early 1970s. In September 1970, the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP) hijacked four airplanes simultaneously, diverting three of them to a remote airfield in Jordan, where they were blown up in front of television cameras after the passengers were released.

Yasser Arafat was born in Cairo, not Palestine -- yet he became the undisputed symbol of Palestinian nationalism for over 40 years. He always insisted he was born in Jerusalem, but Egyptian birth records tell a different story. Regardless of his birthplace, no figure has been more central to the Palestinian national movement.

Arafat became one of the most recognizable figures in the world -- always appearing in his trademark keffiyeh headdress, with a holstered pistol at his side. To Palestinians and many across the developing world, he was a freedom fighter, the embodiment of a stateless people's struggle for self-determination. To Israelis and much of the West, he was a terrorist whose organization had the blood of civilians on its hands. This fundamental divide in perception would define the Israeli-Palestinian conflict for decades.

What was the Six-Day War?

The Six-Day War (June 5-10, 1967) was a brief but transformative conflict in which Israel fought Egypt, Jordan, and Syria simultaneously. Israel launched a preemptive strike after weeks of escalating Arab military mobilization and rhetoric. In just six days, Israel captured the Sinai Peninsula and Gaza Strip from Egypt, the West Bank and East Jerusalem from Jordan, and the Golan Heights from Syria — tripling the territory under its control and beginning a military occupation of Palestinian land that continues today.

The Six-Day War & Its Aftermath (June 1967)

No single event in the second half of the twentieth century reshaped the Middle East more than the Six-Day War of June 1967. In less than a week, the map of the region was redrawn, the balance of power was permanently altered, and an occupation began that continues to this day. The war's consequences -- territorial, political, religious, and psychological -- reverberate through every aspect of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and the broader politics of the Middle East.

Section at a Glance

  • Israel destroys the Egyptian air force on the ground in three hours on June 5, 1967, then captures the Sinai, West Bank, Gaza Strip, East Jerusalem, and Golan Heights in just six days
  • Israeli paratroopers reach the Western Wall on June 7 -- Jews pray at their holiest site for the first time in 19 years, a moment of profound religious and national significance
  • The victory brings 1 million Palestinians under Israeli military occupation, creating a dilemma between trading territory for peace and holding territory for ideology that defines Israeli politics for the next half century
  • UN Resolution 242 establishes the "land for peace" framework, but its deliberate ambiguity -- "territories" vs. "the territories" -- becomes a source of endless dispute

The Buildup: A Region on the Brink

The spring of 1967 saw a rapid escalation of tensions across the Middle East. Border skirmishes between Israel and Syria had been increasing in frequency and intensity, with both sides shelling each other across the Golan Heights. In May, the Soviet Union passed intelligence to Egypt -- intelligence that was almost certainly false -- claiming that Israel was massing troops on the Syrian border for an imminent invasion. Nasser, who had a mutual defense pact with Syria and could not afford to appear weak, responded by mobilizing the Egyptian military.

Nasser moved tens of thousands of troops into the Sinai Peninsula and, in a fateful decision, demanded the withdrawal of the United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF) that had been stationed in the Sinai since the Suez Crisis. To the surprise of many, UN Secretary-General U Thant complied. Then, on May 22, 1967, Nasser announced the closure of the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping -- a move Israel had long declared would be considered an act of war. Egypt, Syria, and Jordan signed mutual defense pacts. Arab radio stations broadcast increasingly bellicose rhetoric, with some commentators calling for Israel to be "driven into the sea."

In Israel, the period known as the "Waiting" (HaHamtana) was one of existential dread. The country's small size meant it had virtually no strategic depth -- a concerted Arab attack could cut the country in two. Reservists were called up, the economy ground to a halt, and rabbis in Jerusalem consecrated public parks as emergency cemeteries. Many Israelis, only 22 years after the Holocaust, feared a second genocide. The sense of being surrounded by enemies bent on annihilation was overwhelming and would profoundly shape Israeli psychology for generations.

The War: Six Days That Changed Everything (June 5--10, 1967)

Rather than wait for an Arab attack, Israel struck first on the morning of June 5, 1967. In a brilliantly executed surprise attack, the Israeli Air Force launched a massive preemptive strike against Egyptian airfields. Flying low to avoid radar detection, Israeli jets destroyed the vast majority of the Egyptian air force on the ground within the first three hours of the war. With air superiority established in a single morning, the outcome of the ground war was all but decided.

On the southern front, Israeli armored forces surged into the Sinai Peninsula, overwhelming Egyptian defenses in a series of rapid flanking maneuvers. Within four days, the entire Sinai -- a vast desert territory roughly three times the size of Israel itself -- was in Israeli hands, with Israeli troops reaching the banks of the Suez Canal. Egyptian losses were catastrophic: thousands of soldiers died in the desert, many of them while retreating through the Sinai on foot without water.

On the eastern front, Israel had initially sent a message to King Hussein of Jordan through UN channels, urging him to stay out of the war. But Hussein, bound by his defense pact with Egypt and apparently deceived by Nasser's early claims of victory, opened fire on Israeli positions in Jerusalem. Israel responded by launching a full offensive against Jordanian forces in the West Bank and East Jerusalem. By June 7, the Old City of Jerusalem -- including the Temple Mount and the Western Wall -- was in Israeli hands for the first time in nearly two thousand years.

On the northern front, Israeli forces stormed the Golan Heights on June 9, capturing the strategically vital plateau from Syria after fierce fighting. Syrian forces, which had been shelling Israeli towns in the Galilee valley below for years, were driven back. By the time a ceasefire took effect on June 10, Israel had tripled the amount of territory under its control.

The Six-Day War of 1967 tripled Israel's territory in just 132 hours -- one of the fastest territorial expansions in modern military history. The Egyptian Air Force, the largest in the Arab world, was destroyed in under 3 hours on the war's first morning, with nearly 300 aircraft eliminated on the ground.

Israeli paratroopers at the Western Wall, June 7, 1967. For the first time in 19 years, Jews could pray at their holiest site. Chief Rabbi Shlomo Goren blew the shofar as soldiers wept.

Jerusalem Reunified: Tears at the Western Wall

Of all the war's outcomes, none carried more emotional and religious weight than the capture of Jerusalem's Old City. Since 1948, East Jerusalem -- including the Jewish Quarter of the Old City, the Western Wall, and the Temple Mount -- had been under Jordanian control. Jews had been barred from visiting the Western Wall, the last remnant of the Second Temple and the holiest site in Judaism. Dozens of synagogues in the Jewish Quarter had been destroyed or desecrated.

When Israeli paratroopers fought their way through the narrow streets of the Old City and reached the Western Wall on June 7, the scenes were among the most emotionally charged moments in modern Jewish history. Hardened soldiers wept openly. Chief Military Rabbi Shlomo Goren blew the shofar (a ram's horn trumpet used in Jewish ritual) and led prayers at the wall. The moment was captured in one of the most iconic photographs of the twentieth century -- three young paratroopers gazing up at the massive stones of the Wall with expressions of awe and disbelief.

Israel quickly moved to annex East Jerusalem, declaring the city "reunified" as the "eternal and indivisible capital of Israel." This annexation has never been recognized by the international community, and the status of Jerusalem remains one of the most sensitive and contested issues in the conflict. For Israelis, the reunification of Jerusalem was a moment of profound national and religious fulfillment. For Palestinians, it was the beginning of an occupation that would strip them of control over their own neighborhoods, holy sites, and daily lives.

Territorial changes after the Six-Day War. Israel captured the Sinai Peninsula and Gaza Strip from Egypt, the West Bank and East Jerusalem from Jordan, and the Golan Heights from Syria.

What are the Occupied Territories?

Occupied territories refers to the lands captured by Israel during the 1967 Six-Day War: the West Bank (including East Jerusalem) from Jordan, the Gaza Strip and Sinai Peninsula from Egypt, and the Golan Heights from Syria. Under international law (the Fourth Geneva Convention), these are considered territories under military occupation, and the occupying power has obligations toward the civilian population. Israel returned the Sinai to Egypt in 1982 as part of the Camp David peace deal, but continues to control the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and Golan Heights. Israel withdrew settlers and soldiers from Gaza in 2005, though it maintains a blockade.

The Occupation Begins

The stunning military victory came with an enormous complication that Israel's leaders immediately recognized: the conquered territories contained approximately one million Palestinian Arabs. Israel now controlled the West Bank (home to roughly 600,000 Palestinians) and the Gaza Strip (roughly 400,000 Palestinians, including a large refugee population). These people were not citizens of Israel and had no political rights within the Israeli system, yet they were now living under Israeli military authority.

Some Israeli leaders, particularly on the left, argued that the territories should be traded back in exchange for peace agreements with the Arab states -- a "land for peace" formula. Others, particularly religious nationalists, saw the conquest as a divine act that had restored the biblical lands of Judea and Samaria (the West Bank) to Jewish sovereignty, and argued that the land should never be returned. This debate -- between trading territory for peace versus holding territory for ideological or security reasons -- would become the defining fault line in Israeli politics for the next half century.

UN Security Council Resolution 242 (November 22, 1967)

In November 1967, the UN Security Council unanimously adopted Resolution 242, which became the foundation for all subsequent peace negotiations. The resolution called for "withdrawal of Israeli armed forces from territories occupied in the recent conflict" in exchange for the right of every state in the area "to live in peace within secure and recognized boundaries." The deliberate ambiguity of the English text -- it said "territories," not "all the territories" or "the territories" -- was intentional and became a source of endless dispute. Israel argued it meant withdrawal from some territories, not all. Arab states insisted it meant a complete withdrawal to pre-war lines. This "land for peace" framework, despite its ambiguity, became the legal and diplomatic baseline for every peace effort that followed.

What are Israeli Settlements?

Settlements are communities built by Israeli civilians in territories occupied by Israel since the 1967 war — primarily the West Bank and East Jerusalem. They range from small hilltop outposts to city-sized communities with tens of thousands of residents. The international community, including the UN and the International Court of Justice, considers them illegal under the Fourth Geneva Convention, which prohibits an occupying power from transferring its civilian population into occupied territory. Israel disputes this characterization. Today, over 700,000 Israeli settlers live beyond the pre-1967 lines.

The Settlement Movement Begins

Almost immediately after the war, the first Israeli settlements were established in the occupied territories. The movement was initially led by religious Zionists who saw the conquest of the West Bank -- the biblical heartland of ancient Israel -- as a messianic event, the beginning of divine redemption. The Gush Emunim ("Bloc of the Faithful") movement, formally established in 1974 but rooted in post-1967 religious fervor, became the driving force behind settlement expansion.

The Israeli government's response was deeply ambivalent. Labor governments in the late 1960s and 1970s approved some settlements -- particularly in the Jordan Valley, which was considered essential for security -- while officially opposing others. In practice, however, settlements that were established "illegally" by ideological settlers were frequently retroactively approved and connected to Israeli infrastructure. After the right-wing Likud party came to power under Menachem Begin in 1977, settlement construction received enthusiastic government support.

The settlements would grow from a handful of outposts in the late 1960s to a vast network of communities housing hundreds of thousands of Israeli citizens, connected by exclusive roads and protected by the Israeli military. They are considered illegal under international law by the vast majority of the international community (a position Israel disputes), and they became one of the single biggest obstacles to any future peace agreement, as each new settlement made it harder to draw a viable border for a Palestinian state.

Yom Kippur War & Camp David

The years following the Six-Day War brought a grinding war of attrition, Palestinian militancy that shocked the world, a devastating surprise attack on Israel's holiest day, an oil embargo that upended the global economy, and finally -- against all odds -- the first peace treaty between Israel and an Arab state. This period demonstrated both the terrible cost of continued conflict and the tantalizing possibility of peace.

  • Palestinian militants carry out spectacular attacks including the 1972 Munich Olympics massacre, killing 11 Israeli athletes -- Israel responds with targeted assassinations across Europe and the Middle East
  • Egypt and Syria launch a surprise attack on Yom Kippur (October 6, 1973), initially overwhelming Israeli defenses before Israel recovers; the war kills 2,700 Israelis and shatters the myth of Israeli invincibility
  • The 1973 OPEC oil embargo quadruples oil prices overnight, weaponizing petroleum and giving Arab states enormous geopolitical leverage
  • Anwar Sadat flies to Jerusalem in 1977, and the Camp David Accords (1978) produce the first-ever peace treaty between Israel and an Arab state -- Sadat is assassinated by Islamic extremists in 1981 for making peace
1967--1970

The War of Attrition

Egypt refused to accept the results of the Six-Day War and, unable to launch a full-scale military campaign to retake the Sinai, pursued a strategy of continuous low-intensity warfare along the Suez Canal. From 1967 to 1970, Egyptian and Israeli forces engaged in relentless artillery duels, commando raids, and air strikes across the canal. The fighting was brutal and largely invisible to the outside world, yet it claimed thousands of lives on both sides.

Nasser's strategy was simple: Israel could not sustain the economic and human cost of permanently defending a front line 200 kilometers from its population centers. But Israel responded with devastating air raids deep into Egyptian territory, forcing Nasser to request Soviet military assistance. By 1970, Soviet pilots were flying combat missions over Egypt and Soviet air defense crews were manning anti-aircraft batteries along the canal. The war ended with a ceasefire in August 1970, with no territorial changes but with the Soviet Union deeply entrenched in the region -- a development that alarmed both the United States and Israel.

September 1970

Black September: The PLO Expelled from Jordan

After the 1967 war, the PLO and its various affiliated guerrilla factions established a massive presence in Jordan, which hosted the largest Palestinian refugee population. Palestinian armed groups operated freely in Jordanian territory, launching attacks against Israel from Jordanian soil and increasingly behaving as a state within a state. They set up checkpoints, collected taxes, and openly challenged the authority of King Hussein. For the young Jordanian monarch, the situation was becoming intolerable -- the PLO's provocations risked drawing devastating Israeli reprisals against Jordan itself.

The crisis came to a head in September 1970, when the PFLP hijacked four commercial airliners and diverted three of them to a remote Jordanian airstrip. King Hussein, faced with what amounted to an armed insurrection on his territory, unleashed the Jordanian army against the Palestinian guerrillas. The fighting was savage and lasted for weeks. Thousands of Palestinians -- fighters and civilians alike -- were killed. The PLO was driven out of Jordan entirely and relocated its base of operations to Lebanon, a move that would have catastrophic consequences for that country in the years to come. Palestinians remember this period as "Black September."

September 5, 1972

The Munich Olympics Massacre

On September 5, 1972, a Palestinian militant group calling itself "Black September" (named after the PLO's expulsion from Jordan) breached the security perimeter of the Olympic Village in Munich, West Germany, during the Summer Olympic Games. Eight gunmen stormed the quarters of the Israeli Olympic team, killing two athletes immediately and taking nine others hostage. They demanded the release of 234 Palestinian prisoners held in Israeli jails, plus the release of the German far-left militants Andreas Baader and Ulrike Meinhof.

What followed was a nightmarish standoff broadcast live on television to a global audience. After hours of failed negotiations, the hostages and their captors were transported to a military airfield where a catastrophically botched rescue attempt by West German police resulted in the deaths of all nine remaining hostages, five of the eight attackers, and one German police officer. The massacre stunned the world and became one of the defining images of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. The Games were briefly suspended, then controversially resumed.

Israel's response was swift and covert. Prime Minister Golda Meir authorized Operation Wrath of God, a years-long Mossad campaign to hunt down and assassinate individuals believed to have planned or facilitated the Munich attack. Mossad agents tracked targets across Europe and the Middle East, killing them in a series of operations that blurred the line between justice and revenge. The operation was not without controversy -- in one notorious incident in 1973, Mossad agents in Lillehammer, Norway, killed an innocent Moroccan waiter they had mistaken for a Palestinian target.

October 6, 1973

The Yom Kippur War

On October 6, 1973 -- Yom Kippur, the holiest day of the Jewish calendar, when most Israelis were fasting and praying in synagogues -- Egypt and Syria launched a coordinated surprise attack on Israel. Egyptian forces crossed the Suez Canal in a meticulously planned operation, breaching the supposedly impregnable Bar Lev Line (Israel's chain of fortifications along the canal's east bank) using high-pressure water hoses to blast through the sand embankments. Simultaneously, Syrian armored columns poured across the Golan Heights.

The initial hours and days of the war were the most perilous Israel had faced since its founding. On the Golan Heights, a few hundred Israeli tanks faced thousands of Syrian armored vehicles in desperate holding actions. On the Sinai front, Egyptian infantry armed with Soviet anti-tank missiles inflicted devastating losses on Israeli armor. Israel suffered heavy casualties, and for the first time in its history, there was genuine fear of military defeat. Prime Minister Meir reportedly considered authorizing the use of nuclear weapons -- Israel's undeclared nuclear arsenal -- as a last resort.

But Israel's reserve forces mobilized with remarkable speed, and the tide turned. On the Golan, Israeli counterattacks drove Syrian forces back to the pre-war lines and then advanced to within artillery range of Damascus. On the Sinai front, Israeli General Ariel Sharon led a daring crossing of the Suez Canal, establishing a bridgehead on the Egyptian side and encircling the Egyptian Third Army. By the time a UN-brokered ceasefire took effect on October 25, Israel had regained the military upper hand, but at a terrible cost: approximately 2,700 Israeli soldiers killed, more than all but the 1948 war.

October 1973

The Arab Oil Embargo

On October 17, 1973, while the Yom Kippur War still raged, Arab members of OPEC (the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries) announced an oil embargo against the United States, the Netherlands, and other Western nations that were supplying Israel with military aid. Saudi Arabia, the world's largest oil exporter, led the embargo, which also included production cuts that reduced global oil supply.

The impact was immediate and devastating. Oil prices quadrupled, from roughly $3 per barrel to nearly $12. Gas stations across the United States ran dry, forcing the government to impose rationing. Long lines of cars waiting for fuel became a defining image of the era. The embargo plunged Western economies into recession and demonstrated the extraordinary leverage that oil-producing states wielded over the industrialized world. For the first time, ordinary Americans and Europeans felt the Middle East conflict directly in their daily lives.

The embargo lasted until March 1974 and permanently changed the geopolitics of energy. It demonstrated that oil was not just a commodity but a strategic weapon. It massively enriched the Gulf states, giving them newfound political influence. And it made the United States government acutely aware that instability in the Middle East was not just a foreign policy concern but a direct threat to American economic security -- a realization that would drive US engagement in the region for decades.

What is Camp David?

Camp David is the U.S. presidential retreat in Maryland's Catoctin Mountains, used for high-level diplomatic negotiations. In the context of the Middle East, "Camp David" refers to two landmark summits: the 1978 Camp David Accords, where Egyptian President Sadat and Israeli PM Begin negotiated the first-ever Arab-Israeli peace treaty (mediated by President Carter); and the 2000 Camp David Summit, where President Clinton hosted Israeli PM Barak and Palestinian leader Arafat in a failed attempt to reach a final Israeli-Palestinian peace agreement.

September 1978

Camp David Accords & Egypt-Israel Peace

The Yom Kippur War, despite Egypt's ultimate military defeat, restored Egyptian pride. Anwar Sadat, who had succeeded Nasser as president after his death in 1970, had achieved his strategic objective: shattering the myth of Israeli invincibility and proving that Arab armies could fight effectively. With this restored sense of dignity, Sadat made the most dramatic diplomatic move of the twentieth century. On November 19, 1977, he flew to Jerusalem and addressed the Israeli Knesset (parliament) -- the first Arab leader ever to visit Israel and recognize its existence.

The visit electrified the world. Israelis lined the streets to catch a glimpse of Sadat's motorcade. The Egyptian president and Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin -- a former Irgun commander and hardline nationalist -- discovered an unexpected personal rapport. But translating goodwill into a treaty required intensive American mediation. In September 1978, President Jimmy Carter invited Sadat and Begin to Camp David, the presidential retreat in Maryland's Catoctin Mountains, for what he planned as a three-day summit.

The negotiations lasted thirteen grueling days. Multiple times, the talks came close to complete collapse. Sadat and Begin clashed bitterly over the fate of Israeli settlements in the Sinai and the broader question of Palestinian self-determination. Carter personally shuttled between the two leaders' cabins, cajoling, pressuring, and drafting compromise language. The result was the Camp David Accords, signed on September 17, 1978, which established a framework for Egyptian-Israeli peace.

Egyptian President Anwar Sadat, U.S. President Jimmy Carter, and Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin at Camp David, September 1978.

The formal Egypt-Israel Peace Treaty was signed on March 26, 1979. Israel agreed to a phased withdrawal from the entire Sinai Peninsula, including the dismantlement of Israeli settlements there. Egypt became the first Arab country to recognize Israel and establish full diplomatic relations. Sadat and Begin shared the 1978 Nobel Peace Prize. The treaty held -- and continues to hold to this day -- but it came at a steep price. The Palestinian issue was addressed only vaguely, with a framework for "autonomy" that was never implemented. Other Arab states were furious: Egypt was expelled from the Arab League and diplomatically isolated in the Arab world for a decade.

The Camp David Accords (1978) made Egypt the first Arab country to recognize Israel -- but the peace came at the ultimate price. President Sadat was assassinated 3 years later by members of his own army during a military parade. His killer, Khalid Islambouli, shouted "I have killed Pharaoh!" as he opened fire.

October 6, 1981

The Assassination of Anwar Sadat

On October 6, 1981 -- the eighth anniversary of the Yom Kippur War -- President Sadat was reviewing a military parade in Cairo when a group of soldiers suddenly leapt from a passing vehicle and opened fire on the reviewing stand with automatic weapons and grenades. Sadat was killed almost instantly. The assassins were members of Egyptian Islamic Jihad, an extremist group that considered the peace treaty with Israel a betrayal of Islam and of the Palestinian cause.

The assassination was a stark demonstration of the forces that peace-makers in the Middle East would face for decades. Sadat had made peace at the cost of his life. The killing sent a chilling message to any Arab leader who might contemplate following Sadat's path: making peace with Israel could be a death sentence. Sadat was succeeded by his vice president, Hosni Mubarak, who maintained the peace treaty with Israel but pursued a much more cautious and cold relationship. The "warm peace" that Sadat and Begin had envisioned never materialized; instead, Egypt and Israel settled into what became known as a "cold peace" -- correct diplomatic relations without genuine popular reconciliation.

Iranian Revolution & Lebanon

The late 1970s and 1980s brought two seismic developments that reshaped the Middle East in ways that are still unfolding today. The Iranian Revolution of 1979 created the world's first modern Islamist state, demonstrating that political Islam could topple a Western-backed regime. And the destruction of Lebanon -- a country torn apart by civil war, Palestinian militancy, and Israeli and Syrian intervention -- produced Hezbollah, the Iranian-backed Shia militant organization that would become one of the most powerful non-state actors in the world.

Section at a Glance

  • The Iranian Revolution (1979) topples the US-backed Shah and establishes the world's first modern Islamist state under Ayatollah Khomeini, inspiring political Islam movements across the region
  • The Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988) kills approximately 1 million people, features chemical weapons attacks, and ends with no territorial changes -- Khomeini compares accepting the ceasefire to "drinking poison"
  • Israel invades Lebanon in 1982 to destroy the PLO; the Sabra and Shatila massacre kills 800-3,500 Palestinian refugees in camps under Israeli military control, carried out by Lebanese Christian militiamen
  • Hezbollah is founded in 1982 with Iranian funding and training, growing from a small militia into a state-within-a-state with 100,000+ rockets aimed at Israel by the 2000s
1979

The Iranian Revolution

For decades, Iran had been ruled by Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, a Western-allied monarch who used oil wealth to modernize the country while brutally suppressing political dissent through his feared secret police, SAVAK. The Shah's aggressive secularization campaigns -- banning the veil, undermining the power of the Shia clergy, promoting Western cultural values -- alienated large segments of Iranian society, from traditional bazaar merchants to university students to the powerful Shia religious establishment.

By the late 1970s, a broad coalition of Islamists, leftists, liberals, and nationalists united against the Shah. The revolution's spiritual leader was Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, a senior Shia cleric who had been exiled to Iraq and then France for his outspoken opposition to the monarchy. Khomeini's cassette-taped sermons, smuggled into Iran by the thousands, galvanized the opposition. Massive protests brought the country to a standstill. On January 16, 1979, the Shah fled Iran. On February 1, Khomeini returned from exile to a rapturous reception in Tehran, greeted by millions.

What followed was not the liberal democracy that many of the revolution's participants had hoped for. Khomeini moved swiftly to establish an Islamic Republic -- a theocratic system in which ultimate authority rested with a Supreme Leader drawn from the senior Shia clergy. Rival factions were systematically eliminated. The new constitution enshrined "velayat-e faqih" (guardianship of the jurist) as the governing principle, giving the Supreme Leader authority over all branches of government. Iran became the first modern state governed by Islamic law as interpreted by Shia clerics.

The revolution's international impact was immediate and dramatic. On November 4, 1979, radical Iranian students stormed the U.S. Embassy in Tehran and took 52 American diplomats hostage, holding them for 444 days. The hostage crisis humiliated the United States, contributed to President Carter's defeat in the 1980 election, and severed U.S.-Iranian relations for decades. More broadly, the revolution demonstrated to Muslims worldwide that an Islamic political movement could overthrow a powerful Western-backed government -- an inspiration that would fuel Islamist movements across the Middle East.

1980--1988

The Iran-Iraq War

On September 22, 1980, Iraqi President Saddam Hussein launched a full-scale invasion of Iran, hoping to exploit the post-revolutionary chaos and seize the oil-rich, Arab-majority province of Khuzestan. Saddam also feared that Khomeini's revolutionary ideology would inspire Iraq's own Shia majority (roughly 60% of Iraq's population) to revolt against his Sunni-dominated Ba'athist regime.

What Saddam expected to be a quick victory turned into an eight-year nightmare. Iran rallied behind the revolution, repelling the Iraqi invasion by 1982 and then launching its own offensives into Iraqi territory. The war devolved into static trench warfare reminiscent of World War I, with massive infantry assaults, poison gas attacks, and missile strikes on cities. Iraq used chemical weapons extensively, including against Iranian soldiers and, notoriously, against Iraqi Kurdish civilians in the Halabja massacre of 1988. Iran sent waves of young volunteers -- including teenage boys -- to clear minefields with their bodies.

The international community's response was deeply cynical. The United States and most Western countries quietly supported Iraq with intelligence, weapons, and diplomatic cover, viewing Saddam as a bulwark against the spread of Iran's Islamic revolution. The Soviet Union also provided weapons to Iraq. The war ended in a ceasefire in August 1988 with no territorial changes and approximately one million dead. Khomeini, who had vowed to continue until Saddam's regime was overthrown, compared accepting the ceasefire to "drinking poison." The war's legacy was devastating: two shattered economies, a generation of traumatized young men, and a heavily armed Iraq that would soon turn its aggression toward Kuwait.

The Iran-Iraq War lasted 8 years, killed approximately 1 million people, and ended with the border exactly where it started -- not a single square kilometer changed hands permanently. It was the longest conventional war of the 20th century, and both sides used World War I-style trench warfare complete with poison gas attacks.

1975--1990

The Lebanese Civil War

Lebanon had long been considered the most cosmopolitan country in the Middle East -- a tiny nation on the Mediterranean coast whose population included Maronite Christians, Sunni Muslims, Shia Muslims, Druze, and Palestinian refugees, all held together by a delicate power-sharing arrangement established at independence in 1943. But the arrival of the PLO after its expulsion from Jordan in 1970 destabilized this precarious balance. The PLO established a virtual state-within-a-state in southern Lebanon, launching attacks on Israel and inflaming tensions between the country's various communities.

Civil war erupted in April 1975 after a series of escalating incidents between Palestinian and Maronite Christian militias. What began as a two-sided conflict quickly fractured into a bewilderingly complex multi-faction war. Maronite Christians fought Palestinians, Sunnis fought Christians, Druze fought Christians, and various factions within each community fought each other. Syria sent its army into Lebanon in 1976, ostensibly as a peacekeeping force but effectively as an occupying power. The war destroyed Beirut -- once known as the "Paris of the Middle East" -- and killed an estimated 120,000 people over 15 years.

June 1982

Israel's Invasion of Lebanon

On June 6, 1982, Israel launched "Operation Peace for Galilee," a massive military invasion of Lebanon. The stated goal was to push the PLO back from the Israeli border and end the rocket attacks and cross-border raids that had terrorized towns in northern Israel. But Defense Minister Ariel Sharon had far grander ambitions: he aimed to destroy the PLO's entire infrastructure in Lebanon, install a friendly Maronite Christian government in Beirut, and fundamentally reshape the political landscape of Lebanon in Israel's favor.

Israeli forces advanced rapidly through southern Lebanon and reached the outskirts of Beirut within days. For weeks, the Israeli military besieged West Beirut, where PLO fighters and the organization's leadership were concentrated. The siege involved heavy aerial bombardment and artillery shelling of a densely populated urban area, causing significant civilian casualties and generating international condemnation. Under intense diplomatic pressure, an agreement was reached in August 1982 for the PLO to evacuate Beirut under the supervision of a multinational peacekeeping force. Yasser Arafat and thousands of PLO fighters departed by ship to Tunisia, where the PLO would establish its new headquarters.

September 1982

The Sabra and Shatila Massacre

On September 14, 1982, Bashir Gemayel -- the Maronite Christian president-elect of Lebanon and Israel's ally -- was assassinated by a bomb. In the chaos that followed, Israeli forces allowed their Lebanese Christian Phalangist militia allies to enter the Palestinian refugee camps of Sabra and Shatila in West Beirut, ostensibly to root out any remaining PLO fighters. Over the course of approximately 40 hours, from the evening of September 16 to the morning of September 18, the Phalangist militiamen systematically massacred Palestinian and Lebanese Shia civilians in the camps.

The exact death toll remains disputed -- estimates range from 800 to 3,500 killed, including women, children, and elderly people. Israeli forces controlled the perimeter of the camps throughout the massacre, fired illumination flares at night to provide light, and received reports of killings but did not intervene. The massacre provoked worldwide outrage and massive protests within Israel itself -- an estimated 400,000 Israelis (roughly 10% of the population) demonstrated in Tel Aviv demanding an investigation.

The Israeli government established the Kahan Commission to investigate. The commission's 1983 report found that Israel bore "indirect responsibility" for the massacre: it concluded that Sharon and other senior officials had failed to prevent the killings despite having reason to anticipate Phalangist violence against Palestinians. Sharon was forced to resign as Defense Minister, though he remained in the cabinet and would later become Prime Minister. The massacre became a defining trauma in Palestinian collective memory and a source of deep moral reckoning within Israeli society.

What is Hezbollah?

Hezbollah (Arabic for "Party of God") is a Shia Islamist political party and militant organization based in Lebanon. Founded in 1982 with Iranian funding and training in response to Israel's invasion of Lebanon, it operates as both a powerful political party within Lebanon's government and as a heavily armed militia. Hezbollah is designated a terrorist organization by the United States, the EU, and several other countries, but is considered a legitimate resistance movement by much of the Arab and Muslim world. By the 2020s, it had amassed an estimated 150,000+ rockets and missiles aimed at Israel.

~1985

The Rise of Hezbollah

Israel's invasion and subsequent occupation of southern Lebanon had an unintended consequence that would haunt the region for decades: it catalyzed the creation of Hezbollah ("Party of God"), a Shia militant organization backed and funded by Iran's revolutionary government. The Shia community in southern Lebanon -- historically the poorest and most marginalized sect in the country -- had initially welcomed the Israeli invasion because it expelled the PLO, which had been operating in their territory without regard for the local population. But as the Israeli occupation dragged on, bringing checkpoints, raids, and detentions, the Shia turned against Israel with a fury.

Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) dispatched advisors and trainers to Lebanon's Bekaa Valley, where they helped organize, arm, and ideologically indoctrinate a new Shia militant force. Hezbollah formally announced its existence in 1985 with an "Open Letter" that declared its commitment to the destruction of Israel, the establishment of an Islamic state in Lebanon, and loyalty to Iran's Supreme Leader. The organization combined military operations against Israeli forces with extensive social services -- hospitals, schools, welfare programs -- that built deep loyalty among the Shia population.

Hezbollah pioneered the tactic of suicide bombing in the Middle East. On October 23, 1983, truck bombs simultaneously struck the U.S. Marine barracks and a French paratrooper building at Beirut International Airport, killing 241 American servicemen and 58 French soldiers. The attack was the deadliest day for the U.S. Marine Corps since Iwo Jima and led directly to the withdrawal of the multinational peacekeeping force from Lebanon. The success of this attack -- driving out the most powerful military in the world through asymmetric warfare -- became a model for militant groups across the region. Hezbollah also took Western hostages throughout the 1980s, using them as leverage in negotiations with Western governments.

Intifada, Oslo & Failed Peace

The final stretch of the twentieth century brought the Israeli-Palestinian conflict to its most dramatic crossroads. A spontaneous uprising changed the world's perception of the conflict. Secret negotiations in Norway produced a historic handshake on the White House lawn. And then, just as peace seemed possible, assassination, terrorism, and political failure conspired to destroy it. The story of this period is one of tantalizing hope and devastating disappointment.

Section at a Glance

  • The First Intifada (1987) transforms global perceptions of the conflict: images of Palestinian teenagers throwing stones at Israeli tanks reframe Palestinians as an occupied people, not just terrorists
  • Hamas is founded in December 1987 by Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, combining militant resistance with social services -- ironically, Israel had initially encouraged the Muslim Brotherhood in Gaza as a counterweight to the secular PLO
  • The Oslo Accords (1993) produce a historic handshake between Rabin and Arafat on the White House lawn, establishing the Palestinian Authority and a framework for phased peace negotiations
  • Israeli PM Yitzhak Rabin is assassinated by a Jewish extremist in November 1995, and the peace process collapses amid Hamas suicide bombings and the failure of the Camp David summit in 2000

What is an Intifada?

Intifada is an Arabic word meaning "shaking off." In the Israeli-Palestinian context, it refers to Palestinian uprisings against Israeli occupation. There have been two major intifadas: the First Intifada (1987-1993), characterized largely by stone-throwing, strikes, and civil disobedience; and the Second Intifada (2000-2005), which was far more violent, featuring suicide bombings and major Israeli military operations. Both had profound impacts on the conflict and on international perceptions of it.

December 1987

The First Intifada: Stones Against Tanks

On December 8, 1987, an Israeli military truck struck and killed four Palestinian workers at a checkpoint in the Gaza Strip. The incident -- likely an accident -- ignited a powder keg of frustration that had been building for twenty years of occupation. What began as spontaneous riots in the Jabalia refugee camp quickly spread across Gaza and the West Bank, erupting into a sustained popular uprising that the Palestinians called the "Intifada" (Arabic for "shaking off").

The Intifada was fundamentally different from previous Palestinian resistance. It was not led by the PLO in distant Tunisia but by local committees of young people who organized strikes, boycotts, demonstrations, and stone-throwing confrontations with Israeli soldiers. The images that flooded television screens around the world -- Palestinian teenagers hurling rocks at heavily armed Israeli soldiers and tanks -- transformed global perceptions of the conflict. For the first time, many international observers saw the Palestinians not as terrorists but as an occupied people resisting a military occupation.

Israel's response was harsh. Defense Minister Yitzhak Rabin reportedly ordered soldiers to "break their bones" -- a policy of using clubs and batons to beat Palestinian protesters, sometimes on camera. The army imposed curfews, shut down schools and universities, demolished the homes of suspected militants' families, and conducted mass arrests. Over the course of the Intifada (which lasted until 1993), more than 1,100 Palestinians were killed by Israeli forces and approximately 160 Israelis were killed by Palestinians. The uprising demonstrated that the occupation was unsustainable in its current form and created pressure on both sides to find a political solution.

What is Hamas?

Hamas (an Arabic acronym for "Islamic Resistance Movement") is a Palestinian Islamist political and militant organization. Unlike the secular PLO/Fatah, Hamas frames the conflict with Israel in religious terms. It combines armed resistance against Israel with extensive social services (schools, clinics, welfare programs) that built deep grassroots support, especially in the Gaza Strip. Hamas is designated a terrorist organization by the U.S., the EU, and Israel. It won Palestinian legislative elections in 2006 and has controlled Gaza since 2007.

December 1987

Hamas Founded: Islam Enters the Palestinian Struggle

In the same month that the Intifada began, Sheikh Ahmed Yassin -- a wheelchair-bound Gazan cleric who led the Palestinian branch of the Muslim Brotherhood -- founded Hamas (an Arabic acronym for the Islamic Resistance Movement). The founding charter of Hamas, published in 1988, called for the destruction of the State of Israel and the establishment of an Islamic state in all of historic Palestine. It rejected any negotiated settlement that would involve recognizing Israel's right to exist and framed the conflict in explicitly religious terms -- not just as a national struggle but as a religious obligation for all Muslims.

Hamas emerged as a direct rival to the secular PLO and its dominant faction, Fatah. Where the PLO was nationalist, Hamas was Islamist. Where the PLO was willing (at least rhetorically) to negotiate, Hamas insisted on armed resistance as the only path. Crucially, Hamas combined its militant activities with an extensive network of social services -- mosques, schools, clinics, welfare programs, and orphanages -- that filled the vacuum left by the corrupt and ineffective Palestinian Authority and built deep grassroots support, especially among the poor.

Ironically, Israel had initially encouraged the growth of the Muslim Brotherhood in Gaza during the 1980s as a counterweight to the secular PLO, believing that a religious movement focused on prayer and social work would be less dangerous than a nationalist movement focused on armed struggle. This proved to be one of the most catastrophic strategic miscalculations in the history of the conflict. Hamas would grow into a powerful military and political force that would repeatedly derail the peace process through devastating suicide bombings and would eventually take over the Gaza Strip entirely.

1990--1991

Soviet Collapse & the Great Aliyah

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 unleashed one of the largest waves of Jewish immigration in modern history. Approximately one million Jews from the former Soviet Union immigrated to Israel during the 1990s, increasing the country's population by nearly 20%. These immigrants -- many of them highly educated scientists, engineers, musicians, and doctors -- transformed Israeli society, economy, and culture. The tech boom that would make Israel a global innovation hub was fueled in large part by Soviet-trained engineers and physicists.

The demographic impact was enormous. The massive influx of Jewish immigrants strengthened Israel's population relative to the Palestinian population, reinforcing the viability of the Jewish state. It also increased pressure on housing and land, contributing to settlement expansion in the occupied territories. For Palestinians, the arrival of a million new Jewish immigrants underscored their fear that Israel would use demographics to make their claims to the land irreversible.

October 1991

The Madrid Conference

In the aftermath of the 1991 Gulf War -- in which the United States led a coalition to expel Saddam Hussein's forces from Kuwait -- President George H.W. Bush used America's enhanced prestige in the region to convene the Madrid Peace Conference in October 1991. For the first time in the history of the conflict, Israeli and Palestinian representatives sat in the same room for direct negotiations. (Palestinians were officially part of a joint Jordanian-Palestinian delegation, as Israel refused to negotiate directly with the PLO.)

The Madrid Conference did not produce a breakthrough, but it established a framework for negotiations and, crucially, opened a door to direct contact between Israelis and Palestinians. Behind the scenes, both sides recognized that the formal negotiations in Madrid and subsequent sessions in Washington were deadlocked. This realization led to the search for an alternative channel -- a search that would lead to the Norwegian capital and the most dramatic diplomatic breakthrough in the history of the conflict.

What are the Oslo Accords?

The Oslo Accords were a pair of agreements between Israel and the PLO, negotiated secretly in Oslo, Norway. Oslo I (1993) established mutual recognition — the PLO recognized Israel's right to exist, and Israel recognized the PLO as the representative of the Palestinian people. It created the Palestinian Authority (PA), a limited self-governing body for parts of the West Bank and Gaza. Oslo II (1995) divided the West Bank into three zones (Areas A, B, and C) with varying degrees of Palestinian control. The accords were intended as a five-year interim arrangement leading to a final peace deal — that deal was never reached.

September 13, 1993

The Oslo Accords: The Handshake That Shook the World

Throughout 1992 and 1993, Israeli and Palestinian negotiators met in secret in and around Oslo, Norway, far from the media spotlight and the political pressures of Washington. The talks were facilitated by Norwegian diplomats and academics who provided a discreet, informal setting where the two sides could speak candidly. The Israeli team initially consisted of academics and unofficial envoys; the Palestinian team was drawn from the PLO's leadership in Tunis. As the talks progressed and breakthroughs occurred, the Israeli government of Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin became directly involved.

The result was the Declaration of Principles on Interim Self-Government Arrangements -- the Oslo Accords. The agreement represented a mutual recognition that was, in itself, revolutionary: the PLO officially recognized the State of Israel's right to exist in peace and security, renouncing terrorism as a tool of its struggle. Israel, in turn, recognized the PLO as the legitimate representative of the Palestinian people. The accords established the Palestinian Authority (PA), a limited self-governing body that would administer parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip. A five-year interim period was established, during which the two sides would negotiate "final status" issues: the borders of a Palestinian state, the future of Jerusalem, the fate of Palestinian refugees, and the disposition of Israeli settlements.

The handshake: Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin and PLO Chairman Yasser Arafat shake hands on the White House lawn as President Bill Clinton looks on, September 13, 1993.

The signing ceremony on the White House lawn on September 13, 1993, produced one of the most iconic images in modern diplomatic history. As the world watched, President Clinton stood between Rabin and Arafat, gently guiding the reluctant Rabin's hand toward Arafat's outstretched palm. The handshake -- between a former Israeli general and a man Israel had long branded the world's leading terrorist -- seemed to signal that the most intractable conflict in the world might finally be heading toward resolution.

The Oslo Accords were negotiated in secret for 8 months in a Norwegian villa outside Oslo -- even the United States did not know about the talks until near the end. Norwegian diplomats hosted 14 rounds of clandestine meetings, providing the Israelis and Palestinians a private space far from media scrutiny that formal diplomacy could never have offered.

1993--1995

Hope and Opposition: The Oslo Years

The Oslo period was marked by a bitter paradox: even as the peace process advanced, the forces opposing it on both sides intensified their efforts to destroy it. On the Palestinian side, Hamas and Islamic Jihad launched a devastating campaign of suicide bombings targeting Israeli civilians. Buses, cafes, and markets in Israeli cities became killing grounds. Each attack was designed not just to cause casualties but to undermine public support for the peace process among Israelis.

On the Israeli side, the settler movement and the religious right mounted an increasingly aggressive campaign against the Oslo Accords. Rabin was vilified at rallies where protesters carried posters depicting him in a Nazi uniform or in the crosshairs of a rifle scope. Rabbinical authorities associated with the settlement movement issued religious rulings that some interpreted as sanctioning violence against the Prime Minister. The rhetoric was incendiary and, for many, terrifying.

Meanwhile, the practical challenges of implementation were enormous. The Palestinian Authority, established under Arafat's leadership, was plagued by corruption and authoritarianism from the outset. The Israeli military withdrew from some areas but maintained control over others, creating a patchwork of zones with different levels of Palestinian autonomy. Settlement construction continued, undermining Palestinian faith in the process. The five-year interim period was supposed to build trust; instead, it eroded it.

November 4, 1995

The Assassination of Yitzhak Rabin

On the evening of November 4, 1995, Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin attended a massive peace rally in Tel Aviv's Kings of Israel Square (now Rabin Square). Over 100,000 Israelis gathered to show their support for the peace process. Rabin, normally stiff and uncomfortable at public events, was visibly moved by the turnout. He joined in singing "Shir LaShalom" ("Song for Peace"), the lyrics sheet of which was later found in his jacket pocket, stained with his blood.

As Rabin walked to his car after the rally, Yigal Amir -- a 25-year-old Israeli law student and religious extremist -- stepped forward and fired three shots at close range. Two bullets struck Rabin in the back, and he died on the operating table at nearby Ichilov Hospital shortly after. Amir, who was immediately apprehended, stated that he had acted to stop the Oslo peace process, which he believed was a betrayal of the Jewish people and their God-given right to the Land of Israel. He later claimed that rabbinical rulings he had received justified the killing under Jewish law.

The assassination sent shockwaves through Israeli society and around the world. Hundreds of thousands of Israelis, many of them young people, gathered in spontaneous vigils, lighting memorial candles and weeping in the streets. Leaders from around the world attended the funeral, including Jordan's King Hussein and Egypt's President Mubarak. Many Israelis -- and many observers internationally -- believed that the assassination of Rabin effectively killed the peace process. The man who had the military credibility and political authority to make territorial concessions was gone, and no Israeli leader of comparable stature would emerge to take his place.

1996--1999

Netanyahu & the Slowing of Peace

In the wake of Rabin's assassination, his successor, Shimon Peres, called early elections. A new wave of Hamas suicide bombings in early 1996 -- four attacks in nine days that killed 60 Israelis -- shattered what remained of the Israeli public's confidence in the peace process. Benjamin Netanyahu, the leader of the right-wing Likud party, won the election by less than one percentage point on a platform of "peace with security," promising to honor existing agreements while taking a tougher stance on Palestinian compliance.

Netanyahu was deeply skeptical of the Oslo framework and of Arafat personally. Under his premiership, settlement construction accelerated, and the implementation of agreed-upon Israeli withdrawals from Palestinian areas slowed dramatically. The Wye River Memorandum of 1998, negotiated with intensive American involvement, produced limited further Israeli withdrawals in exchange for Palestinian security commitments, but the overall trajectory of the peace process had shifted from progress to stagnation.

The Oslo process had been designed as a five-year interim arrangement leading to a final peace agreement. That five-year period expired in 1998 with no final agreement in sight. The settlements continued to expand, the Palestinian economy continued to deteriorate, and the trust that Oslo was supposed to build had largely evaporated. Both sides blamed the other for the failure, and the window for peace was closing.

July 2000

Camp David II: The Last Best Chance

In 1999, Ehud Barak -- a decorated military hero and Labor party leader -- defeated Netanyahu in a landslide election, promising to revive the peace process and reach a final agreement with the Palestinians. With President Clinton eager to achieve a Middle East peace deal before leaving office, the stage was set for a dramatic summit. In July 2000, Clinton invited Barak and Arafat to Camp David for what was intended to be the culminating negotiation of the Oslo process.

The negotiations lasted two weeks and addressed the core issues that had been deferred since 1993: borders, Jerusalem, refugees, and settlements. According to American and Israeli accounts, Barak offered the most far-reaching proposal any Israeli leader had ever made: a Palestinian state on approximately 90% of the West Bank (with land swaps to compensate for the remaining 10%), Palestinian sovereignty over Arab neighborhoods of East Jerusalem, and a shared arrangement for the holy sites in the Old City. The offer on refugees fell short of the Palestinian demand for a "right of return" but included an acknowledgment of suffering and a compensation mechanism.

Arafat rejected the offer without making a formal counter-proposal. The Palestinians argued that the proposal was inadequate: the remaining 10% of West Bank territory that Israel would keep included major settlement blocs that bisected the proposed Palestinian state, making it non-contiguous and non-viable. They argued that the Jerusalem offer was ambiguous and that the refugee proposal was grossly insufficient. The Israelis and Americans argued that Arafat had been offered the best deal Palestinians would ever get and had walked away without engaging seriously.

The debate over who killed the Camp David talks -- and whether they were ever close enough to succeed -- has consumed scholars and diplomats ever since. Clinton publicly blamed Arafat. Palestinians counter that the offer was never formally presented in writing and that the terms were less generous than the "90% narrative" suggests. What is beyond dispute is the catastrophic aftermath: the failure of Camp David led directly to the Second Intifada -- a wave of violence that killed thousands on both sides and buried the hopes of the Oslo generation under rubble and blood.

Multi-Faith & Multi-Narrative Perspectives

The events of 1950-2000 are among the most contested in this entire history. The same events carry fundamentally different meanings depending on whose lens you look through. Understanding these competing narratives is essential to understanding why peace has been so elusive.

Section at a Glance

  • Israelis see the 1967 war as defensive and Jerusalem's reunification as historic justice; Palestinians see it as aggressive expansion and an illegal annexation that continues today
  • Over 700,000 Israeli settlers now live beyond the Green Line -- Israel calls them communities with biblical roots, Palestinians call them illegal colonies designed to make a Palestinian state impossible
  • The Oslo process is remembered by Israelis as a generous peace offer rejected by Arafat and answered with suicide bombings; Palestinians see it as a framework that entrenched occupation while offering only limited self-governance
  • Both narratives carry genuine pain and documented truth -- the inability to acknowledge the other side's experience is itself a major barrier to peace

What is the Green Line?

The Green Line is the 1949 armistice border between Israel and its neighbors (Jordan, Egypt, Syria, Lebanon), drawn in green ink on the map during ceasefire negotiations. It is not a permanent, internationally recognized border but is widely treated as the baseline for any future peace agreement. Territory beyond the Green Line — the West Bank, East Jerusalem, Gaza Strip, and Golan Heights — was captured by Israel in the 1967 war and is considered "occupied territory" by most of the international community.

What is the Two-State Solution?

The two-state solution is the most widely endorsed framework for resolving the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. It envisions an independent Palestinian state (comprising the West Bank and Gaza Strip, with East Jerusalem as its capital) existing alongside Israel, roughly based on the pre-1967 borders with agreed land swaps. It is endorsed by the United Nations, the European Union, the Arab League, and (at various points) by both Israeli and Palestinian leaders. However, decades of settlement expansion, the Hamas-Fatah split, and mutual distrust have made its implementation increasingly difficult.

Why Multiple Narratives Matter

Neither narrative above is entirely "right" or "wrong." Both contain genuine grievances, real suffering, and legitimate claims. The inability of the international community to hold multiple truths simultaneously — that Jews deserve security AND Palestinians deserve freedom, that Israel faces real threats AND Palestinians live under real occupation — is itself one of the reasons this conflict persists. Understanding both narratives is not moral relativism; it is the prerequisite for any just resolution.
