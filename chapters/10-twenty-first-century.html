Chapter 10

The 21st Century

2000 CE - Present

The new millennium brought no peace to the lands of Abraham. Instead, it delivered the worst terrorist attack in history, endless wars, revolutions that promised democracy but delivered chaos, and a conflict in the Holy Land that spiraled into a humanitarian catastrophe watched in real time by the entire world. This is the chapter we are still living through.

The Second Intifada & the War on Terror

The 21st century opened with two seismic events that would define the decades to come: a Palestinian uprising far bloodier than the first, and a terrorist attack on American soil that reshaped the entire global order. Both were rooted in the unresolved tensions of the Abrahamic world — the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, the presence of Western military forces in Muslim-majority lands, and the deepening anger of populations who felt humiliated, occupied, and ignored.

Gaza Strip vs. West Bank: What's the Difference?

The Gaza Strip and the West Bank are the two separate territories that make up the proposed Palestinian state, but they are geographically disconnected. The Gaza Strip is a narrow, densely populated coastal territory (~365 sq km) along the Mediterranean, bordering Egypt and Israel; it has been controlled by Hamas since 2007. The West Bank is a larger, landlocked territory (~5,860 sq km) west of the Jordan River, partially administered by the Palestinian Authority under Israeli military occupation. East Jerusalem, captured by Israel in 1967 and later annexed, sits within the West Bank and is claimed by Palestinians as their future capital.

Section at a Glance

  • Ariel Sharon's provocative visit to the Temple Mount (September 2000) triggers the Second Intifada, which kills approximately 3,000 Palestinians and 1,000 Israelis over five years of suicide bombings and military operations
  • Israel constructs a 700km separation barrier that reduces suicide bombings by 90% but snakes deep into the West Bank, annexing roughly 10% of Palestinian territory and separating farmers from their land
  • The 9/11 attacks (2001) kill nearly 3,000 Americans and launch the "War on Terror" -- the US invades Afghanistan (2001) and Iraq (2003), destabilizing the entire region for two decades
  • The Iraq War, launched on false WMD claims, costs over 200,000 Iraqi civilian lives, empowers Iran, and creates the power vacuum that eventually births ISIS
September 2000 - February 2005

The Second Intifada (Al-Aqsa Intifada)

On September 28, 2000, Ariel Sharon — then Israel's opposition leader, soon to become Prime Minister — made a provocative visit to the Temple Mount, known to Muslims as Haram al-Sharif, accompanied by over a thousand armed police. Sharon declared that the site would remain under permanent Israeli control. The visit was deliberately inflammatory, coming at a time when Israeli-Palestinian peace talks at Camp David had just collapsed in failure, and Palestinian frustration was at a boiling point.

The next day, Palestinians protested at the Al-Aqsa Mosque. Israeli police responded with rubber bullets and live ammunition, killing several Palestinians. Within days, the protests had spread across the West Bank, Gaza, and even into Arab towns within Israel. But unlike the first intifada of 1987 — which was characterized largely by stone-throwing and civil disobedience — the second intifada quickly became horrifyingly violent on both sides.

Palestinian militant groups, including Hamas, Islamic Jihad, and the Al-Aqsa Martyrs' Brigades (affiliated with Arafat's own Fatah party), launched a devastating campaign of suicide bombings targeting Israeli civilians. Buses were blown up in Jerusalem and Tel Aviv. Cafes, markets, and hotels were attacked. The Passover massacre at the Park Hotel in Netanya in March 2002 killed 30 Israeli civilians celebrating the Jewish holiday. For Israelis, daily life became a landscape of terror — parents afraid to send children to school, commuters dreading the bus, diners eyeing the door.

Israel responded with overwhelming military force. Operation Defensive Shield (2002) sent tanks and troops into every major Palestinian city in the West Bank. Targeted assassinations killed Hamas leaders and militant commanders, often via helicopter-launched missiles that also killed bystanders. Curfews confined entire cities to their homes for days at a time. The Palestinian Authority's compound in Ramallah was besieged, leaving Arafat effectively imprisoned in the rubble of his own headquarters. By the time the violence subsided, approximately 3,000 Palestinians and 1,000 Israelis had been killed. The already fragile trust between the two peoples was shattered. A generation on both sides was traumatized, radicalized, and convinced that the other side understood nothing but force.

2002 Onward

The Separation Barrier / Wall

In the midst of the second intifada, with suicide bombings killing Israeli civilians on a near-weekly basis, the Israeli government under Prime Minister Sharon began constructing a massive physical barrier between Israel and the West Bank. The barrier consists of concrete walls up to 8 meters (26 feet) high in urban areas — particularly around Jerusalem and Bethlehem — and electrified fencing with patrol roads, trenches, and surveillance systems elsewhere. It stretches for over 700 kilometers.

Israel calls it a "security fence" and presents compelling evidence for its effectiveness: suicide bombings dropped by over 90% after its construction. For Israelis who lived through the horror of the second intifada, the barrier literally saved lives. The numbers are hard to argue with — the wave of terror attacks that defined 2001-2003 slowed to a trickle by 2006.

Palestinians call it the "apartheid wall" and see something very different. The barrier does not follow the 1967 Green Line (the internationally recognized boundary). Instead, it snakes deep into the West Bank, encircling Israeli settlements and annexing de facto about 10% of West Bank territory to the Israeli side. Palestinian farmers are separated from their olive groves and fields. Villages are cut off from neighboring towns. Some Palestinian communities find themselves completely encircled by the wall, forced to pass through military checkpoints to reach schools, hospitals, and markets. In 2004, the International Court of Justice issued an advisory opinion declaring the barrier illegal under international law. Israel rejected the ruling. The wall remains standing — a physical embodiment of the conflict's intractability.

Israel's separation barrier stretches over 700 km — more than twice the length of the pre-1967 border it roughly follows. At its tallest, the concrete wall sections reach 8 meters (26 feet), nearly twice the height of the Berlin Wall. The International Court of Justice declared it illegal in 2004, but it remains standing two decades later.

September 11, 2001

The 9/11 Attacks

On the morning of September 11, 2001, 19 hijackers affiliated with al-Qaeda — the jihadist network founded by Osama bin Laden — seized control of four commercial airliners in the United States. Two planes were flown into the Twin Towers of the World Trade Center in New York City, causing both towers to collapse. A third struck the Pentagon in Washington, D.C. The fourth, likely targeting the U.S. Capitol or White House, crashed into a field in Pennsylvania after passengers fought the hijackers. Nearly 3,000 people were killed — the deadliest terrorist attack in history.

Osama bin Laden's stated motivations were specific and political: the presence of American military bases in Saudi Arabia (the land of Mecca and Medina, Islam's two holiest sites), unwavering U.S. support for Israel and its treatment of Palestinians, and the devastating U.S.-led sanctions on Iraq that had contributed to hundreds of thousands of Iraqi civilian deaths in the 1990s. This was not, at its core, a religious war of "Islam versus Christianity" or "Islam versus Judaism" — though many people on all sides would frame it that way. It was a catastrophic act of political violence by an extremist organization that claimed to act in Islam's name, even as the vast majority of the world's 1.8 billion Muslims condemned it.

The consequences were world-changing. In the United States, 9/11 created a wave of fear, grief, and anger that fundamentally reshaped American foreign policy, civil liberties, and attitudes toward Muslims. Hate crimes against Muslims and Sikhs (often mistaken for Muslims) spiked immediately. The Patriot Act expanded government surveillance powers. Airport security was transformed. And President George W. Bush declared a "War on Terror" that would launch military campaigns across the Muslim world, reshape alliances, and cost trillions of dollars and hundreds of thousands of lives over the next two decades. For Muslims in America and Europe, 9/11 marked the beginning of an era of suspicion, profiling, and the constant demand to "condemn" violence they had nothing to do with.

September 11, 2001: Smoke billows from the Twin Towers of the World Trade Center as seen from the Statue of Liberty. Nearly 3,000 people were killed in the deadliest terrorist attack in history.
October 2001

The War in Afghanistan

Less than a month after 9/11, the United States invaded Afghanistan. The stated objectives were clear: destroy al-Qaeda's operational base, capture or kill Osama bin Laden, and remove the Taliban government that had sheltered him. The Taliban — an ultraconservative Islamist movement that had seized power in 1996 and imposed a brutal interpretation of Sharia law, banning girls from school, destroying pre-Islamic cultural heritage (the Bamiyan Buddhas), and executing people in football stadiums — refused to hand over bin Laden.

The initial military campaign, conducted with U.S. air power and Afghan Northern Alliance ground forces, toppled the Taliban government within weeks. But bin Laden escaped into the mountains of Tora Bora and eventually into Pakistan. What was supposed to be a swift operation became the longest war in American history — stretching from 2001 to 2021. The U.S. and NATO attempted to build a democratic Afghan state, pouring billions into infrastructure, education, and training of Afghan security forces. But corruption, ethnic divisions, and the Taliban's resilience in the countryside undermined every effort.

In August 2021, as U.S. forces completed their withdrawal under a deal negotiated by the Trump administration and executed by the Biden administration, the Taliban swept back to power in a matter of days. The Afghan government and its 300,000-strong army collapsed almost without a fight. Chaotic scenes at Kabul airport — Afghans clinging to departing planes, desperate crowds at the gates — became the defining images of America's two-decade war. Over 170,000 people had been killed in the conflict, including nearly 50,000 Afghan civilians. The Taliban were back in charge, women were once again barred from secondary education and most employment, and the entire effort appeared to have been for nothing.

March 2003

The Iraq War

In March 2003, the United States, joined by the United Kingdom and a "coalition of the willing," invaded Iraq. The Bush administration claimed that Saddam Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction (WMDs) and had links to al-Qaeda — both claims that would prove to be false. Secretary of State Colin Powell made the case for war at the United Nations Security Council, presenting intelligence that was later revealed to be deeply flawed or fabricated. France, Germany, Russia, and China opposed the invasion. Millions marched in protest worldwide in February 2003, in the largest coordinated anti-war demonstrations in history.

The initial invasion was militarily swift. Baghdad fell within three weeks. Saddam was toppled, eventually captured hiding in a hole near Tikrit, put on trial by an Iraqi court, and executed in December 2006. But the aftermath was catastrophic. The U.S. decision to disband the Iraqi army and ban members of Saddam's Baath Party from government (a policy called "de-Baathification") threw hundreds of thousands of armed, trained Sunni men out of work and out of power overnight. They became the backbone of a Sunni insurgency.

Iraq descended into a sectarian civil war between its Sunni and Shia communities — a fault line that Saddam had brutally suppressed but never resolved. Shia militias, many backed by Iran, conducted revenge killings against Sunnis. Sunni insurgents, including al-Qaeda in Iraq (the precursor to ISIS), launched devastating attacks on Shia mosques and markets. The bombing of the Al-Askari Mosque in Samarra in 2006 — one of the holiest sites in Shia Islam — pushed the country to the brink of all-out religious war. By some estimates, over 200,000 Iraqi civilians were killed in the years following the invasion. Millions became refugees. The power vacuum created by Saddam's removal empowered Iran (a Shia theocracy) to extend its influence across Iraq, and eventually birthed ISIS, the most brutal jihadist group the world had seen. The Iraq War is widely regarded as one of the greatest strategic blunders in modern history — a war launched on false pretenses that destabilized an entire region.

November 2004

The Death of Yasser Arafat

Yasser Arafat — the keffiyeh-wearing revolutionary who had led the Palestinian national movement for nearly four decades — died on November 11, 2004, at a French military hospital. He was 75 years old. He had spent his final years confined by the Israeli military to his compound in Ramallah, the Muqata'a, which had been largely reduced to rubble by Israeli forces during Operation Defensive Shield in 2002. Israel refused to let him leave, and he refused to leave permanently, knowing he would never be allowed to return.

Arafat's legacy is deeply contested. To many Palestinians, he was the father of the nation — the man who put the Palestinian cause on the world stage, who led the PLO from obscurity to international recognition, who won the Nobel Peace Prize alongside Rabin and Peres. To many Israelis and Western critics, he was a terrorist who never truly abandoned violence, who rejected a generous peace offer at Camp David in 2000 (a characterization Palestinians fiercely dispute), and whose corrupt leadership squandered billions in international aid.

He was succeeded by Mahmoud Abbas, his longtime deputy, who won the January 2005 presidential election. Abbas — an aging, suit-wearing bureaucrat — could not have been more different from the charismatic, mercurial Arafat. Abbas opposed the second intifada and believed in negotiation over violence, but he proved unable to deliver results at the negotiating table. As of 2026, Abbas remains the president of the Palestinian Authority, despite his term having officially expired in 2009, with no elections held since. His authority is limited to parts of the West Bank, and his legitimacy in the eyes of many Palestinians has long since evaporated.

The Gaza Problem

Gaza — a 365-square-kilometer strip of land along the Mediterranean coast, one of the most densely populated places on Earth — became the defining theater of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict in the 21st century. What began with a hopeful Israeli withdrawal turned into a blockade, a power struggle, and a recurring cycle of devastating wars that killed thousands and resolved nothing.

Section at a Glance

  • Ariel Sharon unilaterally withdraws all 8,000 Israeli settlers from Gaza in August 2005, but Israel retains control of borders, airspace, and coastline -- Palestinians argue the occupation merely changed form
  • Hamas wins free and fair Palestinian elections in January 2006, then seizes full control of Gaza in June 2007 after a violent power struggle with Fatah, splitting the Palestinian national movement in two
  • Israel imposes a comprehensive land, sea, and air blockade on Gaza; unemployment exceeds 50%, electricity runs only a few hours daily, and 97% of groundwater becomes unfit for human consumption
  • Four major Israel-Hamas wars between 2008 and 2021 follow an identical pattern: rockets from Gaza, massive Israeli military response, devastating civilian casualties, ceasefire, nothing changes
August 2005

Israel's Withdrawal from Gaza

In a dramatic and unilateral move, Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon — the lifelong hawk, the godfather of the settlement movement, the architect of Israel's invasion of Lebanon in 1982 — ordered the complete withdrawal of all Israeli soldiers and settlers from the Gaza Strip. Approximately 8,000 settlers who had lived in 21 settlements across Gaza, some for decades, were forcibly evacuated. Soldiers carried weeping settlers from their homes. Synagogues were demolished. It was one of the most gut-wrenching moments in Israeli history, dividing the country between those who saw it as a necessary step for peace and those who saw it as a betrayal of the Zionist project.

Sharon framed the withdrawal as a strategic decision, not a peace gesture — Israel could not sustain the cost of protecting a small settler population surrounded by 1.4 million Palestinians. Critics on the right accused him of surrendering to terrorism. Critics on the left noted that while Israel had left Gaza, it retained control over its borders, airspace, coastline, and electromagnetic spectrum — leading Palestinians and international observers to argue that the occupation had not truly ended but merely changed form. The withdrawal would, within two years, produce consequences that nobody had predicted and nobody could undo.

January 2006

Hamas Wins Palestinian Elections

In January 2006, the Palestinian Legislative Council held elections that were widely considered free, fair, and transparent by international observers, including monitors from the European Union and the Carter Center. Hamas — the Islamist militant movement founded during the first intifada, which had carried out dozens of suicide bombings against Israeli civilians, and whose charter called for the destruction of Israel — won a decisive majority, taking 74 of 132 seats. The result stunned the world.

The vote was not primarily an endorsement of Hamas's ideology or its violence. Palestinians were exhausted by the corruption and incompetence of Fatah, which had dominated Palestinian politics for decades under Arafat and Abbas. Fatah officials had enriched themselves with international aid money while ordinary Palestinians saw no improvement in their lives and no progress toward statehood. Hamas, by contrast, ran a disciplined campaign focused on clean governance, social services (it operated hospitals, schools, and charities), and resistance to occupation. Many Palestinians voted for Hamas not because they wanted an Islamic state, but because they wanted accountability.

The international response was devastating. The United States, the European Union, and Israel refused to recognize the Hamas government because Hamas would not meet three conditions: recognize Israel's right to exist, renounce violence, and accept previously signed agreements. International aid was cut off. A financial embargo was imposed. The message was clear: democracy was acceptable only if it produced the "right" result. Fatah, encouraged by the U.S., refused to hand over security forces. A dangerous power struggle between the two Palestinian factions was now inevitable.

June 2007

Hamas Seizes Control of Gaza

By June 2007, the power struggle between Hamas and Fatah had turned violent. After months of clashes, assassinations, and failed unity negotiations, Hamas launched a swift military takeover of the Gaza Strip. In five days of intense fighting, Hamas fighters overran Fatah security positions, seized government buildings, and took complete control. Fatah members were killed, tortured, or thrown from rooftops. Others fled to the West Bank.

The Palestinian national movement was now split in two — geographically, politically, and ideologically. Hamas controlled Gaza, ruling its 2 million residents as a de facto state-within-a-statelet, maintaining armed forces, collecting taxes, and enforcing its version of Islamic governance. Fatah and the Palestinian Authority, under Mahmoud Abbas, retained control of parts of the West Bank (the parts not under direct Israeli military control). The two factions would attempt reconciliation repeatedly over the following years — signing agreements in Cairo, Doha, and elsewhere — but none would hold. This split meant that no single Palestinian leader could negotiate on behalf of all Palestinians, making any comprehensive peace deal effectively impossible.

What is the Iron Dome?

Iron Dome is Israel's mobile air defense system, operational since 2011, designed to intercept and destroy short-range rockets and artillery shells. Developed by Israeli defense company Rafael with U.S. funding, it uses radar to detect incoming projectiles and fires interceptor missiles to destroy them mid-air. Iron Dome has a reported interception rate of approximately 90% and has been credited with saving many Israeli lives during rocket attacks from Gaza and Lebanon. However, critics note that its success creates an asymmetry: Israeli civilians are largely protected while Palestinian civilians in Gaza have no equivalent defense system.

The Iron Dome missile defense system has a reported interception rate of approximately 90% and has intercepted thousands of rockets since becoming operational in 2011. Each interceptor missile costs around $50,000, while the rockets it destroys often cost a few hundred dollars to build — creating a stark economic asymmetry alongside the life-saving technology.

2007 Onward

The Blockade of Gaza

Following Hamas's takeover, Israel imposed a comprehensive land, sea, and air blockade on the Gaza Strip, with Egypt sealing its border crossing at Rafah for most of the subsequent years. Israel controls all entry and exit points (with the partial exception of the Egyptian border), the airspace, the coastline, the telecommunications infrastructure, and the flow of goods. Concrete, steel, and construction materials were initially banned or severely restricted. Fishing zones were limited to a few nautical miles — fishermen who ventured further were fired upon by the Israeli navy. For years, caloric calculations were reportedly used to determine the minimum amount of food that should be allowed in.

Israel frames the blockade as a legitimate security measure, necessary to prevent Hamas from importing weapons — particularly rockets and the materials to build them. This is not an abstract concern: Hamas and other militant groups in Gaza fired thousands of rockets at Israeli towns and cities over the years, terrorizing the populations of Sderot, Ashkelon, and communities along the border. Iron Dome, Israel's missile defense system (operational from 2011), intercepted many of these rockets, but the psychological toll on Israeli civilians — the constant sirens, the 15-second dash to shelters — was enormous.

Critics — including the United Nations, the International Committee of the Red Cross, and numerous human rights organizations — describe the blockade as a form of collective punishment against an entire civilian population, illegal under international humanitarian law. Under the blockade, Gaza's economy collapsed. Unemployment regularly exceeded 50%, higher among youth. Electricity was available for only a few hours a day. Water quality deteriorated to the point where 97% of Gaza's groundwater was declared unfit for human consumption. A generation of Gazans grew up knowing nothing but the blockade — no ability to leave, no airport, no seaport, no freedom of movement, and increasingly, no hope.

Gaza is one of the most densely populated places on Earth — approximately 2.3 million people packed into just 365 sq km, roughly twice the population density of London. Before the blockade, Gaza had a functioning airport (opened in 1998 with great fanfare); it was destroyed by Israeli bulldozers in 2001 and never rebuilt.

2008 - 2021

The Repeating Cycle: Gaza Wars

Between 2008 and 2021, Israel and Hamas fought four significant military conflicts, each following an almost identical pattern: a period of rising tension, rocket fire from Gaza, massive Israeli military operations, devastating civilian casualties (overwhelmingly Palestinian), international condemnation, a ceasefire, and then nothing changes. The cycle would repeat.

Operation Cast Lead (Dec 2008 - Jan 2009)

Launched after a breakdown of a ceasefire and escalating rocket fire, Operation Cast Lead was a three-week assault combining air strikes and a ground invasion. Israel used white phosphorus in densely populated areas — a charge documented by Human Rights Watch. Approximately 1,400 Palestinians were killed, including hundreds of civilians and over 300 children, according to Palestinian and UN sources. Thirteen Israelis were killed, including 3 civilians. The UN's Goldstone Report accused both Israel and Hamas of war crimes, though its author later partially retracted some findings regarding Israel. Gaza's infrastructure suffered severe damage, and the international community condemned the scale of civilian casualties.

Operation Pillar of Defense (November 2012)

Triggered by an escalation of cross-border violence, this 8-day conflict began with Israel's targeted killing of Hamas military commander Ahmed Jabari. Hamas responded with hundreds of rockets, reaching Tel Aviv and Jerusalem for the first time. Approximately 170 Palestinians and 6 Israelis were killed. The conflict ended through Egyptian-brokered ceasefire negotiations. It was the first major test of Israel's Iron Dome missile defense system, which intercepted the majority of rockets targeting populated areas, reinforcing Israeli confidence in the technology while raising questions about the asymmetry of the conflict.

Operation Protective Edge (July - August 2014)

The most devastating of the pre-2023 conflicts, this 50-day war was triggered by the kidnapping and murder of three Israeli teenagers in the West Bank, followed by the revenge killing of a Palestinian teenager in Jerusalem. Israel launched a massive air and ground campaign, targeting Hamas tunnels that crossed into Israeli territory. Approximately 2,200 Palestinians were killed, including over 500 children. Entire neighborhoods in Gaza — Shejaiya, Rafah, Beit Hanoun — were reduced to rubble. Seventy-three Israelis were killed, including 67 soldiers. Gaza's infrastructure was devastated: thousands of homes destroyed, hospitals and schools damaged, and the sole power plant knocked out. The UN estimated it would take 20 years to rebuild. It never fully did before the next war.

The May 2021 War (11 days)

Sparked by tensions in Jerusalem — Israeli police raids on Al-Aqsa Mosque during Ramadan and the threatened eviction of Palestinian families in the Sheikh Jarrah neighborhood — Hamas issued an ultimatum to Israel and then fired rockets toward Jerusalem. Israel responded with 11 days of intensive airstrikes on Gaza, targeting Hamas commanders and infrastructure, including the destruction of several high-rise towers. Approximately 260 Palestinians and 13 Israelis were killed. This conflict was notable for the unprecedented wave of violence between Jewish and Arab citizens within Israel itself, with lynch mobs and riots in mixed cities like Lod, Haifa, and Acre — a development that shocked Israelis who had believed coexistence within the state was firmly established.

What is Hezbollah?

Hezbollah (Arabic for "Party of God") is a Shia Islamist political party and militant organization based in Lebanon. Founded in 1982 with Iranian funding during Israel's occupation of southern Lebanon, it operates as both a major political force within Lebanon's government and a heavily armed militia — effectively a state within a state. Designated a terrorist organization by the U.S. and EU, Hezbollah is backed by Iran and serves as a key component of Iran's regional "axis of resistance" against Israel. By the 2020s, it possessed an estimated 150,000+ rockets and missiles.

July - August 2006

The Second Lebanon War

On July 12, 2006, Hezbollah — the Shia militant organization and political party based in southern Lebanon, funded and armed by Iran — launched a cross-border raid into Israel, killing several soldiers and kidnapping two. Israel responded with a massive 34-day military campaign that included intensive aerial bombardment of Lebanon, a naval blockade, and eventually a ground invasion of southern Lebanon. Hezbollah retaliated by firing approximately 4,000 rockets into northern Israel, reaching as far south as Haifa, Israel's third-largest city. Over a million Israelis were displaced or confined to shelters.

The human toll was devastating: approximately 1,200 Lebanese were killed (the majority civilians), and 165 Israelis (including 44 civilians). Much of southern Lebanon's infrastructure was destroyed — bridges, roads, power stations, and Beirut's southern suburbs (Hezbollah's stronghold, known as the Dahiyeh) were flattened. Israel also bombed the Jiyyeh power station, causing a massive oil spill along the Lebanese coast.

The war ended with UN Security Council Resolution 1701, which called for a ceasefire and the deployment of Lebanese armed forces and expanded UN peacekeepers in southern Lebanon. But the strategic outcome was ambiguous at best. Hezbollah's leader, Hassan Nasrallah, declared a "divine victory" simply because his organization had survived a full-scale Israeli assault — something no Arab army had managed. Israel, which had failed to recover its kidnapped soldiers (they were later returned dead in a prisoner exchange), secure any strategic objectives, or destroy Hezbollah's military capacity, faced a damning internal inquiry (the Winograd Commission). Hezbollah, far from being diminished, used the post-war period to massively expand its arsenal, acquiring an estimated 150,000 rockets and missiles by the 2020s — far more than before the war.

What was the Arab Spring?

The Arab Spring was a wave of anti-government protests, uprisings, and revolutions that swept across the Arab world beginning in late 2010. Triggered by decades of authoritarian rule, corruption, unemployment, and lack of political freedom, the movement toppled long-standing dictators in Tunisia, Egypt, Libya, and Yemen. However, most countries that experienced uprisings either descended into civil war (Syria, Libya, Yemen), saw military coups restore authoritarian rule (Egypt), or crushed protests with force (Bahrain). Only Tunisia achieved even temporary democratic governance.

The Arab Spring & the Rise of ISIS

Beginning in late 2010, a wave of popular uprisings swept across the Arab world, fueled by decades of authoritarian rule, corruption, economic stagnation, and a young population connected by social media. For a brief, electrifying moment, it seemed like democracy might finally take root in the Middle East. Instead, most revolutions ended in civil war, military coups, or the rise of the most brutal jihadist movement the world had ever seen.

Section at a Glance

  • A Tunisian street vendor's self-immolation (December 2010) triggers the Arab Spring, toppling dictators in Tunisia, Egypt, Libya, and Yemen -- but only Tunisia achieves even temporary democracy
  • Syria descends into civil war after Assad's brutal crackdown on protesters; over 500,000 are killed, 6 million flee the country, and Russia and Iran intervene to prop up the regime
  • ISIS emerges from the wreckage of the Iraq War, declaring a "caliphate" across parts of Iraq and Syria in 2014, imposing medieval brutality, and inspiring terrorist attacks worldwide
  • Egypt's democratic revolution is reversed by a military coup in 2013; Libya collapses into warring factions; Yemen descends into famine -- the spring becomes a long winter
December 2010 - 2012

The Arab Spring

On December 17, 2010, Mohamed Bouazizi, a 26-year-old Tunisian street vendor, set himself on fire in front of a government building in the provincial town of Sidi Bouzid. A policewoman had confiscated his vegetable cart — his only source of income — and reportedly slapped and humiliated him. When he went to complain, officials refused to see him. His act of desperation, captured on mobile phones and spread through social media, ignited a revolution that no one predicted and no government was prepared for. Bouazizi died on January 4, 2011. By January 14, Tunisia's dictator of 23 years, Zine El Abidine Ben Ali, had fled the country.

The Tunisian revolution — later called the "Jasmine Revolution" — sent shockwaves across the Arab world. If Tunisians could topple their dictator, why not Egyptians? Libyans? Syrians? Within weeks, protests erupted across the region, driven by similar grievances: corruption, unemployment (especially among the young), police brutality, and the suffocating absence of political freedom. Social media — Facebook, Twitter, YouTube — played a crucial role, allowing protesters to organize, share footage of government violence, and bypass state-controlled media.

February 11, 2011: Tahrir Square in Cairo, packed with hundreds of thousands of Egyptians celebrating the fall of President Mubarak after 18 days of protests. The euphoria was short-lived — a military coup in 2013 restored authoritarian rule.
2011 - 2013

The Outcomes: Country by Country

Tunisia: The Only Success Story

Tunisia managed what no other Arab Spring country could: a genuine democratic transition. A new constitution was drafted through consensus between secular and Islamist parties. Free elections were held. The Tunisian National Dialogue Quartet won the Nobel Peace Prize in 2015 for its role in mediating the transition. However, even Tunisia's success proved fragile. Economic problems persisted, political disillusionment grew, and in 2021, President Kais Saied suspended parliament and consolidated power, effectively ending the democratic experiment. The one success story of the Arab Spring may have had an expiration date.

Egypt: Revolution, Counter-Revolution

On January 25, 2011, hundreds of thousands of Egyptians poured into Tahrir Square in Cairo, demanding the end of Hosni Mubarak's 30-year rule. After 18 extraordinary days of protest — met with police violence, internet shutdowns, and the deployment of thugs on camels and horses — Mubarak stepped down on February 11. Egypt held its first free election, which was won by Mohamed Morsi of the Muslim Brotherhood. But Morsi proved a divisive and ineffective leader, pushing through an Islamist-leaning constitution and alienating secular Egyptians, the military, and the judiciary. In July 2013, General Abdel Fattah el-Sisi led a military coup, overthrowing Morsi. The subsequent crackdown was brutal: the Rabaa massacre of August 2013 saw security forces kill at least 800 pro-Morsi protesters (Human Rights Watch called it the worst mass killing in Egypt's modern history). Sisi became president and has ruled with an iron fist ever since, presiding over the most repressive government in Egypt's modern history. The revolution had come full circle — from dictatorship to dictatorship, with a brief, chaotic democratic interlude.

Libya: From Dictator to Chaos

Protests against Muammar Gaddafi's 42-year dictatorship quickly escalated into armed rebellion. A NATO military intervention, authorized by the UN Security Council to "protect civilians," provided crucial air support to the rebels. Gaddafi was captured and killed by rebel fighters in October 2011, in gruesome footage that went viral. But the aftermath was disastrous. Libya fractured into competing territories controlled by rival militias, warlords, and eventually two competing governments. The country became a hub for weapons trafficking (arms flooded across the Sahel, fueling conflicts in Mali and beyond), human smuggling (with migrants from sub-Saharan Africa enduring horrific conditions), and extremism. The power vacuum allowed ISIS to establish a foothold in the coastal city of Sirte. Libya remains unstable to this day.

Yemen: Humanitarian Catastrophe

Protests forced President Ali Abdullah Saleh to step down, but the transition collapsed. The Houthi movement — a Zaydi Shia rebel group from Yemen's north, backed by Iran — seized the capital Sana'a in 2014. In 2015, a Saudi Arabia-led military coalition intervened to restore the internationally recognized government. The resulting war created what the United Nations called the world's worst humanitarian crisis: over 150,000 people killed, millions on the brink of famine, cholera outbreaks affecting hundreds of thousands, and a generation of children growing up in a destroyed country. The Houthis would later play a significant role in the regional escalation following October 7, 2023, attacking commercial shipping in the Red Sea.

Bahrain: Crushed with Saudi Help

Bahrain's Shia-majority population (ruled by a Sunni monarchy, the Al Khalifa family) staged massive pro-democracy protests centered on the Pearl Roundabout in Manama. The government's response was swift and brutal: Saudi Arabia sent troops across the causeway to help crush the uprising. The Pearl Roundabout monument was demolished. Protesters were arrested, tortured, and imprisoned. Doctors who treated wounded protesters were put on trial. The crackdown succeeded in ending the visible protests but did nothing to address the underlying sectarian discrimination. The Bahrain uprising, more than any other, exposed the contradiction at the heart of Western policy in the region: the United States maintained its Fifth Fleet headquarters in Bahrain and said little as its ally crushed a pro-democracy movement.

The Arab Spring protests of 2011 toppled 4 governments in 2 years (Tunisia, Egypt, Libya, Yemen) — but only Tunisia maintained a democratic transition, and even that experiment was effectively reversed by 2021. The uprisings were triggered by a single street vendor's self-immolation in a provincial Tunisian town — proof that one act of desperation can reshape an entire region.

2011 - Present

The Syrian Civil War

The worst outcome of the Arab Spring — by almost any measure — was Syria. In March 2011, teenagers in the southern city of Daraa spray-painted anti-government graffiti on a school wall, inspired by the revolutions in Tunisia and Egypt. They were arrested and tortured by the security forces. When their families protested, security forces opened fire. The protests spread. The government of President Bashar al-Assad, an Alawite (a minority Shia sect) ruling a majority Sunni country, responded with escalating brutality: mass arrests, torture, snipers firing on peaceful demonstrators, and eventually artillery bombardments of entire neighborhoods.

By late 2011, defections from the Syrian army had created an armed opposition, and the country descended into full-scale civil war. The conflict rapidly became a proxy war involving virtually every major regional and global power. Assad was backed by Russia (which provided devastating air power and vetoed UN action repeatedly), Iran (which sent military advisors and billions in financial support), and Hezbollah (which sent thousands of fighters from Lebanon). Various rebel factions received support from Turkey, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the United States — though the opposition was fragmented into hundreds of groups with competing agendas, ranging from secular democrats to hardline jihadists.

The humanitarian toll was staggering: over 500,000 people killed, 13 million displaced (half the country's pre-war population), and 6.6 million forced to flee Syria entirely — creating the largest refugee crisis since World War II. Chemical weapons were used repeatedly against civilians, most horrifically in the Ghouta attack of August 2013, which killed over 1,400 people. President Obama had declared chemical weapons use a "red line" but ultimately did not follow through with military action, a decision that emboldened Assad and damaged American credibility in the region. Russia's military intervention in September 2015 tipped the balance decisively in Assad's favor. The dream of the Syrian revolution — a democratic Syria — was buried under rubble, barrel bombs, and the cynical calculations of great powers. Assad eventually fell in late 2024 when rebel forces captured Damascus, ending his family's decades-long grip on power, though Syria's future remained deeply uncertain.

What is ISIS / ISIL / Daesh?

ISIS stands for the Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (also called ISIL — Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant). Daesh is the Arabic acronym for the same group, often used by opponents because the word sounds similar to Arabic words meaning "to trample" or "bigot." The group grew out of al-Qaeda in Iraq during the U.S. occupation and declared a "caliphate" in 2014, claiming religious authority over all Muslims worldwide. It is distinct from other Islamist groups in its extreme brutality, its territorial ambitions, and its sophisticated use of social media for recruitment and propaganda. Its territorial "caliphate" was militarily defeated by 2019, but its ideology persists.

2014 - 2019

The Rise and Fall of ISIS

Out of the chaos of Syria and the wreckage of post-invasion Iraq emerged the Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (ISIS, also known as ISIL or Daesh). Its roots lay in al-Qaeda in Iraq, the Sunni insurgent group that had fought the American occupation. Under the leadership of Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, a shadowy Iraqi with a PhD in Islamic studies, the group rebranded, reorganized, and exploited the power vacuums on both sides of the Iraq-Syria border.

In June 2014, ISIS fighters swept out of Syria and captured Mosul, Iraq's second-largest city, in a matter of days. The Iraqi army — trained and equipped by the United States at a cost of $25 billion — simply melted away, abandoning American weapons, vehicles, and equipment that ISIS gleefully seized. On June 29, standing in the Great Mosque of al-Nuri in Mosul, al-Baghdadi declared the establishment of a "caliphate" — an Islamic state claiming religious authority over all Muslims worldwide. At its peak, ISIS controlled territory the size of the United Kingdom, spanning from eastern Syria to central Iraq, with a population of approximately 8 million people.

The brutality of ISIS was medieval in its cruelty and modern in its propagation. The group produced slickly edited videos of beheadings, immolations (Jordanian pilot Moaz al-Kasasbeh was burned alive in a cage), and mass executions. It systematically enslaved thousands of Yazidi women and girls — an ancient religious minority in northern Iraq — in what the United Nations formally recognized as genocide. It destroyed irreplaceable archaeological treasures in Palmyra, Nimrud, and Mosul. And it inspired or directed terrorist attacks across the globe: Paris (November 2015, 130 killed), Brussels (March 2016), Nice (July 2016), Manchester (May 2017), and dozens more.

The military campaign to defeat ISIS was waged primarily by a U.S.-led international coalition providing air power and special forces support, in partnership with Kurdish fighters (the Syrian Democratic Forces in Syria and the Peshmerga in Iraq) and Iraqi government forces backed by Iranian-supported Shia militias. The battle for Mosul (2016-2017) lasted nine months and left much of the city in ruins. The final ISIS territorial stronghold, the village of Baghouz in eastern Syria, fell in March 2019. Al-Baghdadi was killed in a U.S. special forces raid in October 2019. But while the "caliphate" was destroyed as a territorial entity, ISIS's ideology persists, its cells continue to operate across Africa, Central Asia, and Southeast Asia, and the conditions that produced it — sectarianism, governance failures, and the despair of marginalized Sunni populations — remain largely unaddressed.

ISIS Territory (2014-2017): At its peak in 2014-2015, the Islamic State controlled territory across eastern Syria and western Iraq — an area roughly the size of the United Kingdom, with a population of ~8 million.
2011 - 2023

The Saudi-Iran Cold War

Running beneath all of these events — the Arab Spring, the Syrian war, the rise of ISIS, the conflicts in Yemen and Bahrain — was a deeper structural conflict: the rivalry between Saudi Arabia and Iran. This is often described as a "Sunni-Shia" struggle, but that framing, while partially accurate, is also misleading. At its core, this is a geopolitical competition between two regional powers — one a Sunni monarchy allied with the United States, the other a Shia theocracy that defines itself in opposition to American hegemony — each seeking dominance in the Middle East.

The proxy wars were everywhere. In Yemen, Saudi Arabia led a coalition bombing campaign against the Iran-backed Houthis. In Lebanon, Iran funded and armed Hezbollah, which Saudi Arabia designated as a terrorist organization. In Iraq, Iran-backed Shia militias gained enormous power after the defeat of ISIS, effectively giving Tehran a corridor of influence stretching from Iran through Iraq and Syria to Lebanon — the so-called "Shia Crescent." In Bahrain, Saudi Arabia intervened to crush a Shia-majority uprising. In Syria, Iran poured resources into saving Assad while Saudi Arabia funded rebel groups trying to topple him.

The weaponization of the Sunni-Shia divide — a theological schism dating back to the 7th century — for 21st-century geopolitical purposes has been one of the most destructive dynamics in the modern Middle East. Millions of ordinary Sunnis and Shias who lived together peacefully for centuries found their identities politicized and their communities pitted against each other by leaders in Riyadh and Tehran who cared more about regional influence than religious truth. In a surprising diplomatic shift, China brokered a Saudi-Iran normalization agreement in March 2023, which led to a reduction in some tensions — though the underlying rivalry, and the devastation it has caused, remains a defining feature of the contemporary Middle East.

What is "Normalization" in Diplomacy?

Normalization refers to the process of establishing full, formal diplomatic relations between countries that previously had no official ties or were in a state of hostility. In the Middle East context, it specifically describes Arab or Muslim-majority countries establishing diplomatic relations, trade, and cultural exchanges with Israel. For decades, most Arab states refused to recognize Israel, conditioning any relationship on resolution of the Palestinian issue. The Abraham Accords broke this linkage, with several Arab states normalizing relations with Israel without a Palestinian peace deal.

What are the Abraham Accords?

The Abraham Accords are a series of normalization agreements between Israel and several Arab states, brokered by the Trump administration and signed beginning in September 2020. The UAE, Bahrain, Morocco, and Sudan all established formal diplomatic relations with Israel — the first Arab-Israeli peace agreements since Jordan's treaty in 1994. Named after the patriarch Abraham, shared by Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, the accords were driven by shared concerns about Iran and economic interests rather than resolution of the Palestinian conflict, which was effectively sidelined.

From Abraham Accords to October 7

The late 2010s and early 2020s brought a dramatic reshuffling of Middle Eastern alliances. Arab states that had spent decades refusing to recognize Israel began normalizing relations, sidelining the Palestinian cause. Then, on October 7, 2023, the deadliest day for Jews since the Holocaust shattered every assumption about the conflict — and the war that followed became one of the defining moral crises of our time.

  • Saudi-Israel normalization appears within reach by September 2023; many analysts believe the prospect of permanent Palestinian marginalization motivates the timing of what happens next

December 2017

Trump Recognizes Jerusalem as Israel's Capital

On December 6, 2017, President Donald Trump announced that the United States would officially recognize Jerusalem as the capital of Israel and begin the process of moving the American embassy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem. The decision broke with decades of American foreign policy — every previous president, Republican and Democrat alike, had maintained the embassy in Tel Aviv, recognizing that Jerusalem's final status was one of the core issues to be resolved in Israeli-Palestinian negotiations.

For Israelis, the recognition was a long-overdue acknowledgment of reality: Jerusalem had been Israel's functioning capital since 1949, hosting the Knesset (parliament), the Supreme Court, and the Prime Minister's office. For Palestinians, it was a devastating blow. East Jerusalem — including the Old City with the Al-Aqsa Mosque, the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, and the Western Wall — had been occupied by Israel since 1967, and Palestinians had always envisioned it as the capital of their future state. The recognition effectively told Palestinians that the United States, the supposed "honest broker" of peace negotiations, had taken the other side.

The move was condemned by the United Nations General Assembly in a vote of 128 to 9. Protests erupted across the Palestinian territories and the broader Muslim world. But the diplomatic consequences were less severe than many predicted. No Arab country broke relations with the United States. And the recognition foreshadowed a broader shift: the Palestinian issue, long described as the "central cause" of the Arab world, was increasingly being sidelined by Arab governments more concerned with the Iranian threat, economic modernization, and their own geopolitical interests.

September 2020

The Abraham Accords

September 15, 2020: The Abraham Accords signing ceremony at the White House. UAE Foreign Minister Abdullah bin Zayed, Israeli PM Netanyahu, US President Trump, and Bahraini Foreign Minister Al Zayani.

On September 15, 2020, in a ceremony on the White House lawn, the United Arab Emirates and Bahrain signed normalization agreements with Israel — the first Arab states to establish formal diplomatic relations with the Jewish state since Jordan in 1994. The agreements were brokered by the Trump administration and named, with deliberate symbolism, the "Abraham Accords," invoking the patriarch shared by all three Abrahamic faiths.

Morocco and Sudan followed with their own normalization agreements in the subsequent months. For Israel, the accords were a diplomatic triumph of historic proportions. Direct flights opened between Tel Aviv, Dubai, and Manama. Israeli tourists flooded to the UAE. Business deals, intelligence-sharing, and military cooperation flourished. For the signatory Arab states, the benefits were equally tangible: the UAE received advanced F-35 fighter jets from the U.S. (part of the deal), Morocco received American recognition of its sovereignty over Western Sahara, and Sudan was removed from the U.S. state sponsors of terrorism list.

The accords represented a fundamental shift in the Arab world's approach to Israel. For decades, the Arab consensus — codified in the 2002 Arab Peace Initiative — was that normalization would come only after Israel agreed to a Palestinian state. The Abraham Accords abandoned that linkage entirely. The signatory states prioritized their own strategic interests — principally the shared threat from Iran and the economic opportunities of partnership with Israel's tech-driven economy — over Palestinian statehood. For Palestinians, the accords were a betrayal. They had lost their veto over Arab-Israeli relations. Their cause, once the unifying issue of the Arab world, had become an obstacle that Gulf states were willing to step around.

The Abraham Accords are named after the shared patriarch of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — a deliberate invocation of common heritage. Abraham (Ibrahim in Arabic, Avraham in Hebrew) is revered as the "father of nations" by all three faiths, making his name a powerful symbol for agreements meant to bridge the deepest divides in the Abrahamic world.

2022 - 2023

The Saudi-Israel Normalization Drive

The ultimate prize in this new diplomatic landscape was Saudi Arabia. As the custodian of Mecca and Medina — the two holiest sites in Islam — Saudi recognition of Israel would be transformative, effectively ending the Arab world's broader rejection of the Jewish state. Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS), the de facto ruler of Saudi Arabia, signaled increasing openness to normalization. Israeli officials flew to Saudi Arabia. American diplomats shuttled between Riyadh, Jerusalem, and Washington.

The emerging deal was ambitious: Saudi Arabia would recognize Israel in exchange for a U.S.-Saudi defense treaty, American support for a Saudi civilian nuclear program, and some form of Israeli concessions on the Palestinian issue (likely a commitment to a "pathway" toward statehood, though the details remained vague). By September 2023, senior officials on all sides were saying a deal was within reach. MBS told Fox News that normalization was getting "closer" every day. It would have been the most significant diplomatic realignment in the Middle East since Camp David in 1978.

Many analysts believe that it was precisely this prospect — the normalization of Saudi-Israeli relations, which would have further marginalized the Palestinian cause and cemented Hamas's irrelevance — that motivated the timing of what happened next.

Context: Why October 7 Happened When It Did

By late 2023, several converging factors set the stage for a major escalation. Saudi-Israeli normalization appeared imminent, which would have further marginalized Hamas and the Palestinian cause. Hamas had governed an increasingly impoverished Gaza under a 16-year blockade, with no political horizon for its population. Israel's security establishment had assessed Hamas as "deterred" and shifted attention to the West Bank and Iran. Meanwhile, Israel was consumed by a domestic crisis over judicial reform that had divided the country and distracted its intelligence services. Many analysts believe Hamas calculated that a dramatic attack would shatter the normalization process and force the Palestinian issue back to the center of global attention.

October 7, 2023

The October 7 Attack

In the early morning hours of Saturday, October 7, 2023 — the Jewish holiday of Simchat Torah, and 50 years and one day after the start of the Yom Kippur War — Hamas launched the most devastating attack on Israel in the country's history. Beginning at approximately 6:30 AM with a massive barrage of rockets (over 3,000 in the first minutes), Hamas fighters breached the security fence surrounding the Gaza Strip in multiple locations using explosives, bulldozers, and paragliders. Israeli surveillance systems were disabled by drone strikes. The vaunted barrier — billions of dollars of technology, sensors, and fortifications — was overrun in minutes.

Approximately 3,000 militants poured into southern Israel and attacked military bases, kibbutzim (communal farming communities), small towns, and the Nova music festival — an outdoor rave attended by thousands of young Israelis near Kibbutz Re'im. What followed were hours of horrific violence. Families were massacred in their homes. Entire kibbutzim — Be'eri, Kfar Aza, Nir Oz — were devastated. At the Nova festival, hundreds were hunted across open fields and gunned down. Approximately 1,200 people were killed, the vast majority civilians — making it the deadliest day for Jewish people since the Holocaust. Approximately 250 people were taken hostage back into Gaza, including children, elderly people, and soldiers.

The attack exposed catastrophic failures in Israeli intelligence and military preparedness. Israel's security establishment had assessed that Hamas was "deterred" and focused on internal Palestinian governance rather than military confrontation. Warnings from female intelligence observers stationed along the border, who had reported unusual Hamas training activity in the weeks before the attack, had been dismissed. The Israeli military took hours to respond effectively, leaving border communities to fend for themselves. For a nation built on the promise of "never again" — that Jews would never again be defenseless against those who sought to destroy them — the trauma was existential. The shock, grief, and rage were overwhelming.

October 2023 - 2025

The Gaza War

Israel's response was immediate and unprecedented in its scale. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu declared that Israel was "at war" and vowed to destroy Hamas. The Israeli Air Force launched the most intensive bombing campaign in the history of the conflict, striking thousands of targets across Gaza — Hamas command centers, tunnel networks, weapons depots, and the homes of Hamas commanders. Gaza's 2.3 million residents were ordered to evacuate from the north to the south, then from parts of the south, then again and again, each time into smaller, more overcrowded zones with fewer resources.

A ground invasion followed, with Israeli forces pushing into Gaza City and systematically working through the strip. Israel argued that Hamas had embedded its military infrastructure within civilian areas — placing command centers under hospitals, storing weapons in schools and mosques, and using the civilian population as human shields. Hamas and international observers disputed some of these claims while acknowledging that Hamas's tunnel network (estimated at 500+ kilometers) did run beneath civilian infrastructure. The fundamental question — how to fight an enemy embedded in one of the most densely populated places on Earth without massive civilian casualties — had no satisfactory answer.

The civilian toll was devastating. Tens of thousands of Palestinians were killed, a significant proportion of them women and children. Much of Gaza's infrastructure was destroyed — hospitals, schools, universities, mosques, churches, water treatment facilities, roads. The UN estimated that over 70% of Gaza's housing stock was damaged or destroyed. A humanitarian catastrophe unfolded: food supplies were severely restricted, clean water was nearly nonexistent, medical facilities were overwhelmed or destroyed, and famine conditions emerged, particularly in northern Gaza. Most of Gaza's 2.3 million people were displaced, many multiple times, crowded into tent cities with minimal sanitation.

Israel maintained that its operations were targeting Hamas and taking measures to minimize civilian harm, including warning calls, evacuation orders, and precision strikes. Critics — including UN officials, humanitarian organizations, and many governments — argued that the scale of destruction and civilian death toll was disproportionate and potentially constituted war crimes, regardless of Hamas's own violations of international law. The debate over proportionality, self-defense, collective punishment, and the laws of war became the central moral argument of the era.

January 2024

The ICJ Genocide Case

In December 2023, South Africa filed a case against Israel at the International Court of Justice (ICJ) in The Hague, accusing Israel of committing genocide against the Palestinian people in Gaza under the 1948 Genocide Convention. South Africa's legal team presented evidence including statements by Israeli government officials (some of whom had used dehumanizing language about Palestinians), the scale of civilian casualties, the destruction of civilian infrastructure, and the restrictions on humanitarian aid.

In January 2024, the ICJ issued provisional measures ordering Israel to take all measures within its power to prevent acts of genocide, to ensure the provision of humanitarian assistance, and to preserve evidence. The court did not rule on whether genocide was occurring — that determination would take years — but the fact that the ICJ found the case plausible enough to issue provisional measures was itself significant. Israel rejected the case as "morally repugnant" and argued that it was acting in self-defense against a genocidal terrorist organization.

The legal and moral debate intensified. Some legal scholars and governments argued that the scale of civilian casualties and destruction met the legal threshold for genocide or, at minimum, constituted war crimes and crimes against humanity. Others argued that genocide requires specific intent to destroy a people "as such," and that Israel's actions, however devastating, were directed at Hamas rather than Palestinians as a group. The International Criminal Court (ICC) separately issued arrest warrants for both Israeli leaders and Hamas commanders. Regardless of the eventual legal outcomes, the proceedings reflected the depth of the international divide over the conflict and marked a new chapter in the use of international law to address wartime conduct.

What is the BDS Movement?

BDS stands for Boycott, Divestment, and Sanctions — a Palestinian-led movement launched in 2005 that calls on individuals, companies, and governments to economically pressure Israel to end the occupation, grant equal rights to Palestinian citizens of Israel, and recognize the right of return for Palestinian refugees. Modeled on the anti-apartheid movement against South Africa, BDS campaigns target companies with ties to Israeli settlements or the military occupation. Supporters see it as nonviolent resistance; critics (including many Israelis and Western governments) argue it unfairly singles out Israel, sometimes veers into antisemitism, and seeks to delegitimize the state rather than change its policies.

2023 - 2025

Global Impact: Protests, Polarization, and Rising Hatred

The Gaza war triggered the largest wave of global protests related to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict in history. Millions marched in cities across Europe, the Middle East, Asia, Africa, and the Americas, demanding a ceasefire and an end to what many called Israel's "genocide" in Gaza. The protests were particularly intense on university campuses — in the spring of 2024, student encampments at Columbia University, UCLA, the University of Michigan, the Sorbonne, and dozens of other institutions demanded that their universities divest from companies profiting from the conflict, drawing explicit comparisons to the anti-apartheid divestment movement of the 1980s.

The polarization was extreme. Public discourse collapsed into two irreconcilable positions: one side saw Israel as a nation exercising its legitimate right to self-defense after the worst massacre of Jews since the Holocaust, while the other saw Israel as a colonial power committing genocide against an occupied people. Nuance — the ability to condemn Hamas's atrocities on October 7 AND oppose the scale of Israel's military response — became increasingly difficult to maintain. Social media amplified the most extreme voices on both sides. Friendships, families, and communities were torn apart.

Both antisemitism and Islamophobia surged globally. Jewish communities reported record levels of threats, vandalism, and violent attacks. Jewish students on campuses felt unsafe. Synagogues were vandalized. Muslim and Arab communities faced similar spikes in hate crimes, discrimination, and harassment. A six-year-old Palestinian-American boy was stabbed to death in Illinois in a hate crime. The conflict's poison seeped into communities thousands of miles from Gaza, confirming that the Israeli-Palestinian conflict — rooted in the shared geography and contested history of the Abrahamic faiths — remains the most emotionally charged political issue on Earth.

2023 - 2024

Regional Escalation

The Gaza war did not stay contained. It threatened to engulf the entire region in a wider conflict — the nightmare scenario that policymakers had feared for decades. Hezbollah opened a "support front" for Hamas almost immediately, launching rockets and anti-tank missiles at northern Israel from southern Lebanon. Israel responded with strikes on Hezbollah positions. A low-intensity border war escalated through 2024, with tens of thousands of Israeli and Lebanese civilians evacuated from border areas. Israel launched a major military operation against Hezbollah in late 2024, delivering devastating blows to the organization, including the killing of its longtime leader Hassan Nasrallah in a massive airstrike on Beirut's southern suburbs.

In April 2024, Iran launched its first-ever direct military strike on Israeli territory — over 300 drones, cruise missiles, and ballistic missiles fired from Iranian soil. It was a response to an Israeli strike on an Iranian diplomatic building in Damascus that killed senior Iranian military commanders. While the vast majority of the projectiles were intercepted by Israel, the United States, the United Kingdom, France, and Jordan in an unprecedented coordinated defense, the attack crossed a threshold that had held for four decades: Iran and Israel had never before engaged in direct, acknowledged military strikes on each other's territory.

Yemen's Houthi movement, declaring solidarity with Palestinians, began attacking commercial shipping in the Red Sea with missiles and drones, disrupting one of the world's most important trade routes. A U.S.-led naval coalition was deployed to protect shipping, and American and British forces struck Houthi positions in Yemen. The Houthis' ability to threaten global commerce — and their willingness to do so over a conflict thousands of miles away — demonstrated how deeply interconnected the Abrahamic world's conflicts had become.

2023 - 2025

The Hostage Crisis

Of the approximately 250 people taken hostage by Hamas on October 7 — Israelis and foreign nationals, ranging in age from infants to the elderly — their fate became one of the most agonizing dimensions of the conflict. In late November 2023, a week-long ceasefire facilitated by Qatar and Egypt secured the release of approximately 100 hostages in exchange for Palestinian prisoners held in Israeli jails. The released hostages — many of them women, children, and elderly people — described harrowing conditions of captivity in Gaza's tunnel network.

But dozens of hostages remained in Gaza, and their fate tore at the fabric of Israeli society. Families of the hostages demanded that the government prioritize a deal to bring them home, accusing Netanyahu of prolonging the war for political reasons. Massive weekly protests in Tel Aviv called for a ceasefire and hostage deal. The tension between the military objective of destroying Hamas and the moral imperative of saving the hostages became Israel's most painful internal dilemma. Some hostages were confirmed dead. Others were reportedly killed by Israeli airstrikes. Each piece of news deepened the agony. Negotiations continued fitfully through 2024 and into 2025, mediated by Qatar, Egypt, and the United States, with Hamas demanding a permanent ceasefire and full Israeli withdrawal in exchange for the remaining hostages.

2026

Where Things Stand

As of early 2026, the situation remains deeply unstable and unresolved. The Gaza Strip has been devastated on a scale that defies easy comprehension — entire neighborhoods erased, critical infrastructure destroyed, and a population of 2.3 million people left displaced, traumatized, and facing a humanitarian crisis of immense proportions. The question of who will govern Gaza, how it will be rebuilt, and whether Hamas has been truly eliminated as a military force remains unanswered.

Israel continues to grapple with the trauma of October 7 and its aftermath — the largest military operation in decades, deep divisions in society over the conduct of the war and the hostage crisis, and the question of what "victory" actually looks like. The Abraham Accords framework has been strained but not destroyed; Saudi normalization, which seemed imminent before October 7, has been indefinitely delayed. The two-state solution — long declared dead by some and essential by others — has been given new rhetorical life by the international community, but the conditions on the ground make its realization harder than ever.

The broader region remains volatile. Iran's nuclear program continues to advance. Hezbollah has been significantly weakened but not destroyed. Syria remains in transition after Assad's fall. Yemen's war grinds on. And the fundamental questions at the heart of this 4,000-year story — who has the right to this land, how do peoples with competing claims and incompatible narratives share sacred space, and whether justice and peace can coexist — remain as far from resolution as they have ever been.

Where We Stand: Perspectives

The Israeli-Palestinian conflict is defined not just by competing claims to land, but by competing narratives about history itself. Understanding both narratives — not to equate them morally, but to understand why each side sees the conflict as it does — is essential to understanding why resolution has proved so elusive.

Section at a Glance

  • Israelis frame the conflict as a 3,000-year indigenous connection to the land and a survival necessity after the Holocaust; Palestinians frame it as European colonial dispossession of the people who actually lived there
  • October 7 is seen by Israelis as proof that no negotiated peace is possible with Hamas; Palestinians see the Gaza war as confirmation that Israel's goal is permanent subjugation, not coexistence
  • The two-state solution remains the international consensus but faces growing obstacles: 700,000+ settlers, a divided Palestinian leadership, and Israeli governments hostile to Palestinian statehood
  • Generational change is reshaping the debate worldwide, with younger people increasingly viewing the conflict through a human rights lens rather than Cold War-era geopolitics

Growing support among younger Palestinians who see two states as no longer viable. Some right-wing Israelis also envision one state — but with continued Jewish control, not equal rights. Israelis overwhelmingly reject it: given demographic realities, a one-state solution would end Israel as a Jewish-majority state. Palestinians fear it would mean permanent subjugation under Israeli control. Neither national movement is willing to abandon its identity. No model for how to merge two deeply hostile populations into a single functioning democracy.
Confederation Two separate states (Israel and Palestine) that share certain institutions — possibly open borders, shared Jerusalem governance, coordinated security, freedom of movement and residency across both states. Something like an Israeli-Palestinian "EU" model. A small but growing number of Israeli and Palestinian academics and civil society groups. Some international think tanks. Has gained traction as a "third way" between two states and one state. Requires an unprecedented level of trust and cooperation between two populations currently at war. No historical precedent for confederating an occupier and occupied. Would require resolution of refugees, Jerusalem, and settlements — the same issues that have blocked every other proposal. Remains largely theoretical.
Status quo Continuation of the current situation: Israel controls all territory, the PA administers parts of the West Bank, Hamas controls Gaza (or its ruins), no negotiations, no resolution. Periodic escalations managed through military force and ceasefires. No one officially advocates for it, but it is the de facto policy of most Israeli governments since the early 2010s. "Managing the conflict" rather than resolving it has become the dominant approach. As October 7 demonstrated, the status quo is not stable — it produces catastrophic violence at unpredictable intervals. It entrenches occupation, breeds radicalization on both sides, and ensures that each generation inherits the conflict of its parents. Many argue that what some call the "status quo" is actually a slow-motion annexation.

Why Multiple Narratives Matter

Understanding competing narratives is not the same as treating them as morally equivalent. It is possible to recognize the historical suffering of both peoples, acknowledge the legitimacy of both national movements, condemn violence against civilians on all sides, and still reach conclusions about justice and rights. The goal of studying history is not to arrive at a comfortable "both sides" neutrality — it is to understand why people believe what they believe, what they have experienced, and what they fear. Only with that understanding can any meaningful conversation about the future begin. The greatest obstacle to peace has never been a lack of proposals — it has been a lack of willingness to see the other side as fully human.

Final Reflection

4,000 years of shared origins, divergent paths, golden ages of coexistence, and devastating conflicts. The story of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam is ultimately the story of humanity — our capacity for profound wisdom AND profound cruelty, sometimes in the name of the same God.

Understanding this history — from Abraham's covenant to today's headlines — doesn't give us easy answers. But it gives us context, empathy, and the knowledge that coexistence IS possible, because it has happened before.
