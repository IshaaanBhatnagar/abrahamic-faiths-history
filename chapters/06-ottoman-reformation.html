Chapter 6

Ottoman Empire & Reformation

1300 – 1700 CE

The fall of Constantinople, the expulsion of Jews and Muslims from Spain, the shattering of Christian unity by the Protestant Reformation, and the rise of a vast Islamic empire that offered refuge to the displaced. These four centuries reshaped the religious map of the world and gave birth to the modern nation-state.

Rise of the Ottomans

Where does the name "Ottoman" come from?

The Ottoman Empire gets its name from Osman I (also rendered "Othman"), the Turkish tribal leader who founded a small principality in northwest Anatolia (modern Turkey) around 1299. "Ottoman" is simply the European rendering of "Osmanli" -- "the people of Osman." From this modest beginning, the empire grew into one of the largest and longest-lasting in history, eventually spanning three continents and lasting over 600 years (1299-1922).

From a small Turkish principality on the frontier of the Byzantine Empire, the Ottomans built one of the most powerful and long-lasting empires in history. Their conquest of Constantinople in 1453 ended over a millennium of Christian Roman civilization in the East and sent shockwaves through Europe that would reshape geopolitics, trade, and religious identity for centuries to come.

Section at a Glance

  • Osman I founds a small frontier beylik around 1299 that grows into a superpower by blending Turkish, Persian, Byzantine, and Islamic traditions
  • The Ottomans cross into Europe in the 1350s and dominate the Balkans after the Battle of Kosovo (1389), using the devshirme system to build an elite Janissary corps from conscripted Christian boys
  • Mehmed II conquers Constantinople in 1453, ending 1,100 years of Byzantine Christian civilization and converting Hagia Sophia into a mosque
  • The millet system grants religious minorities (Greek Orthodox, Armenian, Jewish) communal self-governance -- more tolerant than most European states of the era, but still hierarchical
c. 1299

Osman I Founds the Ottoman Beylik

In the late 13th century, the once-mighty Seljuk Sultanate of Rum was crumbling under Mongol pressure, and Anatolia had fractured into dozens of small Turkish principalities known as beyliks. Among these was a modest territory in northwest Anatolia, near the Byzantine frontier, led by a tribal chieftain named Osman (also rendered Othman). The word "Ottoman" is simply the European rendering of "Osmanli" — the people of Osman. His beylik was small, but it held a strategic advantage: it sat on the border with the weakening Byzantine Empire, attracting ghazi warriors (frontier fighters motivated by a blend of Islamic zeal, plunder, and adventure) from across the Islamic world.

Osman's genius lay not only in military prowess but in political flexibility. He welcomed warriors regardless of ethnic background, incorporated Byzantine administrative practices, and married into influential local families. By his death around 1326, the Ottomans had captured the important Byzantine city of Bursa, which became their first real capital. What had begun as a minor frontier state was already punching well above its weight, and the pattern of pragmatic governance that Osman established — blending Turkish, Persian, Byzantine, and Islamic traditions — would define the empire for centuries.

1350s – 1389

Rapid Expansion Across the Balkans

Under Osman's successors — Orhan, Murad I, and Bayezid I — the Ottomans expanded with breathtaking speed. In the 1350s, they crossed the Dardanelles into Europe, establishing a foothold at Gallipoli. This was a momentous development: for the first time, a Muslim power held permanent territory in southeastern Europe. From Gallipoli, Ottoman armies pushed into Thrace, capturing Adrianople (Edirne) in 1369, which became their European capital. The Balkans, fragmented among squabbling Christian kingdoms, were unable to mount a unified defense.

The pivotal moment came at the Battle of Kosovo on June 15, 1389, when Sultan Murad I faced a coalition of Serbian, Bosnian, and other Balkan forces under Prince Lazar. The battle was bloody and its outcome ambiguous — both Murad I and Prince Lazar were killed — but it effectively ended Serbian independence and opened the Balkans to Ottoman domination. Over the following decades, Bulgaria, much of Greece, and large parts of the Balkans fell under Ottoman control. The Ottoman devshirme system — conscripting Christian boys, converting them to Islam, and training them as elite Janissary soldiers or administrators — created a powerful military caste that was both feared and, paradoxically, a vehicle for social mobility within the empire. By 1400, the Ottomans controlled more European territory than Asian, and Constantinople was virtually encircled.

May 29, 1453

The Fall of Constantinople

Fausto Zonaro's depiction of Mehmed II's army entering Constantinople through a breached gate

Constantinople had stood as the capital of Eastern Christianity for 1,100 years. Founded by the Roman Emperor Constantine in 330 CE, it was the seat of the Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Empire, the center of the Greek Orthodox world, and one of the most fortified cities on earth. Its legendary Theodosian Walls, built in the 5th century, had repelled Arab armies, Bulgarian hordes, Viking raiders, and even the Fourth Crusade's initial assaults (though the Crusaders did sack the city in 1204, fatally weakening it). By 1453, however, the once-mighty Byzantine Empire had shrunk to little more than the city itself and a few scraps of territory. Inside the walls, the population had dwindled from a peak of perhaps 500,000 to barely 50,000. The "Queen of Cities" was a shadow of its former self.

Sultan Mehmed II, just 21 years old but ferociously ambitious, assembled an army of 80,000-100,000 men and a fleet of over 120 ships. His secret weapon was a massive cannon designed by the Hungarian engineer Urban — a 27-foot-long bronze monster that could fire stone balls weighing over 600 pounds. The siege began on April 6, 1453, and for seven weeks, the great cannon pounded the ancient walls while Constantine XI, the last Byzantine Emperor, organized a desperate defense with perhaps 7,000 defenders (including 2,000 foreign volunteers, among them the Genoese commander Giovanni Giustiniani). In an audacious maneuver, Mehmed had 70 ships dragged overland on greased logs to bypass the chain blocking the Golden Horn harbor. On the night of May 28, the final assault came. Constantine XI reportedly stripped off his imperial regalia and charged into the breach with his men. His body was never identified. By morning, Constantinople — the city that had endured for over a millennium — had fallen.

The news sent shockwaves across Europe. Church bells tolled, and the Pope called (unsuccessfully) for a new crusade. For the Islamic world, it was a triumphant fulfillment of a famous hadith (a hadith is a recorded saying or action of the Prophet Muhammad, passed down through a chain of narrators; the collected hadiths are second in authority only to the Quran in Islamic law and theology) attributed to the Prophet Muhammad, who had reportedly said: "You will conquer Constantinople. What a wonderful army that army will be, and what a wonderful commander that commander will be." Mehmed styled himself "Kayser-i Rum" — Caesar of Rome — claiming the legacy of the Roman Empire for himself. The city was renamed Istanbul (from the Greek "eis tin polin" — "into the city"), though it would continue to be called Constantinople in the West for centuries. The fall marked the definitive end of the Roman Empire in any form, and it fundamentally altered the balance of power between Christianity and Islam.

1453 – 1460s

Mehmed the Conqueror's Pragmatic Tolerance

Mehmed II, painted by the Venetian artist Gentile Bellini in 1480 — a symbol of the Sultan's engagement with European culture

Mehmed II was not merely a conqueror; he was a sophisticated ruler with a genuine interest in philosophy, science, and the cultures he absorbed. After allowing his troops the customary three days of plunder (standard practice in medieval warfare for a city that refused surrender), Mehmed moved quickly to rebuild and repopulate Constantinople. He understood that a depopulated ruin was worthless; he needed a thriving, cosmopolitan capital. To this end, he established the millet system, a form of religious communal autonomy that would become a hallmark of Ottoman governance.

Under the millet system, each recognized religious community — Greek Orthodox, Armenian Christian, Jewish — was granted the right to govern its own internal affairs: marriage, divorce, inheritance, education, and religious practice were all managed by communal leaders. Mehmed personally appointed Gennadios II as the Greek Orthodox Patriarch and guaranteed the continuation of the Patriarchate, which had been the spiritual head of Eastern Christianity for over a thousand years. He also actively invited Jews to settle in Istanbul, recognizing their value as merchants, artisans, and physicians. Christians and Jews were classified as dhimmis (protected peoples) — they paid a special tax (jizya) and faced certain restrictions, but they were free to practice their faiths and were protected by law. This was not modern religious freedom, but in a 15th-century context — an era when European states were expelling or forcibly converting religious minorities — Ottoman tolerance was remarkable and attracted displaced communities from across the continent.

The Ottoman Empire's millet system allowed each religious community to govern its own personal law -- Jewish courts for Jews, canon law for Christians, sharia for Muslims. This form of legal pluralism was virtually unknown in Europe until the modern era, where the assumption was one kingdom, one law, one faith. Elements of the millet system's approach to communal autonomy can still be seen today in Israel's personal status laws, where marriage and divorce are governed by each citizen's religious community.

537 CE – 1453 – Today

Hagia Sophia: Sacred Space Across Faiths

Hagia Sophia in modern Istanbul — its dome and minarets a visual symbol of its layered Christian and Islamic history

No building better embodies the intertwined history of Christianity and Islam than Hagia Sophia. Originally built in 537 CE by the Byzantine Emperor Justinian I, it was the largest cathedral in the world for nearly a thousand years. Its massive dome — 105 feet in diameter, rising 180 feet above the floor — was an engineering marvel that seemed to float on a ring of light from 40 windows at its base. The historian Procopius, writing at the time of its construction, said the dome appeared "suspended from heaven by a golden chain." The interior was adorned with gold mosaics, marble columns plundered from ancient temples, and shimmering walls that created an otherworldly atmosphere. For nearly a millennium, it was the cathedral church of the Ecumenical Patriarch and the site of imperial coronations.

When Mehmed II entered Constantinople, Hagia Sophia was one of his first stops. According to tradition, he was so awed by the building's beauty that he struck a soldier who was prying up the marble floor, declaring: "The plunder of goods and captives is yours; the buildings are mine." He ordered the building converted into a mosque. Islamic calligraphy was added, a mihrab (prayer niche oriented toward Mecca) was installed, and over the following decades, four towering minarets were erected at its corners. The Christian mosaics — depicting Christ, the Virgin Mary, and Byzantine emperors — were gradually plastered over, though not destroyed. For nearly 500 years, Hagia Sophia served as the principal mosque of the Ottoman Empire. In 1934, under Ataturk's secular Turkish Republic, it was converted into a museum, and the plaster covering the mosaics was carefully removed, revealing the Christian art alongside the Islamic calligraphy in a powerful symbol of coexistence. In 2020, the Turkish government reconverted it to a mosque, a decision that drew international controversy and underscored how this 1,500-year-old building remains a living flashpoint in the ongoing dialogue between civilizations.

1492: The Year That Changed Everything

No single year in history packed more world-altering events into twelve months. The fall of the last Muslim kingdom in Europe, the expulsion of an entire religious community, and the voyage that would connect the Old World to the New — all happened in 1492. These events did not occur in isolation; they were deeply intertwined, driven by the same religious fervor, monarchical ambition, and economic calculation.

Section at a Glance

  • Ferdinand and Isabella conquer Granada on January 2, 1492, ending 781 years of Muslim rule in Iberia and destroying the multi-faith civilization of Al-Andalus
  • The Alhambra Decree (March 1492) expels all Jews from Spain -- roughly 200,000 people forced to convert, flee, or die -- ending a community that had flourished for over a millennium
  • The Ottoman Empire, under Sultan Bayezid II, welcomes Spanish Jews, with Bayezid reportedly mocking Ferdinand for impoverishing Spain to enrich Turkey
  • Columbus sails from Spain in August 1492 backed by the same monarchs, launching European colonization of the Americas and reshaping the global balance of power
January 2, 1492

The Fall of Granada: End of Al-Andalus

The Alhambra — the exquisite palace-fortress of the Nasrid dynasty, the last Muslim rulers of Iberia

On January 2, 1492, the armies of Ferdinand of Aragon and Isabella of Castile entered the city of Granada, accepting the surrender of Boabdil (Abu Abdallah Muhammad XII), the last sultan of the Nasrid dynasty. With this, 781 years of Muslim presence in the Iberian Peninsula came to an end. The Reconquista — the long, often halting Christian campaign to recapture the peninsula from Islamic rule — was finally complete. It had begun in 711, when a Berber-led Muslim army crossed the Strait of Gibraltar and rapidly conquered nearly all of Visigothic Spain. For centuries, Muslim-ruled Al-Andalus had been one of the most sophisticated civilizations in medieval Europe, renowned for its architecture (the Alhambra, the Mezquita of Cordoba), its scholarship (translations of Greek philosophy, advances in mathematics and medicine), and its periods of convivencia — the relatively peaceful coexistence of Muslims, Christians, and Jews.

As Boabdil rode out of Granada for the last time, he paused on a mountain pass to look back at his lost kingdom and reportedly wept. His mother, Aixa, is said to have delivered one of history's most cutting rebukes: "Weep like a woman for what you could not defend as a man." Whether or not this exchange actually occurred, the "Last Sigh of the Moor" (El Ultimo Suspiro del Moro) became a powerful symbol of loss and nostalgia. The Treaty of Granada had promised Muslim inhabitants the right to continue practicing their faith, keep their property, and maintain their customs. These promises were systematically broken over the following years. Forced conversions began, Arabic books were burned, and the remaining Muslims (moriscos) faced escalating persecution until their final expulsion from Spain in 1609-1614. A civilization that had flourished for nearly eight centuries was erased from European soil.

What do "Sephardic" and "Ashkenazi" mean?

These are the two major cultural branches of the Jewish diaspora. Sephardic Jews (from "Sepharad," the Hebrew word for Spain) trace their heritage to the Iberian Peninsula and, after the expulsions described below, to North Africa, the Ottoman Empire, and the Middle East. Ashkenazi Jews (from "Ashkenaz," the Hebrew word for Germany) trace their roots to medieval France and Germany, later spreading to Eastern Europe. The two groups developed distinct languages (Ladino vs. Yiddish), liturgical traditions, customs, and cuisines, though they share the same core religious texts and beliefs.

March 31, 1492

The Alhambra Decree: Expulsion of the Jews

Less than three months after the fall of Granada, Ferdinand and Isabella issued the Alhambra Decree (also known as the Edict of Expulsion) on March 31, 1492. It ordered all Jews in Spain to convert to Christianity or leave the kingdom within four months. Jews had lived in Iberia for over a thousand years — since Roman times — and Sephardic Jewish culture (from "Sepharad," the Hebrew name for Spain) was one of the richest intellectual and cultural traditions in the Jewish world. Major figures like Maimonides, the great philosopher and legal scholar, and Judah Halevi, the poet, were products of Iberian Jewish civilization. Now, in a stroke of a pen, this entire world was condemned to extinction.

Approximately 200,000 Jews faced the impossible choice: abandon the faith of their ancestors or abandon everything they owned. Those who chose exile lost their property, were forbidden from taking gold or silver, and had to sell their homes and businesses at fire-sale prices. Thousands converted — these conversos (also called "New Christians" or, pejoratively, marranos) nominally accepted Christianity but many continued to practice Judaism in secret, at enormous personal risk. Those who left scattered across the Mediterranean in what became the great Sephardic diaspora. Many went to Portugal, only to face forced conversion there in 1497. Others fled to North Africa, Italy, the Netherlands, and — most significantly — the Ottoman Empire, which actively welcomed them.

The Alhambra Decree was not reversed until 1968, and Spain did not formally offer citizenship to descendants of expelled Sephardic Jews until 2015. The cultural and economic damage to Spain was enormous: the country lost some of its most educated, entrepreneurial, and cosmopolitan citizens. The Sephardic communities that took root elsewhere — in Thessaloniki, Istanbul, Amsterdam, and beyond — carried with them the Ladino language (a form of medieval Spanish written in Hebrew script), distinctive liturgical traditions, and a bittersweet memory of the land they had been forced to leave.

Established 1478 — Lasted 350+ Years

What is an Inquisition?

An inquisition (from the Latin inquisitio, meaning "inquiry") is a formal investigation into heresy -- beliefs or practices that contradict official Church teaching. The term came to refer to institutional bodies established by the Catholic Church (and in Spain's case, the Crown) to identify, interrogate, and punish suspected heretics. The Spanish Inquisition was the most famous and long-lasting, primarily targeting Jews and Muslims who had converted to Christianity but were suspected of secretly practicing their original faiths.

The Spanish Inquisition

The Spanish Inquisition, formally established by papal bull in 1478 at the request of Ferdinand and Isabella, was the enforcement mechanism behind Spain's drive for religious uniformity. Unlike the medieval Papal Inquisition, the Spanish Inquisition was controlled by the Spanish crown, making it as much a tool of state power as of religious orthodoxy. Its primary targets were conversos — Jewish converts to Christianity suspected of secretly practicing Judaism — and later moriscos (Muslim converts) suspected of maintaining Islamic practices in private. The first Grand Inquisitor, Tomas de Torquemada (himself of converso ancestry, ironically), oversaw the creation of a vast bureaucratic apparatus of surveillance, denunciation, and punishment.

The Inquisition's methods included imprisonment, interrogation under torture (the rack, the strappado, waterboarding), and the auto-da-fe ("act of faith") — a public ceremony in which the accused were sentenced and, in some cases, burned at the stake. It is estimated that around 2,000 people were executed during the Inquisition's long history, and tens of thousands more were imprisoned, fined, or forced into public humiliation. Beyond the direct victims, the Inquisition created a pervasive culture of fear and suspicion. "Limpieza de sangre" (purity of blood) statutes barred anyone with Jewish or Muslim ancestry from holding public office, joining religious orders, or attending universities. The Inquisition was not formally abolished until 1834 — over 350 years after its founding — making it one of the longest-running institutional persecutions in history.

August 3, 1492

Columbus Sets Sail

On August 3, 1492 — one day after the final deadline for Jewish expulsion from Spain — Christopher Columbus set sail from the port of Palos de la Frontera with three ships: the Nina, the Pinta, and the Santa Maria. The timing was not coincidental. Several historians have noted that Columbus's expedition was partly funded by wealth confiscated from expelled Jews and conversos, and that key figures in securing royal backing for the voyage (such as Luis de Santangel, a converso financier) came from the very community being destroyed. Some scholars have even speculated, though without definitive proof, that Columbus himself may have been of converso origin.

Columbus's expedition to find a western sea route to Asia was driven by the same religious and economic motivations that fueled the Reconquista and the expulsions. Ferdinand and Isabella saw themselves as champions of Christendom, and they dreamed of outflanking the Islamic world by finding new trade routes and new peoples to convert. When Columbus stumbled upon the Caribbean islands on October 12, 1492, he set in motion the European colonization of the Americas — a process that would devastate indigenous populations, create vast colonial empires, flood Europe with New World silver and gold, and permanently alter the demographics, ecology, and power structures of the entire planet. The year 1492, then, was not just a turning point for Spain or for European Jews and Muslims; it was the hinge on which the medieval world swung into the modern one.

1492 – 1500s

The Ottoman Welcome: Bayezid II and the Sephardic Refugees

While Ferdinand and Isabella were expelling Jews, Sultan Bayezid II of the Ottoman Empire saw an extraordinary opportunity. He reportedly mocked Ferdinand's supposed wisdom: "You call Ferdinand a wise king — he who has impoverished his own country and enriched mine!" Bayezid sent Ottoman ships to the coasts of Spain and Portugal to transport Sephardic Jewish refugees to Ottoman lands. He issued a firman (imperial decree) welcoming the exiles and threatening punishment for any Ottoman subject who mistreated them.

The influx of Sephardic Jews enriched the Ottoman Empire enormously. They brought with them advanced knowledge of European languages, trade networks, medicine, and the newly invented printing press (the first Hebrew printing press in the Ottoman Empire was established in Istanbul in 1493, decades before the Ottomans allowed Arabic or Turkish printing). Sephardic communities took root in Istanbul, Thessaloniki (Salonika), Izmir, Sarajevo, and cities across the empire. Thessaloniki, in particular, became a majority-Jewish city — a remarkable phenomenon in the pre-modern world. Sephardic Jews served as physicians to sultans, diplomats, and key intermediaries in trade between the Ottoman and European worlds. The contrast between the Ottoman embrace and the Spanish rejection of the same people became one of the most telling comparisons in the history of religious tolerance.

When Spain expelled its Jews in 1492, Sultan Bayezid II sent Ottoman ships to rescue them from the coasts of Iberia. He reportedly declared: "Ferdinand is a fool -- he impoverishes his own country to enrich mine." History proved him right: the Sephardic refugees brought printing presses, medical expertise, European trade networks, and linguistic skills that enriched the Ottoman Empire for centuries.

The Human Cost of the Expulsions

The expulsion of Jews from Spain in 1492 was one of the largest forced population transfers in pre-modern history. An estimated 200,000 Jews were displaced, losing their homes, businesses, and centuries of cultural heritage. Thousands died from disease, shipwreck, and exploitation during their journeys into exile. Those who stayed and converted (conversos) lived under constant suspicion, subject to Inquisitorial surveillance for generations. The destruction of Muslim life in Iberia was equally devastating: after the moriscos were finally expelled in 1609-1614, entire regions of Spain were depopulated, and the agricultural expertise that had sustained Iberian farming for centuries was lost. These were not abstract policy decisions — they were acts of mass cruelty that shattered families, destroyed communities, and inflicted generational trauma whose echoes persist to this day.

The Protestant Reformation

What was "the Reformation"?

The Protestant Reformation was a 16th-century movement to reform what its leaders saw as corruption and theological error in the Roman Catholic Church. The word "reform" comes from the Latin for "to shape again" -- the reformers believed Christianity needed to be reshaped back to its original, biblical form. When the Catholic Church rejected these reforms, the movement split Western Christianity permanently, creating the Protestant churches (Lutheran, Calvinist, Anglican, and many others) alongside the existing Catholic Church. "Protestant" itself comes from the Latin protestari -- "to declare publicly."

In 1517, an obscure German monk challenged the authority of the Pope and shattered the religious unity of Western Christendom. The Protestant Reformation was not just a theological dispute — it was a political earthquake, a media revolution, and a social upheaval that permanently divided Europe along religious lines and set the stage for centuries of conflict.

Section at a Glance

  • Martin Luther posts his 95 Theses in 1517, attacking the sale of indulgences and sparking a movement that shatters Western Christian unity permanently
  • Luther's three radical principles -- sola scriptura, sola fide, and the priesthood of all believers -- eliminate the need for papal authority and a priestly class as intermediaries to God
  • The printing press turns Luther's ideas into the first viral media event in history: 300,000 copies of his works circulate within three years

1400s – 1510s

Background: Corruption in the Catholic Church

By the late medieval period, the Catholic Church — the dominant institution in Western Europe for over a thousand years — was riddled with corruption that even its own members acknowledged. The papacy had become entangled in Italian power politics; popes maintained lavish courts, funded wars, and engaged in nepotism on a grand scale. Pope Alexander VI (Rodrigo Borgia, r. 1492-1503) openly fathered children, used the papal office to enrich his family, and allegedly hosted orgies in the Vatican. Pope Julius II (r. 1503-1513) personally led armies into battle. Pope Leo X (r. 1513-1521), a Medici, was reportedly so focused on art patronage and luxury that he quipped upon his election: "God has given us the papacy; let us enjoy it."

The most visible and theologically problematic symptom of corruption was the sale of indulgences. In Catholic theology, an indulgence was a remission of the temporal punishment due for sins that had already been forgiven — essentially, a reduction of time in purgatory. Originally, indulgences were granted for acts of piety (pilgrimage, prayer, charitable works). By the early 1500s, however, they were being sold outright for cash. Johann Tetzel, a Dominican friar commissioned to sell indulgences in Germany to fund the construction of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, reportedly used the jingle: "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs." For ordinary Christians, this was a scandalous commercialization of salvation — and it was the specific abuse that provoked Martin Luther to act.

October 31, 1517

Martin Luther and the 95 Theses

The iconic (and likely mythologized) moment: Luther posting his 95 Theses on the church door at Wittenberg, 1517

On October 31, 1517 — the eve of All Saints' Day — Martin Luther, an Augustinian monk and theology professor at the University of Wittenberg, posted his "Disputation on the Power and Efficacy of Indulgences" (the 95 Theses) on the door of the Castle Church. The church door served as a kind of community bulletin board, and Luther's initial intent may have been simply to provoke an academic debate. But the 95 Theses were quickly translated from Latin into German, printed on the recently invented printing press (developed by Johannes Gutenberg in the 1440s), and distributed across Germany with extraordinary speed. Within weeks, Luther's arguments were being read in cities hundreds of miles from Wittenberg. It was, in a sense, the first viral media event in history.

Luther's arguments struck at the heart of papal authority. He argued that the Pope had no power over purgatory, that the sale of indulgences was a fraud, and that Christians could not buy their way to salvation. As the controversy escalated, Luther went further: he challenged the entire structure of the Catholic Church, arguing that salvation came through faith alone (sola fide), that the Bible was the sole authority on Christian doctrine (sola scriptura), and that every baptized Christian was a priest (the priesthood of all believers) — meaning there was no need for an intermediary clerical hierarchy between the individual and God. When Pope Leo X issued a papal bull threatening excommunication, Luther publicly burned it. At the Diet of Worms in 1521, summoned before Emperor Charles V to recant, Luther famously declared: "Here I stand. I can do no other. God help me." He was declared an outlaw, but was protected by the powerful Elector Frederick of Saxony, who sheltered him at Wartburg Castle. There, Luther translated the New Testament into German, making scripture accessible to ordinary people for the first time.

Martin Luther's 95 Theses spread across Germany in roughly two weeks and across all of Europe within two months -- thanks to the printing press. Between 1517 and 1520, an estimated 300,000 copies of Luther's various writings were printed and sold. This was the first "viral" content in human history, and it demonstrated that a new technology could shatter the information monopoly of an institution that had held power for over a thousand years.

1523 – 1543

Luther's Dark Legacy: Antisemitism

In 1523, Luther wrote "That Jesus Christ Was Born a Jew," a relatively sympathetic tract that argued Christians should treat Jews kindly in hopes of converting them. Luther believed that once the corruptions of Catholicism were swept away and the "true" Gospel was preached, Jews would naturally recognize Jesus as the Messiah. When this did not happen — when Jews continued to practice their own faith — Luther's attitude turned viciously hostile.

In 1543, Luther published "On the Jews and Their Lies," one of the most virulent antisemitic texts in European history. In it, he called for the burning of synagogues, the destruction of Jewish homes, the confiscation of Jewish books and property, the prohibition of rabbinic teaching under penalty of death, and the revocation of safe-conduct for Jews on highways. He described Jews in dehumanizing terms and urged secular authorities to drive them out. This was not a marginal footnote in Luther's career; it was a substantial work that influenced Protestant attitudes toward Jews for centuries. During the Nazi era, "On the Jews and Their Lies" was reprinted and cited as justification for anti-Jewish policies. At the Nuremberg trials, the Lutheran bishop Martin Sasse published a compendium of Luther's antisemitic writings to support the regime. Luther's legacy is thus deeply contradictory: he championed individual conscience and scriptural authority, yet he also provided theological ammunition for some of history's worst persecutions. Modern Lutheran churches have formally repudiated his antisemitic writings, but the damage they caused over centuries cannot be undone.

1534

Henry VIII and the Church of England

While Luther split from Rome over theology, England's break was driven by something far more personal: a king's desire for a divorce. Henry VIII wanted to annul his marriage to Catherine of Aragon (who had failed to produce a male heir) so he could marry Anne Boleyn. When Pope Clement VII refused the annulment — partly because Catherine was the aunt of Emperor Charles V, who effectively held the Pope hostage — Henry took matters into his own hands. The Act of Supremacy (1534) declared the King of England the "Supreme Head of the Church of England," severing ties with Rome entirely.

The English Reformation was thus initially about authority, not doctrine — Henry considered himself a good Catholic in all other respects and had even written a treatise against Luther that earned him the papal title "Defender of the Faith." But the break from Rome opened the door to Protestant influences. Under Henry's son Edward VI, genuine Protestant reforms were introduced. Under his daughter Mary I ("Bloody Mary"), Catholicism was briefly restored, and nearly 300 Protestants were burned at the stake. Finally, Elizabeth I established a "middle way" — the Elizabethan Settlement — that made the Church of England broadly Protestant in theology but retained many Catholic rituals and structures. This uniquely English compromise (the Anglican via media) created a church that was neither fully Reformed nor fully Catholic, and it shaped English (and later British and global) religious culture down to the present day.

1530s – 1560s

John Calvin and the Reformed Tradition

If Luther was the Reformation's passionate spark, John Calvin was its rigorous architect. A French lawyer and theologian who fled Catholic France, Calvin established himself in Geneva, Switzerland, transforming the city into what Scottish reformer John Knox called "the most perfect school of Christ that ever was on earth since the days of the Apostles." Calvin's theology centered on the absolute sovereignty of God and the doctrine of predestination — the idea that God had already determined, before the creation of the world, who would be saved and who would be damned. Human beings could not earn salvation through works or even choose it through faith; salvation was entirely God's decision.

Calvin's Geneva was a theocratic experiment: the church governed public morals, enforced strict codes of behavior (gambling, dancing, and lavish dress were prohibited), and punished heresy severely — the most infamous case being the burning of Michael Servetus in 1553 for denying the Trinity. Calvinism spread rapidly through France (Huguenots), the Netherlands (Dutch Reformed), Scotland (Presbyterians), and eventually to the English Puritans who would colonize New England. Some historians, most notably Max Weber, have argued that the Calvinist "work ethic" — the idea that worldly success might be a sign of God's favor — helped lay the psychological foundations for modern capitalism. Whatever the merits of that thesis, Calvinism was undeniably one of the most influential intellectual and social movements of the early modern period.

1545 – 1563

The Counter-Reformation: Rome Responds

The Catholic Church did not accept the Reformation passively. The Counter-Reformation (also called the Catholic Reformation) was a vigorous, multi-pronged response that reformed internal abuses, clarified doctrine, and launched an aggressive campaign to win back lost territory. Its centerpiece was the Council of Trent, which met in three sessions between 1545 and 1563. Trent reaffirmed core Catholic doctrines — the necessity of both faith and works for salvation, the authority of tradition alongside scripture, the real presence of Christ in the Eucharist, and the seven sacraments. But it also addressed legitimate grievances: the sale of indulgences was curtailed, clerical education was improved, and bishops were required to actually reside in their dioceses (a remarkable indictment of how lax things had become).

The other great engine of the Counter-Reformation was the Society of Jesus (the Jesuits), founded in 1540 by Ignatius of Loyola, a Basque soldier who experienced a religious conversion while recovering from war wounds. The Jesuits were organized with military discipline, took a special vow of obedience to the Pope, and threw themselves into education, scholarship, and foreign missions with extraordinary energy. They established schools and universities across Europe (many of which still exist today), sent missionaries to Asia, Africa, and the Americas, and served as confessors and advisors to Catholic monarchs. By the end of the 16th century, the Counter-Reformation had stabilized the Catholic position in southern Europe (Spain, Portugal, Italy, much of France, southern Germany, Poland) and had launched a global missionary effort. But the religious map of Europe was now permanently divided — Protestant north, Catholic south — and this division would fuel devastating wars for over a century.

Coffee was originally a Muslim drink, cultivated in Yemen and spread through Ottoman coffeehouses. When it reached Europe in the 16th century, some Christians called it "the devil's drink" and demanded it be banned. The controversy reached Pope Clement VIII, who agreed to taste the suspicious beverage before ruling -- and reportedly enjoyed it so much that he declared: "This devil's drink is so delicious we should cheat the devil and baptize it." Coffee was approved, and European cafe culture was born.

Key Reformation Doctrines Compared

Sola Fide — Faith Alone

Luther's most fundamental doctrine. He argued that human beings are saved by faith in God's grace alone, not by performing good works, buying indulgences, or following church rituals. This came from his reading of Paul's Epistle to the Romans: "The righteous shall live by faith." This directly challenged the Catholic position that both faith and works were necessary for salvation, and it undercut the entire apparatus of saints, relics, pilgrimages, and indulgences that the medieval church had built up over centuries.

Sola Scriptura — Scripture Alone

Protestants held that the Bible was the sole infallible source of religious authority. The Catholic Church's "Sacred Tradition" — the accumulated teachings of church councils, papal decrees, and the Church Fathers — was rejected as a co-equal source of doctrine. This had revolutionary implications: if the Bible was the ultimate authority, then every literate person could (and should) read it for themselves, rather than relying on priestly interpretation. Luther's translation of the Bible into German, and the subsequent flood of vernacular Bibles across Europe, democratized religious knowledge and fatally undermined the clergy's monopoly on scriptural interpretation.

Priesthood of All Believers

Luther argued that every baptized Christian was, in effect, a priest — there was no special spiritual status conferred by ordination. The entire hierarchical structure of the Catholic Church — from parish priests through bishops, archbishops, cardinals, and the Pope — was, in Luther's view, a human invention without biblical warrant. This was not merely a theological point; it was a direct attack on the political and economic power of the clerical establishment. If every believer had equal access to God, the massive institutional apparatus of the Church — and the wealth and authority it commanded — was unnecessary.

Predestination (Calvin)

Calvin took Luther's emphasis on God's sovereignty to its logical extreme. If salvation is entirely by God's grace (and not by human effort), then God must have already chosen — before the foundation of the world — who would be saved (the "elect") and who would be damned (the "reprobate"). This doctrine of double predestination was both terrifying and liberating: terrifying because one's eternal fate was already sealed, liberating because it removed all anxiety about whether one had done "enough" good works. Paradoxically, Calvinists often lived extremely disciplined, industrious lives — not to earn salvation, but as evidence that they were among the elect.

The Gutenberg Revolution

The printing press, developed by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440, was the technological catalyst that made the Reformation possible. Before printing, producing a single Bible took a scribe months of painstaking labor. By the 1500s, a print shop could produce hundreds of copies of a pamphlet in a single day. Between 1517 and 1520, Luther's writings sold an estimated 300,000 copies — an astonishing number for the era. Without the printing press, Luther's ideas would likely have remained a local academic dispute. With it, they ignited a continental revolution.

The Ottoman Golden Age

Under Suleiman the Magnificent, the Ottoman Empire reached its zenith — the largest and most powerful state in the Mediterranean world, stretching from Hungary to Yemen, from Algeria to Iraq. It was also an era of remarkable cultural and religious diversity, where Jews expelled from Europe found new homes, and mystical traditions flourished in the holy cities of the Galilee.

1520 – 1566

Suleiman the Magnificent

Suleiman I, known in the West as "the Magnificent" and in the Islamic world as "the Lawgiver" (Kanuni), reigned for 46 years (1520-1566) and presided over the Ottoman Empire at the peak of its power, wealth, and cultural achievement. Under his rule, the empire's territory expanded to encompass southeastern Europe (including Hungary after the Battle of Mohacs in 1526), the Middle East, North Africa, and the shores of the Indian Ocean. His navy, commanded by the legendary admiral Barbarossa, dominated the Mediterranean. At its height, the Ottoman Empire controlled approximately 2 million square miles and 25 million people — a population comparable to the largest European states of the era.

Suleiman's reign was not merely one of military expansion; it was a golden age of art, architecture, literature, and law. The great architect Sinan — often compared to Michelangelo — designed hundreds of structures, including the Suleymaniye Mosque in Istanbul, one of the masterpieces of Islamic architecture. Suleiman personally oversaw a comprehensive reform of the Ottoman legal code, harmonizing secular law (kanun) with Islamic law (sharia) in a system that governed the empire for centuries. His court was a center of poetry, calligraphy, and miniature painting. He was himself a gifted poet who wrote under the pen name "Muhibbi" (the Lover), and his epic love for his wife Hurrem Sultan (Roxelana) — a former slave who became the most powerful woman in the Ottoman world — became legendary.

1535 – 1541

Suleiman Rebuilds Jerusalem's Walls

One of Suleiman's most enduring physical legacies stands in the holy city of Jerusalem. Between 1535 and 1541, he ordered the complete reconstruction of the walls surrounding the Old City — the same walls that visitors see today, with their distinctive crenellations, gates, and towers. The walls stretch for approximately 2.5 miles and include the iconic Damascus Gate, Jaffa Gate, and Lions' Gate. Suleiman's rebuilding was both a practical defensive measure and a statement of imperial prestige: Jerusalem was sacred to all three Abrahamic faiths, and by restoring its walls, Suleiman presented himself as its protector and patron.

Under Ottoman rule, Jerusalem was a multi-faith city where Jews, Christians, and Muslims lived side by side, each community governing its own holy sites. The status quo arrangements that still govern the Church of the Holy Sepulchre today — with different Christian denominations controlling specific sections of the church under rules established by Ottoman firmans — date from this period. Suleiman also improved the water supply to the city and beautified the Dome of the Rock with the blue tiles that give it its distinctive appearance today. The Ottoman period in Jerusalem was generally one of relative calm and coexistence — a contrast to both the Crusader era that preceded it and the conflicts that would follow.

15th – 17th Centuries

The Millet System: Religious Autonomy

The Ottoman millet system was one of the most innovative approaches to managing religious diversity in the pre-modern world. Under this system, each major religious community — Greek Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Jewish, and later others — was recognized as a "millet" (from the Arabic word for "nation" or "community") with significant autonomy over its internal affairs. Each millet had its own religious leader (the Greek Orthodox Patriarch, the Armenian Patriarch, the Chief Rabbi) who was responsible to the Sultan for the community's behavior and tax payments.

Within their millets, these communities ran their own courts, schools, and charitable institutions. Marriage, divorce, inheritance, and personal status were governed by each community's own religious law — Jewish law for Jews, canon law for Christians, sharia for Muslims. Non-Muslims paid the jizya, a special tax in lieu of military service, and faced certain restrictions (they could not ride horses in some periods, were required to wear distinctive clothing, and their houses could not be taller than those of Muslims). These restrictions were unevenly enforced and varied greatly by time and place. The millet system was not equality in the modern sense — it was a hierarchical arrangement with Muslims at the top — but it provided a framework for religious coexistence that allowed minority communities to survive and, in many cases, thrive for centuries. It stands in sharp contrast to the forced conversions and expulsions that characterized much of contemporary European practice.

1500s – 1600s

Jewish Life Flourishes Under Ottoman Rule

The Ottoman Empire became the most important center of Jewish life in the early modern world. Sephardic refugees from Spain and Portugal, Ashkenazi Jews from Central Europe, and indigenous Romaniote Jews (Greek-speaking Jews who had lived in the region since Roman times) created vibrant, diverse communities across the empire. Istanbul became a major Jewish center, with synagogues, printing presses, and rabbinic academies. But the most remarkable Jewish city in the Ottoman Empire was Thessaloniki (Salonika), where Sephardic Jews became the majority population. Thessaloniki's Jewish community was so dominant that the city virtually shut down on Shabbat — the port closed, businesses stopped, and the city rested. This unique status lasted from the late 15th century until the 20th century, when the community was destroyed in the Holocaust.

Perhaps the most extraordinary chapter of Ottoman Jewish life unfolded in Safed (Tzfat), a small hilltop city in the Galilee region of what is today northern Israel. In the 16th century, Safed became the world capital of Jewish mysticism — Kabbalah. The great Rabbi Isaac Luria (known as "the Ari," or the Lion) arrived in Safed in 1570 and developed a revolutionary mystical system that reimagined the nature of creation, the structure of the divine, and the purpose of human existence. Lurianic Kabbalah — with its concepts of tzimtzum (divine contraction), shevirat hakelim (the shattering of the vessels), and tikkun olam (the repair of the world) — became the dominant form of Jewish mysticism and profoundly influenced Jewish theology, liturgy, and practice for centuries. Safed was also home to Rabbi Joseph Karo, who authored the Shulchan Aruch ("The Set Table") in 1563, which became the definitive code of Jewish law — the standard reference for Orthodox Jewish practice that is still consulted daily by rabbis and scholars around the world.

1501

The Safavid Empire: The Sunni-Shia Divide Hardens

In 1501, a fifteen-year-old named Ismail, leader of the militant Safaviyya Sufi order, conquered Tabriz and declared himself Shah of Persia. His first act was momentous: he proclaimed Twelver Shia Islam the state religion of his new empire. This was a revolutionary move. Although Persia had significant Shia populations, it was still predominantly Sunni. Shah Ismail I imposed Shia Islam by force, importing Shia scholars from Lebanon and Iraq, destroying Sunni mosques or converting them, and persecuting those who refused to convert. Within a generation, Persia was transformed into a Shia state — and it has remained one ever since, forming the historical core of what is today the Islamic Republic of Iran.

The Safavid conversion had enormous geopolitical consequences. The Sunni-Shia divide, which had existed since the 7th century as a theological and political disagreement within Islam, now mapped onto a great-power rivalry. The Ottoman Empire (Sunni) and the Safavid Empire (Shia) became bitter enemies, fighting repeated wars over Mesopotamia (modern Iraq), the Caucasus, and eastern Anatolia. Each side defined itself partly in opposition to the other: the Ottomans persecuted Shia populations (especially the Qizilbash in Anatolia, who were seen as a Safavid fifth column), while the Safavids persecuted Sunnis. This Ottoman-Safavid rivalry shaped the Sunni-Shia geopolitics of the Middle East for centuries — and its echoes are clearly audible today in the tensions between Saudi Arabia (Sunni) and Iran (Shia), and in the sectarian conflicts that have torn apart Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Yemen, and Bahrain.

The Ottoman Empire at its peak (c. 1683). At its greatest extent, the empire stretched from the gates of Vienna in the west to the Persian Gulf in the east, and from the Ukrainian steppes in the north to the Horn of Africa in the south. It controlled the holy cities of Mecca, Medina, and Jerusalem, and its capital Istanbul was one of the largest cities in the world.

Religious Wars & the Peace of Westphalia

The Reformation shattered Christian unity, but it also unleashed over a century of devastating religious wars. From France to the Holy Roman Empire, Catholics and Protestants slaughtered each other in conflicts that destroyed entire regions. The exhaustion and horror produced by these wars ultimately led to a revolutionary idea: that the state, not the church, should be sovereign — and that religious uniformity was not worth the cost of perpetual war.

1562 – 1598

The French Wars of Religion

France was torn apart by eight successive civil wars between Catholics and Huguenots (French Calvinists) between 1562 and 1598. The Huguenots, though a minority (perhaps 10% of the population), were concentrated among the nobility and the urban merchant class, giving them political and military power far beyond their numbers. The wars were characterized by shifting alliances, foreign intervention (Spain backed the Catholics, England and the Dutch backed the Huguenots), and appalling atrocities committed by both sides.

The most infamous episode was the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre, which began on August 24, 1572. With leading Huguenot nobles gathered in Paris for the wedding of the Protestant Henry of Navarre to the Catholic princess Marguerite de Valois — a marriage intended to promote reconciliation — King Charles IX, pressured by his mother Catherine de Medici, ordered the assassination of Huguenot leaders. The targeted killings quickly spiraled into a general massacre. Catholic mobs rampaged through Paris, butchering Huguenots in their homes and on the streets. The violence spread to other cities across France. Estimates of the dead range from 5,000 to 30,000 over the following weeks. Pope Gregory XIII celebrated the massacre with a Te Deum and had a commemorative medal struck. The wars finally ended with the Edict of Nantes (1598), in which Henry IV — the former Protestant Henry of Navarre, who had converted to Catholicism with the famous remark "Paris is worth a Mass" — granted Huguenots limited religious tolerance and political rights. It was an imperfect and ultimately fragile peace, but it was a landmark in the history of religious tolerance.

1618 – 1648

The Thirty Years' War

The Thirty Years' War (1618-1648) was the most destructive conflict in European history before the 20th century. It began as a religious conflict between Catholic and Protestant states within the Holy Roman Empire — triggered by the Defenestration of Prague in 1618, when Protestant Bohemian nobles threw two Catholic imperial governors out of a window (they survived, landing in a dung heap). But it quickly escalated into a general European war, drawing in Spain, France, Sweden, Denmark, the Dutch Republic, and numerous German principalities. By its end, the war had become less about religion and more about the raw struggle for power among European states — illustrated most vividly by Catholic France allying with Protestant Sweden against Catholic Habsburg Spain and Austria.

The devastation was almost beyond comprehension. Germany — the war's primary battlefield — was ravaged beyond recognition. Armies marched and counter-marched across the countryside, burning farms, sacking cities, and leaving famine and plague in their wake. Some regions of Germany lost 30-50% of their population; the total death toll is estimated at 4-8 million people, making it proportionally one of the deadliest conflicts in human history. The city of Magdeburg was sacked in 1631 by imperial forces, and approximately 20,000 of its 25,000 inhabitants were killed — an atrocity so shocking that "Magdeburg" became a verb meaning "to utterly destroy." The war shattered the illusion that religious truth could be imposed by force and created an overwhelming appetite for peace, stability, and a new political order.

While Christians were slaughtering each other over theological differences in the Thirty Years' War, the Ottoman Empire -- just to the southeast -- was governing a population of Muslims, Christians, and Jews under the millet system with relative stability. The Ottoman ambassador to France reportedly expressed bewilderment that Europeans would destroy entire countries over doctrinal disagreements that the Ottomans managed through communal autonomy. The contrast was not lost on early Enlightenment thinkers like Voltaire, who held up Ottoman tolerance as a rebuke to European fanaticism.

1648

The Peace of Westphalia: Birth of the Modern World

The Peace of Westphalia, signed in 1648 after years of complex negotiations, ended the Thirty Years' War and established principles that remain the foundation of the international order today. Two treaties — one signed at Osnabruck, the other at Munster — together created what historians call the "Westphalian system." The most revolutionary principle was state sovereignty: each ruler had the right to determine the religion of his own territory (cuius regio, eius religio — "whose realm, his religion"), and no external power — not the Pope, not the Holy Roman Emperor — could intervene in another state's internal affairs. Calvinism was recognized alongside Lutheranism and Catholicism as a legitimate confession, and individuals of minority faiths were guaranteed the right to practice privately.

The implications were staggering. Westphalia effectively ended the medieval idea that Christendom was a single community under the spiritual authority of the Pope and the temporal authority of the Emperor. Instead, Europe was now a system of sovereign states, each with its own domestic religious arrangements, interacting through diplomacy, treaties, and balance of power rather than through shared religious authority. The Pope denounced the treaties as "null, void, invalid, iniquitous, unjust, damnable, reprobate, inane, empty of meaning and effect for all time" — a measure of how thoroughly the old order had been overturned. Westphalia did not end religious conflict overnight, but it marked the decisive turning point toward secularism in European politics. The state, not the church, was now the primary unit of political organization. The age of religious wars was over; the age of the nation-state had begun.

1665 – 1666

The Sabbatean Movement: A Messianic Earthquake

In 1665, a rabbi from Smyrna (modern Izmir) named Shabbetai Zevi declared himself the Messiah, and the Jewish world erupted in excitement. Shabbetai Zevi was a charismatic but troubled figure — today he might be diagnosed with bipolar disorder — who experienced periods of intense mystical exaltation alternating with deep depression. His proclamation, endorsed by the influential kabbalist Nathan of Gaza, who served as his "prophet," touched a nerve deep in the Jewish psyche. After centuries of exile, persecution, expulsion, and massacre, the longing for messianic redemption was overwhelming. News of the Messiah's arrival spread like wildfire through Jewish communities from Amsterdam to Aleppo, from Hamburg to Sana'a. Jews sold their businesses, packed their belongings, and prepared for imminent redemption and return to the Holy Land.

Then came the devastating denouement. In 1666, Shabbetai Zevi traveled to Istanbul, apparently intending to demand the Sultan's crown. Instead, he was arrested and brought before Sultan Mehmed IV. Given the choice between death and conversion to Islam, Shabbetai Zevi chose... Islam. He put on a turban and took the name Aziz Mehmed Effendi. The shock and humiliation that swept through the Jewish world was immense. Most followers abandoned the movement in despair and shame. But a surprising number persisted, developing elaborate theological justifications for the apostasy — arguing that the Messiah had to descend into the realm of impurity to redeem the "holy sparks" trapped there. A secret sect of Sabbatean believers (the Donmeh) survived in the Ottoman Empire for centuries, outwardly Muslim but secretly practicing a hybrid faith. The Sabbatean crisis left deep scars on Jewish consciousness: a heightened skepticism toward messianic claims, a wariness of charismatic leaders, and a trauma that influenced Jewish attitudes toward later movements — including, some argue, both Hasidism and Zionism.

A Turning Point Still With Us

The Westphalian principle of state sovereignty remains the bedrock of international law today. The United Nations system, the concept of non-interference in domestic affairs, the idea that borders are inviolable — all trace back to 1648. But the Westphalian system also created tensions that persist: What happens when state sovereignty conflicts with universal human rights? When does "internal affairs" become a shield for atrocities? These questions — from Kosovo to Syria to Myanmar — are Westphalian dilemmas, still unresolved nearly four centuries later.
