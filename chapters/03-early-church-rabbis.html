Chapter 3

Rise of Christianity & Rabbinic Judaism

100 - 600 CE

In the five centuries after the death of Jesus, two sibling religions emerged from the same root and grew apart forever. Christianity spread from a persecuted Jewish sect into the official religion of the Roman Empire, while Judaism reinvented itself without a Temple, building a new spiritual architecture around the Torah (the first five books of the Hebrew Bible, considered the most sacred text in Judaism), the synagogue (a communal house of prayer and study), and the rabbi (a teacher and interpreter of Jewish law). This chapter traces both transformations and the forces that drove them.

The Apostolic Age

What is an Apostle?

An apostle (from the Greek apostolos, meaning "one who is sent") is a person chosen and sent out by Jesus to spread his message. The term originally referred to the twelve closest disciples Jesus selected during his ministry. After the resurrection, the term expanded to include Paul of Tarsus and others who were commissioned to preach. The "Apostolic Age" refers to the period when these original apostles were alive and actively leading the early Christian movement (roughly 30-100 CE).

The decades immediately following the crucifixion of Jesus (his execution by the Roman method of nailing a person to a wooden cross, a punishment reserved for the most serious offenders) were among the most consequential in the history of Western civilization. A small band of Jewish disciples in Jerusalem transformed their grief into a movement that would eventually reshape the Roman world. The Apostolic Age -- roughly 30 to 100 CE -- saw the birth of the Christian Church, its separation from Judaism, and the writing of the texts that would become the New Testament.

Section at a Glance

  • At Pentecost (~30 CE), the Holy Spirit descends on the disciples and 3,000 are baptized -- Christians regard this as the birthday of the Church
  • Stephen becomes the first Christian martyr (a person who is killed for refusing to renounce their faith), and his stoning triggers a persecution that paradoxically scatters believers and spreads the movement beyond Jerusalem
  • Paul of Tarsus converts from persecutor to apostle and opens Christianity to non-Jews, arguing that Gentile (non-Jewish) converts need not follow the Torah's ritual laws
  • The Gospels and Paul's letters are written between ~50-100 CE, forming the texts that will become the New Testament (the second part of the Christian Bible, containing writings about Jesus and the early Church, distinct from the Old Testament/Hebrew Bible) and defining Christian theology for all time
~30 CE

Pentecost: Birth of the Christian Church

According to the Book of Acts (a New Testament text that narrates the history of the early Christian movement after Jesus's death, chapter 2), fifty days after the resurrection of Jesus, his disciples (his closest followers) were gathered together in Jerusalem for the Jewish festival of Shavuot (a harvest celebration, called "Pentecost" in Greek because it falls fifty days after Passover). Suddenly, the Holy Spirit -- understood by Christians as the presence and power of God active in the world -- descended upon them like "tongues of fire," and they began speaking in languages they had never learned. Jews from across the Roman world who had come to Jerusalem for the festival heard the disciples preaching in their own native tongues -- Parthians, Medes, Elamites, residents of Mesopotamia, Cappadocia, Pontus, Asia, Egypt, Libya, and Rome.

Peter, the chief disciple, stood and delivered a powerful sermon declaring that Jesus of Nazareth, whom the authorities had crucified, had been raised from the dead by God and was the promised Messiah (the long-awaited savior-king foretold in Jewish scripture who would deliver God's people). The crowd was "cut to the heart," and according to Acts, about 3,000 people were baptized (ritually immersed in water as a sign of spiritual cleansing and entry into the community of believers) that day. Christians regard Pentecost as the birthday of the Church -- the moment the movement ceased to be a private group of mourning disciples and became a public, Spirit-empowered community with a mission to the world.

The theological significance of Pentecost cannot be overstated. For Christians, the event fulfilled a prophecy from the Hebrew prophet Joel (one of the biblical prophets, whose writings appear in the Hebrew Bible) that God would "pour out his Spirit on all flesh." It also reversed the Tower of Babel narrative from Genesis (the first book of the Bible), where God confused human languages -- now, through the Spirit, linguistic barriers were overcome. The event established a pattern for Christian identity: the Church would be a universal community transcending ethnic and linguistic boundaries, not confined to one nation or one land.

~30-50 CE

The Early Jerusalem Church

The first Christians did not think of themselves as members of a new religion. They were Jews who believed the Messiah had come. They continued to worship at the Temple, observe the Sabbath (the weekly day of rest on Saturday, commanded in Jewish law), follow dietary laws, and practice circumcision (the ritual removal of the foreskin of male infants, a sign of the covenant between God and the Jewish people since Abraham). What distinguished them was their conviction that Jesus of Nazareth had risen from the dead and would soon return to establish God's kingdom on earth.

The Jerusalem community was led by three central figures known as the "pillars": James (the brother of Jesus, not the apostle James), Peter (Simon Peter, the chief disciple), and John (the "beloved disciple"). James became the primary leader of the Jerusalem church and was known for his rigorous Jewish piety -- later sources describe him as "James the Just," a man who spent so much time on his knees in prayer that they became calloused like a camel's.

The early believers practiced a radical form of communal living described in Acts 2:44-45: "All the believers were together and had everything in common. They sold property and possessions to give to anyone who had need." They shared meals together -- the "breaking of bread" that would become the Eucharist (the central Christian ritual of sharing bread and wine in remembrance of Jesus's last meal with his disciples) -- and met in each other's homes. This was not yet an institutional church with buildings, clergy, or creeds (formal statements of belief). It was a Jewish messianic movement centered on the shared conviction that the crucified Jesus was alive and that the end of the age was imminent.

~34 CE

Stephen's Martyrdom: The First Christian Martyr

Stephen was one of seven men chosen by the apostles to manage the daily distribution of food to the community's widows -- a role that also involved preaching. According to the Book of Acts (a New Testament text narrating the early Church's history), Stephen was a Hellenistic Jew (a Jew from the Greek-speaking world outside Palestine, culturally influenced by Greek customs) who argued boldly in the synagogues that Jesus was the Messiah and that the Temple was not the final dwelling place of God. This was explosive language. For most Jews, the Temple in Jerusalem was the holiest place on earth, the location where God's presence (called the Shekinah, from the Hebrew word for "dwelling") literally resided.

Stephen was arrested and brought before the Sanhedrin (the Jewish high court). He delivered a long speech recounting the entire history of Israel, arguing that the Jewish people had always resisted God's prophets and that the killing of Jesus was the latest instance of this pattern. When he declared that he could see "the heavens opened and the Son of Man standing at the right hand of God," the crowd was enraged. They dragged him outside the city and stoned him to death.

Stephen's death was a turning point. It triggered the first major persecution of the Jesus movement in Jerusalem. Many believers scattered throughout Judea and Samaria, and as they went, they spread their message to new audiences. Paradoxically, persecution became the engine of expansion. Among those who approved of Stephen's execution and actively persecuted the early Christians was a young Pharisee named Saul of Tarsus -- who would soon undergo the most famous conversion in history.

~36 CE

The Conversion of Saul (Paul) on the Damascus Road

Saul of Tarsus was a Pharisee -- a member of the most rigorous Jewish religious party, devoted to exact observance of the Torah. He was also a Roman citizen, born in Tarsus (modern Turkey), educated in Jerusalem under the great rabbi Gamaliel (one of the most respected Jewish teachers of the first century), and utterly convinced that the followers of Jesus were dangerous heretics who needed to be stopped. He obtained letters of authorization from the high priest to travel to Damascus and arrest any Christians he found there.

On the road to Damascus, according to Acts 9, a blinding light from heaven suddenly surrounded him. He fell to the ground and heard a voice saying, "Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?" When Saul asked, "Who are you, Lord?" the voice replied, "I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting." Saul was struck blind for three days and was led into Damascus, where a Christian named Ananias -- reluctant and afraid of this notorious persecutor -- was sent by God to restore his sight and baptize him. Saul emerged as Paul, transformed from the Church's fiercest enemy into its greatest apostle.

Paul's conversion is arguably the most consequential individual event in the history of Christianity. Without Paul, the Jesus movement might have remained a Jewish sect in Palestine. Paul brought three things no other early leader possessed: deep training in Jewish scripture and theology, a cosmopolitan Greek education that allowed him to communicate with the wider Hellenistic world, and Roman citizenship that gave him legal protections and freedom of movement across the Empire. He would use all three to reshape the movement entirely.

Paul never met Jesus during his lifetime. His conversion happened on the road to Damascus after the crucifixion -- making him the only major apostle whose authority rested entirely on a visionary experience rather than personal acquaintance. Yet this outsider wrote more of the New Testament than any other author and shaped Christian theology more profoundly than any of the original twelve disciples.

Caravaggio, "Conversion on the Way to Damascus" (c. 1600-1601). Saul is thrown from his horse by a heavenly light -- the moment that transformed Christianity's fiercest persecutor into its greatest missionary.

~46-58 CE

Paul's Missionary Journeys

Over approximately twelve years, Paul undertook three major missionary journeys across the Roman world, covering thousands of miles by foot and by sea. Starting from Antioch in Syria (which had become the second major center of the Christian movement after Jerusalem), Paul traveled through Cyprus, across modern-day Turkey (Galatia, Phrygia, Mysia), into Greece (Philippi, Thessalonica, Athens, Corinth), and to the coastal cities of Asia Minor (Ephesus). His final journey brought him to Rome itself, where he arrived as a prisoner.

Paul's strategy was deliberate. He traveled along Roman roads and shipping routes, targeting major urban centers where Jewish diaspora communities had established synagogues. He would begin by preaching in the synagogue, arguing from the Hebrew scriptures that Jesus was the promised Messiah. When some Jews accepted and others rejected his message, he would turn to the Gentiles (non-Jews) in the city. In this way, Paul established small house churches in city after city -- communities of believers who would continue meeting, worshipping, and spreading the faith after he moved on.

Paul's mission to the Gentiles was revolutionary. He argued passionately that non-Jews could become full members of God's people through faith in Christ without first becoming Jews. They did not need to be circumcised, follow Jewish dietary laws, or observe the Sabbath. This was not merely a practical accommodation -- it was a radical theological claim. Paul argued that the death and resurrection of Jesus had inaugurated a new covenant that transcended the old categories of Jew and Gentile, slave and free, male and female (Galatians 3:28). This universal vision made Christianity accessible to the entire Roman world.

~49-62 CE

Paul's Epistles: The Theology of Christianity

Paul's letters to the churches he founded are the earliest written documents in the New Testament -- older than any of the Gospels. His epistles (formal letters sent to early Christian communities, which were later collected as scripture) to the Romans, Corinthians (two letters), Galatians, Philippians, Thessalonians (two letters), and Philemon form the theological backbone of Christianity. Written to address specific problems and questions in his scattered congregations, these letters nevertheless articulate a comprehensive worldview that has shaped Western thought for two millennia.

The central theme of Paul's theology is salvation by grace through faith. In his letter to the Romans -- often considered the most important theological document in Christian history -- Paul argues that all humans, both Jews and Gentiles, are trapped in sin and unable to save themselves through their own moral efforts or by following the Law. Salvation comes only as a free gift (grace) from God, received through trust (faith) in Jesus Christ, whose death on the cross atoned for human sin. This doctrine of justification by faith would become the cornerstone of Protestant Christianity (the branch of Christianity that broke away from the Catholic Church in the 1500s) when Martin Luther (a German monk and reformer) rediscovered it.

Paul also developed the doctrines of original sin (all humanity shares in Adam's fallen nature), the mystical body of Christ (the Church as a single organism with many members), the resurrection of the body (not merely the immortality of the soul), and the second coming of Christ. His ethical teachings emphasized love as the highest virtue: "If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong" (1 Corinthians 13). Paul's vision of Christianity was simultaneously deeply Jewish in its roots and radically universal in its reach -- a paradox that would define the faith forever.

~49 CE

The Council of Jerusalem: The Decisive Break

The Council of Jerusalem, described in the Book of Acts (chapter 15), was the first major council of the Christian Church and one of the most consequential meetings in religious history. The question before the gathering was deceptively simple: Must Gentile converts to the Jesus movement be circumcised and follow the Law of Moses? In other words, must a person become a Jew before they can become a Christian?

The debate was fierce. On one side stood a faction of Jewish believers (sometimes called "Judaizers") who insisted that the covenant God made with Abraham required circumcision -- it was written in the Torah, it was non-negotiable, and no one could belong to God's people without it. On the other side stood Paul and Barnabas (Paul's traveling companion and fellow missionary), who reported the dramatic conversions of Gentiles in Antioch, Cyprus, and Asia Minor -- people who had received the Holy Spirit without any connection to Jewish law.

Peter spoke in support of Paul's position, recalling his own experience with the Roman centurion (a military officer commanding roughly 80-100 soldiers) Cornelius, the first Gentile convert. James, the leader of the Jerusalem church and a deeply observant Jew, delivered the decisive judgment: Gentile converts would NOT be required to follow the full Mosaic Law (the body of commandments and regulations attributed to Moses in the Torah). They were asked only to abstain from food sacrificed to idols, from blood, from the meat of strangled animals, and from sexual immorality. This decision was formalized in a letter sent to the churches.

The Council of Jerusalem is the moment when Christianity began its transformation from a Jewish messianic sect into a distinct, universal religion. By removing the requirement of circumcision and Torah observance for Gentile converts, the council opened the door to mass conversion across the Roman world. It also made the eventual separation between Christianity and Judaism inevitable. The two faiths, which had been siblings, now began walking divergent paths -- a split that would have profound and often tragic consequences for the next two thousand years.

Why This Matters

The Council of Jerusalem is often overlooked, but it may be the single most important decision in Christian history. Had the council ruled the other way -- requiring circumcision and full Torah observance -- Christianity almost certainly would have remained a small Jewish sect. The requirement of adult male circumcision was a massive barrier to conversion in the Roman world. By removing it, the council made Christianity accessible to everyone, setting the stage for its explosive growth.

~65-100 CE

The Writing of the Gospels

For the first few decades after Jesus, the stories about his life, teachings, death, and resurrection were transmitted orally. Eyewitnesses told and retold the narratives, and certain collections of sayings and miracle stories likely circulated in written form. But as the eyewitness generation began to die -- and as the movement spread far from Palestine to communities that had never met Jesus -- the need for authoritative written accounts became urgent.

The Gospel of Mark, written around 65-70 CE (possibly in Rome), is almost certainly the earliest. Mark's account is terse, fast-paced, and focused on Jesus as a man of action -- healing, casting out demons, and heading inexorably toward the cross. Mark wrote during or just before the destruction of the Temple, and his Gospel reflects an atmosphere of crisis and urgency. The Gospel of Matthew, written around 80-85 CE, was aimed primarily at a Jewish audience and presents Jesus as the new Moses, the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy. Matthew includes the Sermon on the Mount, the most famous collection of Jesus's ethical teachings.

The Gospel of Luke, also written around 80-85 CE, was addressed to a Gentile audience and emphasizes Jesus's compassion for the poor, for women, and for outsiders. Luke is the only Gospel writer who also wrote a sequel -- the Book of Acts, which narrates the spread of Christianity from Jerusalem to Rome. The Gospel of John, the last to be written (around 90-100 CE), is dramatically different from the other three. John presents Jesus in cosmic, philosophical terms: "In the beginning was the Word (Logos -- a Greek philosophical term meaning reason, logic, or the organizing principle of the universe), and the Word was with God, and the Word was God." John's Gospel is the most explicitly theological, emphasizing Jesus's divinity and his unity with the Father.

Each Gospel was written for a specific community and shaped by that community's concerns. Together, they provide four distinct portraits of Jesus that overlap in many ways but also diverge significantly. The existence of four Gospels rather than one reflects an early Christian instinct to preserve multiple perspectives rather than impose a single authorized version -- a choice that would generate both richness and controversy in the centuries ahead.

~64-67 CE

The Martyrdom of Peter and Paul in Rome

In July of 64 CE, a catastrophic fire swept through Rome, destroying ten of the city's fourteen districts over six days. The emperor Nero, who was widely rumored (perhaps unfairly) to have started the fire himself to make room for his new palace, needed scapegoats. He found them in the small Christian community of Rome. The Roman historian Tacitus describes how Nero had Christians arrested, covered in animal skins and torn apart by dogs, crucified, or set ablaze as human torches to illuminate his gardens at night.

According to strong early church tradition, both Peter and Paul were martyred in Rome during this persecution. Peter, the fisherman from Galilee whom Jesus had called "the rock on which I will build my church," was reportedly crucified upside down -- he allegedly requested this position because he felt unworthy to die in the same manner as his Lord. Paul, as a Roman citizen, was entitled to a more dignified death and was beheaded by sword, traditionally at a site outside Rome called Tre Fontane.

The deaths of Peter and Paul in Rome had enormous consequences. Peter's presence and martyrdom in Rome became the foundation of the papacy (the institution of the Pope, the supreme leader of the Catholic Church) -- the Bishop of Rome would claim authority over all Christendom based on his status as Peter's successor. Paul's execution cemented Rome as a central pillar of the faith. Together, the two apostles transformed Rome from the capital of the empire that persecuted Christians into the spiritual capital of Christianity itself. Their tombs -- St. Peter's Basilica and the Basilica of St. Paul Outside the Walls -- remain among the most important pilgrimage sites in the Christian world.

Temple Destruction & Diaspora

The destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE and the failed revolts that followed constitute the most traumatic events in Jewish history. They ended the sacrificial system that had been the center of Jewish worship for a millennium, scattered the Jewish people across the Roman world, and forced a radical reinvention of Judaism itself. These events also shaped the trajectory of Christianity and planted seeds that would grow into centuries of conflict between the two faiths.

Section at a Glance

  • The Great Jewish Revolt (66-73 CE) erupts against Roman oppression, but internal divisions among Zealots, Sicarii, and other factions fatally weaken the resistance
  • Titus destroys the Second Temple in 70 CE -- ending a thousand years of sacrificial worship and leaving only the Western Wall (a retaining wall) standing
  • The Bar Kokhba Revolt (132-135 CE) fails catastrophically; Emperor Hadrian renames Jerusalem "Aelia Capitolina" and bans Jews from the city entirely
  • Rabbi Yohanan ben Zakkai escapes besieged Jerusalem and establishes the academy at Yavneh, beginning the transformation of Judaism from a Temple religion into Rabbinic Judaism
66-73 CE

The Great Jewish Revolt

Tensions between the Jewish population of Judea and their Roman overlords had been building for decades. Roman governors were often incompetent or deliberately provocative. Taxation was crushing. Roman soldiers showed contempt for Jewish religious sensitivities -- there were incidents of soldiers exposing themselves in the Temple precincts and displaying pagan symbols in Jerusalem. Extremist groups like the Sicarii (literally "dagger-men," assassins who concealed small daggers and stabbed Roman collaborators in crowds) targeted Roman sympathizers, and the Zealots (a militant Jewish faction who believed armed revolt against Rome was a religious obligation) preached armed resistance as a religious duty.

In 66 CE, the Roman procurator Gessius Florus seized a large sum from the Temple treasury. When Jews protested, Roman soldiers massacred civilians. The revolt erupted. Jewish rebels seized control of Jerusalem and defeated a Roman legion sent from Syria -- an astonishing military victory that shocked the Empire. But the Jewish resistance was fatally weakened by internal divisions. Zealots, Sicarii, and various factional leaders fought each other almost as bitterly as they fought Rome. The historian Josephus (a first-century Jewish writer whose works are the most important primary source for this period), himself a Jewish commander who defected to the Romans, describes horrifying scenes of Jewish groups killing each other within the walls of Jerusalem even as the Roman siege tightened.

Emperor Nero dispatched his best general, Vespasian, with a massive army. Vespasian methodically subdued the Galilee and surrounding regions before being recalled to Rome to become emperor. His son Titus took command and turned his attention to Jerusalem. The great city, swollen with refugees and Passover pilgrims (Passover being the major Jewish festival commemorating the Israelites' liberation from slavery in Egypt), was about to face annihilation.

70 CE

The Destruction of the Second Temple

In the spring of 70 CE, Titus encircled Jerusalem with four Roman legions. The siege lasted approximately five months and was one of the most horrific events of the ancient world. Josephus, who witnessed the siege from the Roman camp, describes scenes of starvation so severe that residents ate leather, hay, and -- in at least one account -- their own children. The Roman siege engines relentlessly battered the walls while catapults hurled massive stones into the city. Crucifixions of captured Jews were so numerous that, according to Josephus, the Romans "ran out of wood for crosses and space to put them."

In August of 70 CE, on the ninth day of the Hebrew month of Av -- a date known as Tisha B'Av (literally "the Ninth of Av"), which would become the most mournful day in the Jewish calendar -- the Romans breached the inner walls and set fire to the Temple. Whether Titus ordered the destruction or whether it happened in the chaos of battle is debated by historians, but the result was total. The magnificent structure that Herod the Great (the Roman-appointed king of Judea, who ruled from 37-4 BCE and was famous for his massive building projects) had spent decades expanding and adorning -- one of the wonders of the ancient world, with walls of white marble and gold -- was reduced to rubble and ash. The sacred vessels, including the great golden menorah (a seven-branched candelabrum that was one of the holiest objects in Jewish worship), were seized as war trophies. Only the Western Wall (known in Hebrew as the Kotel), a retaining wall of the Temple Mount platform, survived -- and remains the holiest site where Jews can pray today.

The destruction of the Temple transformed Judaism more profoundly than any other event in its history. For over a thousand years, the Temple had been the center of Jewish worship -- the place where God's presence dwelled, where priests offered daily sacrifices, where the nation gathered three times a year for pilgrimage festivals. With the Temple gone, the entire sacrificial system -- the sin offerings, the burnt offerings, the incense, the Day of Atonement ritual (the holiest day of the Jewish year, when the high priest entered the innermost sanctuary to atone for the sins of the entire people) -- ceased permanently. Judaism would have to find a way to survive without the institution that had defined it. The date of the destruction, Tisha B'Av, became the most mournful day in the Jewish calendar, a day of fasting and lamentation observed to this day.

Francesco Hayez, "Destruction of the Temple of Jerusalem" (1867). The painting captures the chaos and despair as Roman soldiers overwhelm the sacred precinct.

~81 CE

The Arch of Titus

After the fall of Jerusalem, Titus returned to Rome in triumph. The victory parade through the streets of Rome displayed the spoils of war, including the sacred objects looted from the Temple -- the golden menorah (seven-branched candelabrum), the silver trumpets, and the Table of the Showbread. Jewish captives were paraded in chains. The triumphal arch erected in the Roman Forum after Titus's death in 81 CE preserves this scene in stone relief, showing Roman soldiers carrying the menorah on their shoulders.

The Arch of Titus stands in Rome to this day, a monument visible to every tourist who walks through the Forum. For Jews, it has always been a painful symbol. According to tradition, Jews in Rome refused to walk under the arch for centuries. When the State of Israel was established in 1948, the emblem of the new state featured the menorah from the Arch of Titus -- a deliberate statement that the Jewish people had outlasted the empire that destroyed their Temple. The arch intended to celebrate Rome's triumph had become, ironically, a testament to Jewish survival.

The Roman Empire, the Babylonian Empire, the Assyrian Empire -- every great power that conquered or exiled the Jewish people eventually fell. Yet Judaism endured. The rabbis' decision to build a faith around a portable text rather than a fixed temple created what scholars call a "portable homeland." This same strategy of survival-through-learning would later influence both Christian monasticism and Islamic scholarly traditions, as all three Abrahamic faiths built civilizations around sacred books.

The inner relief of the Arch of Titus in Rome, showing Roman soldiers carrying the golden menorah and other sacred objects looted from the Second Temple in Jerusalem.

73 CE

Masada: The Last Stand

Even after the fall of Jerusalem, pockets of Jewish resistance held out. The most famous was Masada, a massive natural fortress perched atop a flat-topped mountain in the Judean Desert near the Dead Sea. Herod the Great had originally built palaces and storehouses on the summit, which was accessible only by a narrow, winding "Snake Path." After the fall of Jerusalem, a group of approximately 960 Jewish rebels -- men, women, and children, mostly Sicarii (a radical faction of Zealots) -- occupied the fortress and held it against Rome.

The Roman Tenth Legion, commanded by Flavius Silva, laid siege to Masada with approximately 8,000 soldiers. Unable to assault the fortress directly due to its cliffs, the Romans employed thousands of Jewish prisoners of war to build an enormous siege ramp on the western side of the mountain -- a feat of engineering that took months and is still visible today. When the ramp was completed and the Romans prepared to breach the walls, the defenders faced a choice: surrender and become slaves, or die free.

According to Josephus (whose account is the only ancient source and is debated by historians), the rebel leader Eleazar ben Ya'ir delivered two impassioned speeches urging mass suicide rather than submission. The defenders agreed. Each man killed his own family, then ten men chosen by lot killed the rest, then one killed the remaining nine, and the last man fell on his own sword. When the Romans breached the walls the next morning, they found only silence and bodies. Two women and five children who had hidden in a cistern survived to tell the tale.

Whether the mass suicide account is historically accurate remains contested. Archaeological evidence at the site is ambiguous -- skeletons have been found, but not in the numbers or configurations Josephus describes. Regardless of the historical details, Masada became a powerful symbol of Jewish resistance. In modern Israel, the phrase "Masada shall not fall again" became a national motto, and Israeli soldiers once took their oath of service atop the mountain. The site, excavated extensively in the 1960s by archaeologist Yigael Yadin, is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of Israel's most visited landmarks.

Aerial view of Masada, the isolated mountain fortress in the Judean Desert. The Roman siege ramp is visible on the left side. The Dead Sea gleams in the background.

~70 CE onward

Yavneh: Judaism Reinvents Itself

With the Temple in ruins, Judaism faced an existential crisis. The entire sacrificial system -- the daily offerings, the sin offerings, the priestly rituals -- was gone. The priestly class (the Sadducees, an aristocratic Jewish faction whose authority was tied directly to Temple rituals) lost their reason for existence. The apocalyptic groups that had expected God's imminent intervention were discredited. Only the Pharisees (a Jewish movement that emphasized study of religious law, prayer, and ethical living, not just Temple ceremonies), had a framework that could survive the catastrophe.

According to rabbinic tradition, during the siege of Jerusalem, Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai escaped the city by having his students carry him out in a coffin (the Zealots were killing anyone who tried to leave). He went to the Roman camp and requested permission to establish an academy at Yavneh (also called Jamnia), a small town on the coastal plain. Vespasian, who did not see a school of Torah study as a military threat, granted the request. This small academy became the seed from which Rabbinic Judaism grew.

At Yavneh, Yochanan ben Zakkai and his successors made a series of revolutionary decisions. They declared that prayer could replace sacrifice -- the three daily prayer services corresponded to the three daily Temple offerings. The synagogue replaced the Temple as the center of worship. The rabbi replaced the priest as the primary religious leader. Study of Torah replaced the offering of animals as the highest form of devotion. A famous passage in the Talmud captures this transformation: when a student wept at the sight of the ruined Temple, Yochanan ben Zakkai comforted him by saying, "We have another means of atonement -- acts of lovingkindness."

This was not merely a pragmatic adjustment -- it was a profound theological reimagination. The Pharisaic idea that every Jewish home could be a miniature Temple, every table an altar, and every meal a sacred act became the foundation of Rabbinic Judaism. This portable, text-centered, community-based faith proved remarkably resilient, carrying the Jewish people through nearly two thousand years of exile, persecution, and dispersal across the globe.

132-136 CE

The Bar Kokhba Revolt: The Final Catastrophe

Sixty years after the destruction of the Temple, the Jewish people made one last, desperate bid for independence. The catalyst was Emperor Hadrian's decision to build a Roman city called Aelia Capitolina on the ruins of Jerusalem, complete with a temple to Jupiter on the Temple Mount. He also reportedly banned circumcision, a direct assault on the most fundamental marker of Jewish identity. These provocations ignited a massive revolt led by a charismatic military commander named Simon bar Koseba, known to history as Bar Kokhba ("Son of the Star").

The great Rabbi Akiva, one of the most revered scholars in Jewish history, declared Bar Kokhba to be the Messiah -- a stunning endorsement that galvanized Jewish support. Initially, the revolt was remarkably successful. Bar Kokhba's forces liberated Jerusalem and much of Judea, established an independent state, minted coins with the inscription "Year 1 of the Redemption of Israel," and may have begun preparations to rebuild the Temple. For a brief, heady period, it seemed as though the dream of national restoration had been realized.

The Roman response was devastating. Hadrian dispatched his best general, Julius Severus (recalled from Britain), with a massive army. The Romans adopted a strategy of systematic annihilation, destroying village after village, sealing rebels in caves, and starving them out. The ancient sources (likely exaggerated but indicative of scale) claim that 580,000 Jews were killed in battle, 50 fortified towns destroyed, and 985 villages razed. Bar Kokhba was killed at his last stronghold, Betar, in 135 CE. Rabbi Akiva was captured and executed by the Romans, who reportedly raked his skin with iron combs.

The aftermath was designed to erase Jewish identity from the land. Hadrian renamed Jerusalem "Aelia Capitolina" and banned Jews from entering the city (except on Tisha B'Av, when they were permitted to come and weep at the ruins). He renamed the province from Judea to "Syria Palaestina" -- a name derived from "Philistines," the ancient enemies of Israel -- a deliberate act of historical humiliation. This renaming is the origin of the name "Palestine." The Bar Kokhba revolt ended Jewish political sovereignty in the land of Israel for nearly 1,800 years, until the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948.

~200 CE

The Mishnah: Codifying the Oral Torah

For centuries, alongside the written Torah (the Five Books of Moses), there existed an "Oral Torah" -- a vast body of legal interpretations, traditions, and teachings transmitted verbally from teacher to student. The rabbis believed that God had given Moses both a written and an oral Torah on Mount Sinai, and that the oral tradition was essential for understanding and applying the written text. But the catastrophes of 70 and 135 CE, the scattering of scholars, and the passage of time threatened to destroy this oral tradition.

Around 200 CE, Rabbi Judah ha-Nasi ("Judah the Prince"), the leading Jewish authority in Roman Palestine, took the unprecedented step of compiling the Oral Torah into a written text: the Mishnah. Organized into six "orders" (sedarim) covering agriculture, festivals, family law, civil and criminal law, Temple service, and ritual purity, the Mishnah is not a legal code in the modern sense. It is a record of debate -- presenting the differing opinions of rabbis on hundreds of topics, often without resolving which opinion is correct. This dialectical structure preserved the tradition of argument and interpretation that would become the hallmark of Jewish intellectual life.

The compilation of the Mishnah was both a practical necessity and a theological statement. By writing down the Oral Torah, Rabbi Judah preserved it from oblivion. But he also created the text that would become the foundation of the Talmud -- the central text of Rabbinic Judaism. The Mishnah became the "constitution" around which all subsequent Jewish legal discussion would orbit, and its compilation marks the transition from the age of the Tannaim (the early rabbis, roughly 10-220 CE, whose legal opinions are recorded in the Mishnah) to the age of the Amoraim (the later rabbis, roughly 220-500 CE, who would debate and comment on the Mishnah, producing the Gemara).

Constantine & State Christianity

For its first three centuries, Christianity was an illegal, intermittently persecuted religion in the Roman Empire. Then, in the span of a single lifetime, it went from the catacombs to the imperial palace. The conversion of Emperor Constantine in 312 CE and the subsequent Christianization of the Roman state represent one of the most dramatic reversals in world history. Within seventy years of Constantine, Christianity became not merely tolerated but mandatory -- the sole legal religion of the largest empire on earth.

Section at a Glance

  • Christians endure nearly 300 years of sporadic persecution, from Nero's attacks in 64 CE to Diocletian's "Great Persecution" (303-311 CE), yet the movement grows to 10-15% of the empire
  • Constantine converts after a vision before the Battle of the Milvian Bridge (312 CE) and issues the Edict of Milan (313 CE), legalizing Christianity throughout the empire
  • The Council of Nicaea (325 CE) -- the first ecumenical council -- defines the doctrine of the Trinity and produces the Nicene Creed, settling (for a time) whether Christ is fully divine
  • Emperor Theodosius makes Christianity the sole legal religion of the Roman Empire in 380 CE, and pagan temples are closed -- completing the reversal from persecuted sect to state religion
64-311 CE

The Age of Persecution

Christians faced sporadic but sometimes ferocious persecution during the first three centuries. Nero's persecution after the fire of Rome in 64 CE was the first, but far from the last. The Roman state viewed Christians with suspicion for multiple reasons: they refused to worship the emperor or the traditional gods (making them appear disloyal), they held secretive meetings (breeding rumors of cannibalism and incest, based on misunderstandings of the Eucharist and the "kiss of peace"), and they were growing in numbers.

Under Emperor Decius (who ruled briefly in 250 CE), the first empire-wide persecution required all citizens to perform a public sacrifice to the Roman gods and obtain a certificate proving they had done so. Christians who refused were imprisoned, tortured, or killed. Under Emperor Diocletian, the "Great Persecution" (303-311 CE) was the most severe and systematic: churches were demolished, scriptures were burned, clergy were arrested, and all Christians were ordered to sacrifice or die. Prominent martyrs of these centuries include Polycarp (bishop of Smyrna, burned alive around 155 CE), Perpetua and Felicity (a noblewoman and her slave, thrown to wild beasts in the arena at Carthage in 203 CE), and countless others.

The catacombs of Rome -- underground tunnel networks originally used as burial sites -- became meeting places for persecuted Christians. The walls of the catacombs bear some of the earliest Christian art: images of the Good Shepherd, Jonah and the whale, the fish symbol (ichthys -- the Greek word for "fish," used as a secret code because its letters formed an acronym for "Jesus Christ, Son of God, Savior"), and prayer figures with raised hands. Paradoxically, persecution strengthened rather than destroyed the Church. The blood of the martyrs became, in the famous phrase of Tertullian (an early Christian writer from North Africa, c. 155-220 CE), "the seed of the Church." The willingness of Christians to die for their faith impressed pagan observers and attracted converts. By the time Constantine rose to power, Christians may have constituted 10-15% of the Roman Empire's population -- a minority, but a substantial and growing one.

312 CE

Constantine's Conversion

In 312 CE, the Roman Empire was wracked by civil war. Constantine, commanding forces in Gaul and Britain, marched on Rome to face his rival Maxentius. On the eve of the Battle of the Milvian Bridge, Constantine reportedly had a vision. The accounts vary: the church historian Eusebius (an early Christian bishop and scholar who wrote the first major history of Christianity, and who claims Constantine told him directly) says the emperor saw a cross of light in the sky above the sun, with the words "In this sign, conquer" (In Hoc Signo Vinces). The earlier account by Lactantius (another Christian writer of the period) says Constantine had a dream in which he was instructed to mark his soldiers' shields with the Chi-Rho symbol -- a monogram formed by superimposing the first two Greek letters of the word "Christ" (chi, X, and rho, P), creating a symbol that looked like a combination of X and P.

Whatever actually happened, Constantine ordered his troops to display the Christian symbol. At the Battle of the Milvian Bridge on October 28, 312 CE, Constantine won a decisive victory. Maxentius drowned in the Tiber River while retreating across a pontoon bridge that collapsed under the weight of his fleeing army. Constantine entered Rome as sole ruler of the Western Empire, attributing his victory to the God of the Christians.

Historians have debated Constantine's sincerity for centuries. Was his conversion genuine, or was it a political calculation by a shrewd ruler who saw the organizational potential of the Church? The truth is probably both. Constantine continued to use pagan imagery on his coins for years, was not baptized until his deathbed (337 CE), and retained the pagan title Pontifex Maximus (chief priest). But he also lavished resources on the Church, built grand basilicas (large, imposing church buildings designed for public worship), including the original St. Peter's in Rome and the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem), granted bishops judicial authority, and made Sunday a public holiday. Whatever his inner convictions, his practical support for Christianity was transformative.

313 CE

The Edict of Milan: Christianity Legalized

In February 313 CE, Constantine met with Licinius (emperor of the Eastern Roman Empire) in Milan. Together they issued what is known as the Edict of Milan, which proclaimed religious toleration throughout the Empire. The edict stated that "no one whatsoever should be denied the opportunity to give his heart to the observance of the Christian religion" and ordered the return of all confiscated Christian property.

The Edict of Milan did not make Christianity the state religion -- that would come later. What it did was end the era of persecution permanently. Christians could now worship openly, build churches, and organize publicly. It also established a principle of religious freedom (at least in theory) that was remarkably advanced for its time. The transformation was immediate and visible: churches sprang up across the Empire, Christian bishops became powerful public figures, and the wealth of the Church grew enormously through imperial patronage and the donations of a population that no longer feared persecution.

The practical effects extended deep into Roman society. Constantine exempted clergy from taxes and compulsory public service, gave bishops the authority to act as civil judges, and made it legal for the Church to receive bequests and own property. He also moved the imperial capital from Rome to a new city he built on the site of the Greek town Byzantium, which he named Constantinople (modern Istanbul). This "New Rome" was Christian from its foundation -- it had no pagan temples, only churches. The shift of power to Constantinople would eventually lead to the division between Western (Latin/Catholic) and Eastern (Greek/Orthodox) Christianity.

325 CE

What is a Creed?

A creed (from the Latin credo, "I believe") is a formal statement of religious belief. Creeds serve as summaries of what a faith community holds to be true -- a shared declaration that defines who is "in" and who is "out." The Nicene Creed, produced at this council, became the most important creed in Christian history. It is still recited in Catholic, Orthodox, and many Protestant worship services today as the definitive statement of core Christian beliefs about God, Jesus, and the Holy Spirit.

The Council of Nicaea

With Christianity now legal and imperially favored, its internal theological disputes became matters of state concern. The most explosive controversy was the Arian dispute. Arius, a priest in Alexandria (the great Egyptian port city that was one of the most important intellectual centers of the ancient world), taught that Jesus (the Son, the Logos) was a created being -- the first and greatest of God's creations, but not eternal and not equal to God the Father. "There was a time when the Son was not," Arius argued. His opponent, Athanasius (also of Alexandria), insisted that the Son was co-eternal with the Father, of the same divine substance -- fully and equally God.

The dispute tore the Church apart. Slogans and counter-slogans were chanted in the streets of Alexandria. Constantine, alarmed that his new religious ally was splitting into warring factions, summoned the first ecumenical (universal) council of the Church at Nicaea (modern Iznik, Turkey) in 325 CE. Approximately 300 bishops attended, most from the Eastern Empire. Constantine himself presided over the opening session, resplendent in purple and gold -- the first time a Roman emperor had involved himself directly in Christian theology.

After weeks of debate, the council overwhelmingly sided with Athanasius and against Arius. The result was the Nicene Creed, which declared that Jesus Christ is "God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten not made, of one substance (homoousios) with the Father." The word homoousios -- meaning "of the same substance" -- was the key term. It asserted that the Son was not merely similar to the Father (homoiousios, differing by a single letter -- the iota that, as the historian Edward Gibbon (author of "The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire") quipped, divided the Christian world), but identical in essence. Arianism was condemned as heresy (a belief officially rejected by the Church as false and dangerous).

The Council of Nicaea set several precedents that would shape Christianity for centuries. It established the principle that doctrinal disputes would be settled by ecumenical councils. It demonstrated that the emperor had a role in Church governance. And it produced the Nicene Creed, which (in a slightly expanded form adopted at Constantinople in 381) remains the standard statement of Christian faith recited in Catholic, Orthodox, and many Protestant churches to this day.

The Nicene Creed, drafted in 325 CE, is still recited word-for-word in Catholic, Orthodox, and many Protestant churches every single Sunday -- making it one of the oldest continuously spoken texts in the world. Over 1,700 years after a committee of bishops debated its wording in a Turkish lakeside town, roughly 2 billion Christians continue to affirm those same phrases.

380 CE

Theodosius I: Christianity Becomes the State Religion

The final step in Christianity's conquest of the Roman Empire came under Emperor Theodosius I. In 380 CE, Theodosius issued the Edict of Thessalonica (a decree issued from the city of Thessalonica in modern Greece), which declared Nicene Christianity (the version of Christian belief defined at the Council of Nicaea) the sole legitimate religion of the Roman Empire. All subjects were required to profess the faith defined at Nicaea. Those who did not -- including Arian Christians, pagans, and heretics of all varieties -- were branded "demented and insane" and subject to "divine punishment" and "the vengeance which our authority, guided by heavenly wisdom, shall decide to inflict."

The practical consequences were enormous. Pagan temples were closed, defunded, and in many cases physically destroyed by Christian mobs. The eternal flame in the Temple of Vesta in Rome (dedicated to the Roman goddess of the hearth, tended by priestesses called Vestal Virgins), which had burned for over a thousand years, was extinguished. The ancient Olympic Games (athletic competitions held every four years in Olympia, Greece, since 776 BCE), because they were associated with pagan religious festivals, were abolished in 393 CE.

Christmas on December 25 was likely chosen to coincide with the Roman festival of Sol Invictus (Latin for "the Unconquered Sun"), a major pagan holiday celebrating the winter solstice (the shortest day of the year, after which daylight begins to return). As Christianity absorbed the empire, it frequently repurposed pagan dates and customs -- transforming their meaning while keeping the calendar familiar.

The great Library of Alexandria's (the ancient world's most famous center of learning, located in Egypt, which had housed hundreds of thousands of scrolls) final destruction (the details are debated by historians) occurred in this period. Pagan philosophical schools were shuttered. The transformation was complete: in less than a century, Christianity had gone from a persecuted minority to the only legal religion of the most powerful empire on earth. The persecuted had become the persecutors.

This reversal raised profound moral and theological questions that Christians have grappled with ever since. Was it right for a faith founded on turning the other cheek to enforce belief through state power? Did the marriage of Church and Empire corrupt Christianity's spiritual message? These questions would echo through the Inquisition (the Church's later campaigns to root out heresy, often through imprisonment and execution), the Reformation (the 16th-century movement that split Western Christianity into Catholic and Protestant branches), and into the modern debates about the separation of church and state. What is undeniable is that Theodosius's edict created a new reality -- Christendom (the term for the territories and societies where Christianity was the dominant religion and the Church wielded political influence), a civilization in which Christianity and political power were fused -- that would define Europe for the next 1,400 years.

~405 CE

What is the Vulgate?

The Vulgate is the Latin translation of the Bible produced by the scholar Jerome in the late 4th/early 5th century. "Vulgate" comes from the Latin word for "common" -- it was meant to be the Bible in the common language of the Roman world. For over a thousand years, it was THE Bible of Western Christianity, the version used in every church, monastery, and university in Europe. Its influence on Western language, law, and culture is immense.

Jerome and the Vulgate Bible

As Christianity spread across the Latin-speaking western Empire, the need for a reliable, standardized Latin translation of the Bible became urgent. Earlier Latin translations (the "Old Latin" or Vetus Latina) were inconsistent and often inaccurate. In 382 CE, Pope Damasus I commissioned Jerome, the most brilliant biblical scholar of his generation, to produce a definitive Latin translation.

Jerome spent over twenty years on the project, working from a cell in Bethlehem. For the Old Testament, he made the revolutionary decision to translate directly from the original Hebrew rather than from the Greek Septuagint (an ancient Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, produced around the 3rd-2nd centuries BCE, which had been the traditional basis for Christian readings of the Old Testament). This decision -- "going back to the Hebrew truth" (Hebraica veritas) -- was controversial. Augustine and others worried that departing from the Septuagint would undermine the Church's authority. But Jerome persisted, producing a translation of exceptional literary quality and scholarly rigor.

The result, known as the Vulgate (from the Latin "vulgata," meaning "common" or "popular"), became the standard Bible of Western Christianity for over a thousand years. It was the Bible of the medieval Church, the Bible from which Thomas Aquinas (the foremost Catholic theologian of the Middle Ages) and the Scholastics (medieval university scholars who used logic and philosophy to explore theology) quoted, the Bible that Johannes Gutenberg (inventor of the European printing press) printed, and the Bible that the Council of Trent (a major Catholic council convened in 1545-1563 to respond to the Protestant Reformation) declared authoritative. Jerome's translation shaped the vocabulary and thought patterns of Western civilization in ways that are difficult to overstate -- legal terms, ethical concepts, and literary allusions that pervade Western culture trace their Latin forms to Jerome's pen.

354-430 CE

Augustine of Hippo: The Architect of Western Theology

Augustine is arguably the most influential Christian thinker after Paul. Born in North Africa (modern Algeria) in 354 CE to a pagan father and a devoutly Christian mother (Monica), Augustine spent his youth in restless intellectual searching. He was a brilliant student of rhetoric, a lover of literature, and -- by his own admission -- a slave to sexual desire. He lived with a concubine for fifteen years and famously prayed, "Lord, give me chastity and continence -- but not yet."

After experimenting with Manichaeism (a dualist religion) and Neoplatonism (Greek philosophy), Augustine experienced a dramatic conversion in a garden in Milan in 386 CE. Hearing a child's voice singing "Take up and read," he opened Paul's epistle to the Romans and read the passage: "Not in reveling and drunkenness, not in debauchery and licentiousness... but put on the Lord Jesus Christ." He was baptized by Ambrose (the influential Bishop of Milan, one of the most powerful churchmen of the era) in 387 CE and eventually became Bishop of Hippo in North Africa, where he spent the rest of his life writing, preaching, and debating.

Augustine's literary output was staggering. His "Confessions" (c. 397 CE) is the first autobiography in Western literature -- a profound meditation on memory, desire, time, and God's grace. His "City of God" (426 CE), written in response to the sack of Rome by the Visigoths (a Germanic tribal group) in 410 CE, argues that human history is a struggle between the "City of God" (those devoted to God) and the "City of Man" (those devoted to earthly power and pleasure). This framework shaped Western political thought for a millennium.

Theologically, Augustine developed or refined several doctrines that became foundational to Western Christianity: original sin (the doctrine that all humans inherit the guilt and sinful nature of Adam, the first human in the biblical creation story, and are therefore incapable of saving themselves), predestination (the idea that God has already chosen, before time began, who will be saved and who will not), the necessity of grace (salvation is entirely God's gift, not a human achievement), and just war theory (the argument that war can be morally justified under specific conditions, such as defending the innocent). His influence on Catholicism and, later, on the Protestant Reformation (both Luther and John Calvin, another key Protestant reformer, drew heavily on Augustine) is incalculable. More than any other figure, Augustine defined the intellectual framework of Western Christianity.

451 CE

The Council of Chalcedon: Christ's Two Natures

If Nicaea settled the question of whether Christ was divine (yes, fully divine), Chalcedon addressed the follow-up question: how do the divine and human natures relate in the one person of Christ? This may sound like an abstract philosophical puzzle, but in the fifth century it was a matter of passionate public controversy. Riots broke out over Christological formulas (technical theological statements about the nature of Christ). Bishops were exiled, deposed, and even murdered. The emperor himself had to intervene.

The Council of Chalcedon (451 CE), convened by Emperor Marcian, produced a formula that remains the standard Christological definition for Catholic, Orthodox, and most Protestant churches: Christ has two complete natures -- divine and human -- united in one person "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation." The divine nature is not diluted by the human, and the human nature is not absorbed by the divine. Christ is fully God and fully man simultaneously.

Not everyone accepted this formula. Those who emphasized Christ's single, unified divine nature (Miaphysites, often inaccurately called Monophysites) rejected Chalcedon and split from the imperial church. This schism (a formal split within a religious community) produced the "Oriental Orthodox" churches that persist to this day: the Coptic Orthodox Church of Egypt (one of the oldest Christian communities in the world, tracing its origins to the apostle Mark), the Armenian Apostolic Church, the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, and the Syriac Orthodox Church. These are among the oldest Christian communities in the world, and their separation from the Chalcedonian churches represents a division that has lasted over 1,500 years. The Chalcedonian split also had political consequences: it weakened Byzantine control over Egypt and Syria, regions where the Miaphysite churches were dominant, contributing to the ease with which Muslim armies would conquer these territories two centuries later.

476-529 CE

What is Monasticism?

Monasticism is the practice of withdrawing from ordinary society to devote oneself entirely to religious life -- prayer, meditation, study, and manual labor. Monks and nuns live in communities called monasteries or convents, typically following a set of rules governing their daily life. Monasticism exists in Christianity, Buddhism, and other traditions. In the Christian West, monasteries became crucial centers of learning, agriculture, and manuscript preservation during the centuries after Rome's fall.

Fall of Rome, Rise of Monasticism, and the Papacy

In 476 CE, the Germanic chieftain Odoacer (a barbarian military commander who had served in the Roman army) deposed the last Western Roman Emperor, Romulus Augustulus -- a teenager whose very name (a diminutive of both "Romulus" and "Augustus") seemed to mock Rome's founding and imperial glory. The Western Roman Empire, which had ruled the Mediterranean world for five centuries, was finished. But Christianity not only survived -- it thrived. The Church became the primary institution providing continuity, education, governance, and social services in the chaos of the post-Roman West.

One of the most important developments of this era was the rise of monasticism. The impulse to withdraw from the world for prayer and spiritual discipline had existed since the third century, when Egyptian hermits like Anthony the Great retreated into the desert. But it was Benedict of Nursia (c. 480-547 CE) who gave Western monasticism its enduring form. Around 529 CE, Benedict founded the monastery of Monte Cassino in central Italy and wrote his famous "Rule of Saint Benedict" -- a practical guide for monastic life organized around the motto "Ora et Labora" (Pray and Work).

Benedict's Rule prescribed a balanced life of communal prayer (eight times daily), manual labor, and sacred reading (lectio divina, a contemplative practice of slowly reading and meditating on scripture). It emphasized stability (monks committed to one community for life), obedience (to the abbot, the head of the monastery), and humility. The Rule was moderate compared to the extreme asceticism of Eastern monks -- Benedict called it "a little rule for beginners." But its very moderation was its genius. Benedictine monasteries spread across Europe and became centers of learning, agriculture, manuscript copying, hospitality, and education. They preserved classical learning through the "Dark Ages" and shaped European civilization in ways that extended far beyond religion.

Meanwhile, the Bishop of Rome was emerging as the preeminent leader of Western Christianity. The claim to papal primacy rested on several foundations: Rome was the largest and wealthiest city in the West, Peter (whom Jesus designated as the "rock") was traditionally regarded as the first Bishop of Rome, and both Peter and Paul had been martyred there. As the Western Empire collapsed and secular authority fragmented, the pope increasingly filled the vacuum. Pope Leo I ("the Great," r. 440-461) famously met Attila the Hun (the fearsome leader of the Hunnic Empire, a nomadic warrior confederation from Central Asia that terrorized Europe) outside Rome in 452 CE and persuaded him to withdraw -- an act that dramatically enhanced papal prestige. By 600 CE, the papacy had become the most powerful institution in Western Europe.

Rabbinic Judaism & the Talmud

What is the Talmud?

The Talmud is the central text of Rabbinic Judaism -- a massive compilation of legal discussions, ethical teachings, stories, and debates produced by generations of rabbis over several centuries. It consists of two layers: the Mishnah (a concise code of Jewish law compiled around 200 CE) and the Gemara (extensive commentary and discussion of the Mishnah). There are two versions: the Jerusalem Talmud (~400 CE) and the more authoritative Babylonian Talmud (~500 CE). Think of it as both a legal encyclopedia and a record of centuries of intellectual debate -- the foundation on which Jewish life, law, and thought have rested for 1,500 years.

While Christianity was conquering the Roman Empire, Judaism was undergoing its own profound transformation. Stripped of the Temple, the priesthood, and political sovereignty, the rabbis built a new Judaism centered on text, study, prayer, and community. The culmination of this effort was the Talmud -- one of the most complex, intellectually demanding, and influential texts in human history.

Section at a Glance

  • The Jerusalem Talmud is compiled around 400 CE in the Galilean academies, preserving Palestinian rabbinic traditions under increasingly hostile Christian Roman rule
  • The Babylonian Talmud (~500 CE) becomes the crowning achievement of Rabbinic Judaism -- 2,711 folio pages of law, debate, stories, and ethical reflection produced in the academies of Sura and Pumbedita
  • The Talmud replaces the Temple as the center of Jewish life, making study itself a form of worship and establishing the rabbi (not the priest) as the spiritual leader
  • The rabbis create a "portable homeland" -- a legal and spiritual framework that enables Judaism to survive and thrive in exile for the next 1,500 years without a Temple, land, or political sovereignty
~400 CE

The Jerusalem Talmud (Talmud Yerushalmi)

After the compilation of the Mishnah around 200 CE, the next several generations of rabbis (the Amoraim) devoted themselves to studying, debating, and commenting on it. These discussions were conducted in two major centers: the academies of Roman Palestine (the Galilee, after Jews were banned from Jerusalem) and the academies of Babylonia (modern Iraq), where a large Jewish community had existed since the Babylonian Exile of 586 BCE (when the Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar conquered Jerusalem, destroyed the First Temple, and deported much of the Jewish population to Mesopotamia).

The Jerusalem Talmud (Yerushalmi) was compiled around 400 CE in the Galilean academies of Tiberias, Sepphoris, and Caesarea. It represents the Palestinian rabbis' commentary (Gemara) on the Mishnah. However, the Jerusalem Talmud was produced under difficult conditions -- the Christian Roman Empire was increasingly hostile to Jews, and the Palestinian academies were declining in resources and personnel. As a result, the Jerusalem Talmud is shorter, less polished, and more difficult to study than its Babylonian counterpart. It covers only four of the six orders of the Mishnah in detail.

Despite its relative obscurity (the Babylonian Talmud would become far more authoritative), the Jerusalem Talmud preserves traditions, customs, and legal opinions that are unique to the Palestinian Jewish experience. It provides invaluable historical information about Jewish life in Roman Palestine during the third and fourth centuries and contains legal discussions and traditions not found in the Babylonian version. Scholars today are increasingly recognizing its importance as an independent witness to the diversity of early rabbinic thought.

~500 CE

The Babylonian Talmud (Talmud Bavli)

The Babylonian Talmud, compiled over approximately three centuries and reaching its final form around 500 CE (with some editorial additions continuing until 600 CE or later), is the crowning achievement of Rabbinic Judaism. Produced in the great academies of Sura and Pumbedita in Mesopotamia, it is a massive work -- 2,711 folio pages in the standard Vilna edition, covering 37 of the 63 tractates of the Mishnah. It is not a book in the conventional sense but a vast, labyrinthine record of rabbinic debate, legal analysis, storytelling, ethical reflection, and intellectual exploration.

The structure of the Talmud is layered. At the center of each page is the Mishnah text (the original law from ~200 CE). Surrounding it is the Gemara -- the record of generations of rabbis discussing, questioning, challenging, and interpreting the Mishnah. The discussions often range far from the original topic, incorporating stories (aggadah), parables, medical advice, astronomical observations, dream interpretations, and ethical maxims. A single page might move from a technical legal question about property damage to a story about a rabbi's encounter with a Roman matron to a meditation on the nature of the afterlife.

The Babylonian Talmud became the authoritative text of Judaism -- more studied, more commented upon, and more central to Jewish life than any other work except the Torah itself. When Jews say "the Talmud" without qualification, they mean the Babylonian Talmud. For over 1,500 years, studying Talmud has been the central intellectual activity of Jewish life. Yeshivot (plural of yeshiva -- schools devoted to the intensive study of the Talmud and Jewish law) became the premier educational institutions of the Jewish world, and the ability to engage in Talmudic argument became the highest mark of intellectual achievement. The Talmud's dialectical method -- its relentless questioning, its tolerance of multiple opinions, its refusal to settle for easy answers -- shaped Jewish culture in ways that extend far beyond religion.

The Babylonian Talmud contains approximately 2.5 million words -- roughly 3 times the length of the entire Bible. Its 2,711 folio pages cover everything from contract law to dream interpretation to the proper way to tie a sandal. A traditional page of Talmud has the Mishnah text at the center, surrounded by layers of commentary from different centuries -- a physical layout that mirrors the centuries of debate it records.

100-600 CE

Jewish Life in the Diaspora

By the end of this period, Jewish communities were scattered across the known world. The largest and most prosperous was in Babylonia (Mesopotamia), where Jews had lived continuously since the Exile of 586 BCE -- over 700 years by the time of the Talmud's compilation. The Babylonian Jewish community was led by the Exilarch (Resh Galuta, "Head of the Exile"), a figure who claimed descent from King David (the legendary ancient king of Israel, regarded as the greatest ruler in Jewish history) and held quasi-governmental authority over Jewish affairs under the Sasanian Persian Empire.

Significant Jewish communities also existed in Alexandria (Egypt), Rome, across North Africa, in Spain (Iberia), in the Rhine Valley (the beginnings of Ashkenazi Jewry -- the Jewish communities of central and eastern Europe, who would become the largest Jewish population in the world), and scattered throughout Asia Minor and the eastern Mediterranean. In each location, the synagogue served as the center of community life -- a place of worship, study, communal assembly, and social welfare. The rabbi, not the priest, was the community's religious authority, and Torah study, not sacrifice, was the highest religious act.

The Midrash tradition flourished during this period. Midrash (from the Hebrew root "darash," meaning "to seek" or "to investigate") refers to creative, imaginative interpretation of scripture. Rabbinic midrashim fill in gaps in the biblical narrative, resolve contradictions, draw ethical lessons, and explore the deeper meanings of the text. For example, where the Torah says only that Abraham smashed his father's idols, the Midrash supplies a vivid dramatic scene. Where the Torah is silent about Moses's childhood, the Midrash provides detailed stories. This tradition of creative interpretation kept the biblical text alive and relevant across changing circumstances, making the Torah not a fixed, dead letter but a living, breathing text that spoke to each new generation.

100-600 CE

The Deterioration of Jewish-Christian Relations

As Christianity grew in power, its relationship with Judaism transformed from sibling rivalry into something far darker. The earliest Christians were Jews, and the separation between the two faiths was gradual and painful -- scholars debate exactly when the "parting of the ways" was complete, with estimates ranging from the late first century to the fourth century. But by the time Christianity became the state religion of Rome, a deeply adversarial dynamic had taken root.

Central to this hostility was the charge of deicide -- the accusation that "the Jews" were collectively responsible for the death of Jesus. This idea, which draws on passages in the Gospels (particularly Matthew 27:25, where the crowd says "His blood be on us and on our children"), was developed and amplified by the Church Fathers. John Chrysostom (whose name means "golden-mouthed," reflecting his fame as a preacher), the eloquent Bishop of Constantinople (late 4th century), delivered a series of sermons "Against the Judaizers" that are among the most virulent anti-Jewish texts in Christian literature, calling synagogues "dwelling places of demons" and Jews "fit for slaughter." Augustine, while somewhat more restrained, argued that Jews should be preserved but in a state of degradation as living witnesses to Christian truth.

Under the Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Empire, these theological attitudes were translated into law. The Justinian Code (a comprehensive codification of Roman law issued in 534 CE by Emperor Justinian I, the most powerful Byzantine ruler) restricted Jewish rights: Jews could not build new synagogues, could not hold public office, could not testify against Christians in court, and were barred from various professions. Jews were increasingly ghettoized and marginalized. The seeds of medieval anti-Judaism -- which would eventually produce the Crusade massacres, the blood libel accusations (the false and vicious claim that Jews murdered Christian children to use their blood in religious rituals), the expulsions, and ultimately the racial antisemitism (hatred and persecution of Jews) of the modern era -- were planted in this period. The tragedy is that two faiths that shared the same patriarchs, the same scriptures, and the same God became locked in a cycle of hostility that would cause immense suffering for centuries to come.

Deep Dives

What is the Talmud?

The Talmud is the central text of Rabbinic Judaism and the primary source of Jewish law (halakha), ethics, philosophy, customs, and history. It is not a single book written by a single author, but a vast compilation of rabbinic discussions accumulated over several centuries (roughly 200-500 CE).

Structure: The Talmud has two layers. The first is the Mishnah (~200 CE), compiled by Rabbi Judah ha-Nasi -- a concise codification of Jewish oral law organized into six orders: Zeraim (agriculture), Moed (festivals), Nashim (women/family law), Nezikin (civil and criminal law), Kodashim (Temple service), and Tohorot (ritual purity). The second layer is the Gemara, which is the extensive rabbinic commentary and discussion on the Mishnah, compiled over the following three centuries. Mishnah + Gemara = Talmud.

Two Talmuds: There are actually two Talmuds: the Jerusalem (Palestinian) Talmud (~400 CE) and the Babylonian Talmud (~500 CE). The Babylonian Talmud is approximately three times longer and became the authoritative version. When people refer to "the Talmud" without qualification, they mean the Babylonian Talmud.

Significance: The Talmud is far more than a legal code. Its pages contain stories, parables, scientific observations, ethical debates, and philosophical reflections. Its dialectical method -- presenting arguments, counter-arguments, hypothetical cases, and minority opinions -- trained generations of Jewish thinkers in rigorous logical analysis. The Talmud made Judaism a religion of learning and argument, where questioning is not just permitted but sacred.

How did Judaism survive without the Temple?

The destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE threatened Judaism's very existence. The entire sacrificial system -- the means by which Jews atoned for sin, celebrated festivals, and maintained their covenant with God -- depended on the Temple. Without it, how could Judaism function?

Prayer replaces sacrifice: The rabbis at Yavneh declared that prayer could serve the same function as animal sacrifice. The three daily prayer services (Shacharit, Mincha, Ma'ariv) were mapped onto the three daily Temple offerings. The Amidah (the "standing prayer," also called the Shemoneh Esrei or "Eighteen Blessings") became the central act of Jewish worship, replacing the sacrificial altar.

The synagogue replaces the Temple: While synagogues existed before 70 CE (as places of study and communal gathering), they now became the primary sacred space. Every synagogue faces Jerusalem, and the Ark containing the Torah scrolls symbolically represents the Holy of Holies. The synagogue became the center of Jewish community life -- a place of worship, education, and mutual support.

The rabbi replaces the priest: In Temple-era Judaism, the priest (kohen) was the central religious figure, performing sacrifices and mediating between God and the people. With no Temple, there were no sacrifices to perform. The rabbi -- a teacher and legal authority, not a priest -- became the community's spiritual leader. Authority shifted from hereditary priestly lineage to scholarly achievement.

Torah study becomes the highest act: Perhaps most remarkably, the rabbis elevated Torah study to a status equal to or even higher than sacrifice. "Since the day the Temple was destroyed," says the Talmud, "the Holy One, Blessed be He, has nothing in this world but the four cubits of halakha" (halakha being the collective body of Jewish religious law derived from the Torah and rabbinic interpretation). Study became worship. The study house (bet midrash) became as sacred as the Temple. This transformation made Judaism uniquely portable: it could be practiced anywhere there was a community, a Torah scroll, and a teacher. It was this portability that allowed Judaism to survive two millennia of exile and dispersal.

Early Jewish-Christian relations

The relationship between Judaism and Christianity evolved through several distinct phases during this period, moving from internal family dispute to outright hostility.

Phase 1 -- Sibling sects (30-70 CE): The earliest Christians were Jews who differed from other Jews primarily in their belief that Jesus was the Messiah. They attended synagogue, observed the Sabbath, and followed the Torah. The "parting" was not yet a separation but an intra-Jewish debate, similar to the disagreements between Pharisees and Sadducees.

Phase 2 -- Growing apart (70-135 CE): The destruction of the Temple and the failure of the Bar Kokhba revolt accelerated the split. The rabbis at Yavneh may have introduced the Birkat ha-Minim (literally "Blessing Concerning the Heretics" -- actually a curse against sectarians and informers) into the synagogue liturgy, which many scholars believe was directed at Jewish Christians, effectively excluding them. Meanwhile, Christians increasingly defined themselves against Judaism, emphasizing the "old" versus "new" covenant.

Phase 3 -- Adversus Judaeos (2nd-4th centuries): As Christianity became a Gentile-majority movement, a genre of literature emerged known as "Adversus Judaeos" (Latin for "Against the Jews"). Church Fathers (influential early Christian theologians and writers whose works shaped orthodox doctrine) like Justin Martyr, Tertullian, and Origen argued that God had rejected the Jews and transferred his covenant to the Church (supersessionism or "replacement theology"). The Jews' suffering -- the destruction of the Temple, exile, dispersal -- was interpreted as divine punishment for rejecting and killing Christ.

Phase 4 -- Imperial hostility (4th-6th centuries): Once Christianity gained state power, theological antagonism translated into legal discrimination. Jews were barred from public office, restricted in their economic activities, forbidden from building new synagogues, and subjected to increasingly humiliating regulations. While complete physical destruction was not the policy (Augustine's doctrine of "witness" held that Jews should be preserved, albeit degraded, as proof of Christian truth), the legal and social degradation of this period established patterns that would intensify dramatically in the medieval era.

Multi-Faith Perspectives

The events of this era -- the destruction of the Temple, the rise of Christianity, the development of Rabbinic Judaism -- are viewed through profoundly different lenses depending on one's religious tradition. Understanding these different perspectives is essential to grasping why these events remain so emotionally and theologically charged even today.

Section at a Glance

  • Judaism mourns the Temple's destruction as the greatest catastrophe in Jewish history, fasting on Tisha B'Av each year; Christianity saw it as fulfillment of Jesus's prophecy and divine judgment
  • Islam acknowledges the Temple Mount (al-Haram al-Sharif) as sacred -- the site of Muhammad's Night Journey -- and views the destruction as God's plan for a people who strayed
  • The "parting of the ways" between Judaism and Christianity is understood differently: Jews see Christians as having departed from Torah; Christians claimed to be the "true Israel"
  • The Talmud is viewed by Jews as the Oral Torah essential for understanding God's law, while Christianity and Islam see their own later revelations as superseding or correcting earlier ones

The Temple's Destruction: Four Perspectives

Topic Secular History Judaism Christianity Islam
What happened Roman legions under Titus destroyed the Jerusalem Temple in 70 CE after a four-year Jewish revolt driven by political, economic, and religious grievances against Roman occupation. It was one of many Roman campaigns to suppress provincial rebellion. The destruction of the Beit HaMikdash (Holy Temple) was the greatest catastrophe in Jewish history. It ended the sacrificial system, scattered the Jewish people, and began a long exile (galut) that would last nearly two millennia. It is mourned every year on Tisha B'Av with fasting, lamentation, and the reading of the Book of Lamentations. The destruction of the Temple was prophesied by Jesus himself (Mark 13:2: "Not one stone will be left upon another"). Many early Christians saw it as God's judgment on the Jewish people for rejecting and crucifying Christ, and as confirmation that the old covenant had been superseded by the new covenant in Christ. The Temple (al-Haikal) is acknowledged as a holy site. Muslims believe the site (al-Haram al-Sharif) is where the Prophet Muhammad ascended to heaven during the Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj). The destruction is understood as part of God's plan and the consequences that befall peoples who stray from His guidance. The site is now home to the Dome of the Rock and Al-Aqsa Mosque.
Why it matters The destruction reshaped the demographics, culture, and politics of the Eastern Mediterranean. It eliminated Jerusalem as a major political center for centuries and catalyzed the transformation of Judaism from a Temple-based cult to a portable, text-based religion -- one of the most remarkable adaptations in religious history. The Temple's destruction is the defining trauma of Jewish collective memory. It forced the radical reinvention of Judaism around Torah, prayer, and community. The longing to rebuild the Temple and return to Jerusalem became central to Jewish liturgy and identity: "Next year in Jerusalem" is recited at every Passover seder. The Western Wall remains the holiest accessible site in Judaism. The destruction validated the Christian claim that God had moved beyond the Temple system. The writer of the Epistle to the Hebrews argues that Jesus is the ultimate high priest and his sacrifice the ultimate offering, making the Temple obsolete. The event confirmed for early Christians that history was unfolding according to God's plan. The sanctity of Jerusalem (al-Quds) and the Temple Mount (al-Haram al-Sharif) is deeply embedded in Islamic tradition. It is the third holiest site in Islam. The historical destruction of the Temple is referenced in the Quran (Surah al-Isra 17:4-7), which speaks of two occasions when the Children of Israel would "cause corruption in the land" and face divine consequences.
Paul and the Gentile mission Paul of Tarsus was a Hellenistic Jewish intellectual who reinterpreted the Jesus movement as a universal salvation religion accessible to non-Jews. His missionary journeys and letters shaped the organizational and theological structure of early Christianity and were critical to its spread beyond Palestine. Paul is viewed as a Jew who departed from Judaism by abolishing the Torah's commandments for Gentile converts. His theology of faith versus works of the Law is seen as a fundamental misunderstanding or rejection of Judaism's core teaching that Torah observance is itself a joyful response to God's covenant, not a burden to be escaped. Paul is the Apostle to the Gentiles, chosen by the risen Christ to bring salvation to the nations. His epistles are sacred scripture. His teaching that salvation comes by grace through faith, not by works of the Law, is foundational to Christian theology. He is venerated as a saint and martyr. Paul (Bulus) is not mentioned in the Quran. Some Muslim scholars view him critically, arguing that he distorted the original monotheistic message of Jesus (Isa) by introducing doctrines like the divinity of Christ, the Trinity, and atonement that are incompatible with Islam's strict monotheism (tawhid). Others take a more neutral historical view.
Constantine and state Christianity Constantine's adoption of Christianity was a pivotal political act that transformed a minority religion into an imperial institution. Whether motivated by genuine faith, political pragmatism, or both, his patronage fundamentally altered the trajectory of both the religion and the Empire. Constantine's establishment of Christian imperial power marks the beginning of centuries of Jewish suffering under Christian rule. The Christianization of Rome led directly to legal restrictions on Jews, forced conversions, and the theological framework that justified persecution. It was a catastrophe for Jewish life in the Roman world. Constantine is seen as God's instrument for ending persecution and allowing the Church to fulfill its mission. The Orthodox Church venerates him as a saint ("Equal to the Apostles"). Catholic tradition honors him as the first Christian emperor. However, some theologians question whether the "Constantinian shift" -- the marriage of Church and state -- compromised the Church's prophetic independence. Islam views Constantine's establishment of Christianity as a mixed legacy. While it ended persecution of monotheists, it also led to the corruption of Jesus's original message through the imposition of Trinitarian doctrine (particularly the Nicene Creed). Islam sees itself as the restoration of the pure monotheism that was distorted by these councils.

The Spread of Christianity & Jewish Diaspora by 600 CE

The Christian World by 600 CE: By the end of this period, Christianity had spread across the entire Roman Empire and beyond -- into Armenia (the first nation to adopt Christianity as a state religion, in 301 CE), Ethiopia, Persia, and even India (the Thomas Christians of Kerala). Jewish diaspora communities existed in parallel across much of this same geography -- in Babylon, Egypt, North Africa, Rome, Iberia, Gaul, and the Rhine Valley -- but increasingly under legal restrictions imposed by the Christian state.

A Note on Perspective

These perspectives are not "right" or "wrong" -- they represent deeply held convictions rooted in centuries of tradition, scripture, and lived experience. Understanding why the same event means such different things to different communities is the first step toward genuine interfaith comprehension. The goal of this course is not to adjudicate between these views but to present them honestly and respectfully.
