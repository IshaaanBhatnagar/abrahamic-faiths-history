Chapter 7

Enlightenment to Nationalism

1700 -- 1900 CE

The Age of Reason shattered old certainties. As European thinkers exalted human reason over divine revelation, the relationship between religion, state, and identity was remade from the ground up. Jews faced the promise and peril of emancipation. The Ottoman world struggled to modernize. And nationalism -- the most powerful force of the 19th century -- began redrawing every map and redefining every community, planting seeds whose harvest would be reaped in blood throughout the 20th century.

The Enlightenment & Religion

The 18th century European Enlightenment was, at its core, a revolution in authority. For over a thousand years, the Church -- Catholic, Orthodox, or Protestant -- had been the ultimate arbiter of truth. The Enlightenment challenged that monopoly with a simple, radical proposition: human reason, not divine revelation, should be the foundation of knowledge, law, and governance. This was not merely an intellectual exercise. It reshaped every institution it touched, from universities to monarchies to the very concept of what it meant to be a citizen.

  • The French Revolution (1789) nationalizes Church property, requires priests to swear loyalty to the state over the Pope, and attempts to de-Christianize France entirely

17th-18th Century

Reason Over Revelation

The Enlightenment built on the Scientific Revolution of the 17th century -- Galileo, Newton, and others had shown that the universe operated according to discoverable natural laws, not divine whim. Thinkers like John Locke (1632-1704) argued that human beings had natural rights -- life, liberty, property -- that existed independently of any king or church. Voltaire (1694-1778) launched devastating satirical attacks on organized religion, particularly the Catholic Church, which he saw as an engine of superstition and intolerance. "Ecrasez l'infame!" ("Crush the infamous thing!") was his famous rallying cry. Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712-1778) proposed a "social contract" in which legitimate government derived its authority from the consent of the governed, not from God. Together, these thinkers dismantled the intellectual scaffolding that had supported religious authority over European political life for more than a millennium.

18th Century

Impact on Christianity: The Rise of Deism

The Enlightenment did not produce a generation of atheists -- at least not at first. Instead, many Enlightenment thinkers gravitated toward Deism: the belief that God created the universe and set it in motion according to natural laws, but does not intervene in daily affairs. No miracles. No answered prayers. No chosen people. God as watchmaker, not as father. This was a profound break from orthodox Christianity, which depended on divine intervention -- the Incarnation, the Resurrection, the sacraments. Deism stripped Christianity down to a philosophical skeleton: a creator God and a moral code, nothing more. Thomas Jefferson famously created his own version of the New Testament, literally cutting out every miracle with a razor blade and keeping only the ethical teachings of Jesus. The implications were enormous: if miracles were suspect, so was the Church's authority to interpret them. Skepticism of miracles led naturally to skepticism of the clergy, of Church wealth, of religious courts, and of the entire structure of ecclesiastical power.

1789

The French Revolution: Church vs. State

The French Revolution of 1789 was the Enlightenment put into violent practice. The revolutionaries did not merely challenge the monarchy -- they challenged the entire ancien regime, and the Catholic Church was one of its pillars. The Church owned roughly 10% of all land in France. Bishops lived like princes. Tithes burdened the poor. When the Revolution came, it came for the Church too. In November 1789, Church property was nationalized -- seized by the state and sold to finance the Revolution. In 1790, the Civil Constitution of the Clergy required all priests and bishops to swear an oath of loyalty to the French state, not the Pope. About half the clergy refused, creating a deep schism in French society. The revolutionary slogan -- "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" -- proclaimed universal rights that transcended religious identity. For the first time in European history, citizenship was being defined not by baptism or confession, but by membership in a political community. This was a seismic shift. During the most radical phase (1793-1794), revolutionaries actively de-Christianized France: churches were looted, turned into "Temples of Reason," priests were executed or forced to renounce their vows. The revolutionary calendar replaced the Christian one, erasing saints' days and even the seven-day week.

What is Emancipation (in the Jewish context)?

Emancipation is the process by which Jews in Europe were granted full civil and political rights as citizens of the countries they lived in. For centuries, Jews had been legally excluded from civic life -- barred from most professions, forbidden from owning land, confined to ghettos, and governed by separate laws. Emancipation meant being recognized, for the first time, as equal citizens rather than tolerated outsiders.

1791

Jewish Emancipation in France

One of the Revolution's most consequential acts came in September 1791, when France's National Assembly granted full citizenship to Jews -- the first time in European history that Jews were recognized as equal citizens of a modern state. This was not a gift freely given; it came after intense debate. The logic was Enlightenment logic: if rights are universal, they cannot be withheld on the basis of religion. But the emancipation came with an implicit bargain: Jews were welcomed as individual citizens, not as a separate community. As Count Stanislas de Clermont-Tonnerre put it in the Assembly debate: "To the Jews as a nation, nothing; to the Jews as individuals, everything." The message was clear -- be French first, Jewish second.

Napoleon Bonaparte took this further. In 1806, he convened an Assembly of Jewish Notables, and in 1807, he reconvened the Grand Sanhedrin -- a deliberate echo of the ancient Jewish supreme court -- to answer twelve questions about Jewish loyalty. Were Jews willing to be loyal citizens of France? Did they consider France their country? Were they willing to serve in the military? Were marriages between Jews and Christians permissible under Jewish law? The Jewish delegates affirmed their loyalty to France and declared themselves "French citizens of the Mosaic faith." Napoleon then issued decrees that both expanded and restricted Jewish rights -- a pattern that would repeat throughout the 19th century. Emancipation was real but conditional, and it could always be revoked.

The Enlightenment Bargain

The Enlightenment offered religious minorities, especially Jews, a transformative deal: abandon your separate communal identity, embrace the universal values of reason and citizenship, and you will be accepted as equals. This bargain would define Jewish life in Europe for the next two centuries -- and its failure would give rise to Zionism.

What is the Haskalah?

The Haskalah (from the Hebrew word for "intellect" or "enlightenment") was the Jewish Enlightenment movement of the 18th and 19th centuries. It urged Jews to adopt secular education, learn European languages, and integrate into broader society while maintaining their Jewish identity. Think of it as the Jewish world's own internal debate about how to respond to modernity -- a debate that produced lasting divisions between secular, Reform, and Orthodox Judaism.

Jewish Paths: Haskalah, Hasidism, Emancipation

The 18th and 19th centuries shattered the relative unity of European Jewish life. Faced with the Enlightenment's challenge and the possibility of emancipation, Jews split into multiple paths: modernizers who embraced secular education and integration, mystics who sought God through joy and ecstasy, traditionalists who defended Talmudic scholarship, and reformers who reinvented Jewish practice for the modern age. These divisions, born in this era, define Jewish life to this day.

Moses Mendelssohn (1729-1786): Father of the Jewish Enlightenment

Moses Mendelssohn was a hunchbacked Jewish philosopher from Dessau who became one of the most celebrated intellectuals in all of Germany. A close friend of the playwright Gotthold Ephraim Lessing (who modeled the wise Jewish character in his play "Nathan the Wise" on Mendelssohn), he moved effortlessly between the Jewish and Christian intellectual worlds -- something no Jew had done before at that level.

Mendelssohn argued that Judaism was not opposed to reason -- in fact, it was uniquely compatible with it. Judaism, he said, was not a set of dogmas but a system of revealed legislation: divine laws governing conduct, not beliefs. Jews could therefore embrace Enlightenment philosophy, learn European languages, study secular subjects, and participate fully in European culture -- all without abandoning their faith. His famous formulation: "Be a Jew at home and a man in the street."

His most consequential practical act was translating the Torah into German (written in Hebrew characters), which opened the door for Jews to learn the German language and, through it, access European culture. This single act was a gateway drug to modernity. Within a generation, Mendelssohn's own grandchildren had converted to Christianity -- including the composer Felix Mendelssohn. This became a bitter irony: the man who tried to bridge Judaism and modernity saw his own family walk across that bridge and never come back.

The Haskalah Movement: Jewish Modernization

The Haskalah (from the Hebrew word for "intellect" or "enlightenment") was the broader movement that grew from Mendelssohn's example. Its adherents, called maskilim, championed secular education, European languages (especially German and later Russian), and integration into broader society. They founded modern Jewish literature -- writing poetry, novels, essays, and journalism in Hebrew, Yiddish, and European languages. They established modern Jewish schools that taught mathematics, science, geography, and languages alongside traditional religious subjects.

The Haskalah was strongest in Germany, Austria, and later in the Russian Empire. In Russia, maskilim like Isaac Baer Levinsohn (the "Russian Mendelssohn") argued that Jews needed secular education to lift themselves out of poverty and gain the respect of the broader society. The movement produced a flowering of Hebrew literature, reviving Hebrew as a literary language after centuries of being used primarily for prayer and religious study. Writers like Judah Leib Gordon urged Jews to modernize with his famous poem: "Be a man when you go out and a Jew in your tent."

But the Haskalah was deeply controversial within Jewish communities. Traditionalists saw it as a Trojan horse for assimilation. And they were not entirely wrong -- many maskilim did eventually drift away from Jewish practice, and some converted to Christianity entirely. The Haskalah's great achievement was creating a modern, secular Jewish intellectual culture. Its great danger was that this culture could become a substitute for, rather than a complement to, Jewish faith.

The Hasidic Movement: Joy, Mysticism, and the Baal Shem Tov

While the Haskalah responded to modernity by embracing it, the Hasidic movement responded to the spiritual crisis of Eastern European Jewry by going deeper into faith. Founded by Rabbi Israel ben Eliezer (c. 1700-1760), known as the Baal Shem Tov ("Master of the Good Name," often abbreviated as the Besht), Hasidism arose in the impoverished Jewish communities of Podolia and Volhynia in what is now western Ukraine.

The context matters: these were communities devastated by the Khmelnytsky massacres of 1648-1649 (in which perhaps 100,000 Jews were killed) and disillusioned by the false messiah Sabbatai Zevi (1666). Traditional rabbinic Judaism, with its emphasis on rigorous Talmudic study, felt remote and inaccessible to ordinary Jews -- many of whom were illiterate and desperately poor. What good was an intellectual tradition if you couldn't read?

The Baal Shem Tov offered a revolutionary alternative: God could be reached not just through scholarly study but through prayer, joy, song, dance, and the sanctification of everyday acts. A simple man praying with a full heart was closer to God than a scholar studying without feeling. Hasidism drew heavily on Kabbalistic mysticism, teaching that divine sparks were hidden in everything and that human beings could elevate these sparks through devout action and intention (kavanah).

Hasidism spread like wildfire through Eastern Europe. It organized around charismatic leaders called rebbes or tzaddikim (righteous ones), who served as intermediaries between their followers and God. Each rebbe founded a court and a dynasty, and followers were fiercely loyal. By the early 19th century, Hasidism had captured perhaps half the Jewish population of Eastern Europe. Major Hasidic dynasties -- Chabad (Lubavitch), Breslov, Satmar, Ger, Belz -- each developed distinctive practices and philosophies, and many survive to this day.

The Hasidic emphasis on reaching God through joy, music, and ecstatic prayer rather than scholarly study alone echoes Sufi Islam's mystical tradition, which similarly stressed a personal, emotional relationship with God over legalistic observance. Both movements arose as popular reactions to what ordinary believers saw as an overly rigid religious establishment -- separated by centuries and faiths, yet remarkably parallel in spirit.

The Orthodox Response: Mitnagdim and the Vilna Gaon

Not everyone welcomed the Hasidic revolution. The most formidable opponent was Rabbi Elijah ben Solomon Zalman (1720-1797), known as the Vilna Gaon (the "Genius of Vilna"). The greatest Talmudic scholar of his generation -- perhaps of any generation -- the Vilna Gaon was a legendary intellectual who reportedly studied 18 hours a day and mastered not only the entire Talmud but also Kabbalah, mathematics, and astronomy.

The Vilna Gaon saw Hasidism as a dangerous distortion of Judaism. He objected to its de-emphasis of Talmudic study, its elevation of the rebbe to near-messianic status, its changes to the prayer liturgy, and what he saw as its emotional excess. In 1772, he issued a ban (cherem) against the Hasidim, forbidding intermarriage with them and even forbidding eating meat they had slaughtered. His followers were called Mitnagdim ("opponents").

The Mitnagdim also opposed the Haskalah, which they saw as an even greater threat -- a path to assimilation and the abandonment of Torah. The Vilna Gaon's legacy was a robust defense of traditional, intellectually rigorous Talmudic Judaism. The great Lithuanian yeshivas (Volozhin, Mir, Slabodka) that became the gold standard of Orthodox Torah learning were direct products of his influence. Ironically, over time, Hasidim and Mitnagdim found common ground in their shared opposition to the Haskalah and, later, to secularism. Today, both are considered part of the Haredi (ultra-Orthodox) world.

Reform Judaism: Reinventing Jewish Practice

In Germany in the early 19th century, a different response to modernity emerged: Reform Judaism. If Hasidism doubled down on faith and the Mitnagdim doubled down on scholarship, Reform Judaism asked a different question: what if Jewish practice itself could be modernized to fit the new era?

The first Reform temple opened in Seesen, Germany, in 1810, founded by Israel Jacobson. It was revolutionary: sermons were delivered in German instead of Yiddish, organ music accompanied services (unheard of in traditional synagogues), men and women sat together, prayers were shortened, and references to returning to Zion and rebuilding the Temple were removed. The Hamburg Temple (1818) went further, producing a new prayer book that eliminated prayers for a personal messiah and for the restoration of the Temple sacrifices. The logic was straightforward: German Jews were Germans. Germany was their homeland, not Palestine. Judaism was a religion, not a nationality.

Leading Reform thinkers like Abraham Geiger (1810-1874) argued that Judaism had always evolved and adapted -- the Talmud itself was a centuries-long process of adaptation. Reform was simply the latest stage. They applied modern historical methods to Jewish texts, asking when and why particular laws were written, and distinguishing between the eternal ethical core of Judaism and its time-bound ritual expressions. This intellectual movement, known as Wissenschaft des Judentums ("Science of Judaism"), produced rigorous academic study of Jewish history and texts.

Orthodox leaders were horrified. Rabbi Moses Sofer (the Chatam Sofer, 1762-1839) of Pressburg issued a famous ruling: "Chadash asur min ha-Torah" -- "Innovation is forbidden by the Torah." He meant it literally. The battle between Reform and Orthodox would define German (and later American) Jewish life for generations.

Jewish Emancipation Across Europe: Progress and Backlash

Following France's lead in 1791, Jewish emancipation gradually spread across Western and Central Europe -- but it was uneven, fragile, and often reversible. Napoleon carried emancipation wherever his armies marched. When his armies retreated, so did Jewish rights. The Congress of Vienna (1815) saw many German states revoke the rights Napoleon had granted.

Napoleon emancipated Jews in every country he conquered -- the Netherlands, Italy, the German states, and beyond. But when he was defeated at Waterloo in 1815, many of those rights were immediately revoked. Jewish emancipation in Europe advanced and retreated with the fortunes of a single French general.

Prussia granted partial emancipation in 1812, but full equality came only in 1871 with German unification -- and even then, Jews were effectively barred from the officer corps, the senior civil service, and university professorships unless they converted. Austria-Hungary gradually emancipated Jews over the course of the 19th century; Italy did the same during unification. Britain allowed Jews to sit in Parliament only in 1858.

What is the Pale of Settlement?

The Pale of Settlement was a vast region along the western border of the Russian Empire -- covering parts of present-day Poland, Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, and Moldova -- where Jews were legally required to live from 1791 to 1917. Outside the Pale, Jews were generally forbidden to reside, own property, or work. It was, in effect, a giant ghetto encompassing an entire region, confining over 5 million Jews to an area the Russian state deemed acceptable for them.

In the Russian Empire, home to the largest Jewish population in the world (over 5 million by 1900), there was no emancipation at all. Jews were confined to the Pale of Settlement -- a vast ghetto comprising parts of present-day Poland, Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, and Moldova. They were barred from most professions, subject to special taxes, and periodically expelled from cities. Tsar Alexander II (r. 1855-1881) loosened some restrictions, allowing wealthy Jews, university graduates, and skilled artisans to live outside the Pale. But his assassination in 1881 triggered a catastrophic backlash.

The pattern across Europe was depressingly consistent: emancipation was granted when liberals were in power, restricted or revoked whenever conservatives returned. Jewish rights were a barometer of broader political liberalism -- and the barometer was wildly unstable.

The Islamic World in Transition

While Europe surged forward on Enlightenment ideals and industrial power, the Ottoman Empire -- for centuries the dominant power in the Islamic world -- entered a long, agonizing decline. Military defeats, territorial losses, and internal stagnation forced a reckoning: how should the Muslim world respond to Western ascendancy? The answers ranged from puritan revivalism to wholesale modernization, and the debate continues to this day.

1683

Ottoman Decline: The Failed Siege of Vienna

The turning point came in 1683, when the Ottoman Empire launched its second siege of Vienna -- and failed catastrophically. A combined force of Habsburg and Polish troops, led by the Polish King Jan III Sobieski, routed the Ottoman army in one of the largest cavalry charges in history. The defeat was not just military; it was psychological. For centuries, the Ottoman advance into Europe had seemed unstoppable. After Vienna, the direction reversed. The Treaty of Karlowitz (1699) forced the Ottomans to cede Hungary, Transylvania, and parts of Croatia to Austria. Over the next two centuries, the empire would hemorrhage territory to Austria, Russia, and eventually to its own nationalist minorities. By the 19th century, European diplomats called the Ottoman Empire the "Sick Man of Europe" -- a phrase attributed to Tsar Nicholas I of Russia.

By 1900, the Ottoman Empire had lost roughly 40% of its territory in just one century -- including Greece, Serbia, Romania, Bulgaria, Egypt, Algeria, and Tunisia. An empire that once stretched from Vienna to Yemen was hemorrhaging land faster than any great power in modern history.

1703-1792

The Wahhabi Movement: Puritan Islam in Arabia

What is Wahhabism?

Wahhabism is a strict, puritanical form of Sunni Islam founded in 18th-century Arabia. It calls for a return to what it considers the "pure" Islam of the Prophet Muhammad's time, rejecting centuries of later tradition, mysticism (Sufism), and the veneration of saints and shrines as innovations and idolatry. Often used interchangeably with Salafism today, it became the religious foundation of Saudi Arabia and one of the most influential -- and controversial -- movements in modern Islam.

In the Arabian Peninsula, far from the Ottoman centers of power, a very different response to Islamic decline was taking shape. Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab (1703-1792), a scholar from Najd in central Arabia, concluded that the Muslim world had declined because Muslims had strayed from the pure, original Islam of the Prophet Muhammad and the early community (salaf). He launched a fierce campaign against what he saw as innovations (bid'ah) and corruption: the veneration of saints' tombs and shrines, Sufi mysticism, the use of amulets and talismans, and any practice not directly supported by the Quran and the hadith (sayings of the Prophet).

In 1744, Abd al-Wahhab made an alliance that would change history. He struck a pact with Muhammad ibn Saud, the ruler of Diriyah, a small town in Najd. The deal was simple: Ibn Saud would provide political and military power; Abd al-Wahhab would provide religious legitimacy. Together, they would build a state founded on purified Islam. This Saudi-Wahhabi alliance is the founding partnership of what would eventually become Saudi Arabia.

The Saudi-Wahhabi forces expanded aggressively. In 1802, they sacked Karbala, the Shia holy city in Iraq, destroying the shrine of Husayn -- a shocking act of desecration that poisoned Sunni-Shia relations for centuries. In 1803-1813, they captured Mecca and Medina, destroying tombs and shrines they considered idolatrous, including those in the famous Jannat al-Baqi cemetery. The Ottomans, horrified by this challenge to their authority as custodians of the Holy Cities, dispatched Muhammad Ali of Egypt to crush the Saudi-Wahhabi state, which he did by 1818. But the alliance between the House of Saud and the Wahhabi religious establishment endured. It would be revived in the 20th century by Abdulaziz ibn Saud, founder of the modern Kingdom of Saudi Arabia (1932), and Wahhabism -- often called Salafism today -- would become one of the most influential and controversial movements in modern Islam.

1805-1849

Muhammad Ali of Egypt: The Modernizer

If Wahhabism was one response to Western ascendancy -- a retreat to fundamentals -- Muhammad Ali Pasha of Egypt (r. 1805-1849) represented the opposite: aggressive, top-down modernization on the European model. An Albanian-born Ottoman officer who seized power in Egypt after Napoleon's brief occupation (1798-1801), Muhammad Ali transformed Egypt into the most powerful state in the Middle East. He built a European-style army trained by French officers, established modern industries (textiles, munitions), sent Egyptian students to study in Paris, and reformed education and agriculture. He was ruthless, brilliant, and thoroughly secular in his approach to power.

Muhammad Ali's Egypt challenged the Ottoman Empire itself, conquering Sudan, the Hejaz (western Arabia), and Syria. Only European intervention (Britain and Austria, who feared a too-powerful Egypt would destabilize the region) forced him to pull back. His legacy was mixed: he proved that a Muslim state could modernize rapidly, but his model was authoritarian and exploitative, extracting wealth from Egyptian peasants to fund his projects. The tension between modernization and authoritarianism would haunt the Middle East for the next two centuries.

1839-1876

The Tanzimat Reforms: Ottoman Modernization

Inspired partly by Muhammad Ali's example and partly by the desperate need to prevent further disintegration, the Ottoman government embarked on the Tanzimat ("reorganization") reforms from 1839 to 1876. The Hatt-i Sharif of Gulhane (1839) promised all Ottoman subjects -- regardless of religion -- security of life, honor, and property. The Hatt-i Humayun of 1856 went further, guaranteeing full religious equality: Christians and Jews were to have the same legal rights as Muslims, serve in the military, and hold government positions.

The Tanzimat introduced modern legal codes (based partly on French law), secular schools, a modern postal system, railroads, and a new civil service. The goal was to create a common Ottoman citizenship -- "Ottomanism" -- that would hold the empire's diverse ethnic and religious communities together. But the reforms satisfied no one fully.

What is Dhimmi Status?

Dhimmi (Arabic for "protected person") was the legal status assigned to non-Muslims -- primarily Jews and Christians -- living under Islamic rule. Dhimmis were allowed to practice their religion and govern their own community affairs, but in exchange they paid a special tax (the jizya) and accepted a subordinate social position. They could not bear arms, build new houses of worship without permission, or hold authority over Muslims. The system offered genuine protection in an era when religious minorities elsewhere faced far worse, but it was explicitly unequal.

Conservative Muslims resented the elevation of non-Muslims to legal equality, which they saw as a violation of the traditional Islamic order (in which non-Muslims were dhimmis -- protected but subordinate). Christian minorities found the reforms too slow and incomplete. And European powers, who had long used the "protection of Christians" as a pretext for intervention, were happy to keep the pressure on regardless of reforms.

1821-1829

Greek War of Independence

The first great nationalist revolt against Ottoman rule came from Greece. Beginning in 1821, Greek Christians -- inspired by Enlightenment ideals, their own classical heritage, and support from the Orthodox Church -- rose against Ottoman rule. The war was brutal on both sides. The Ottoman massacre of Greeks on the island of Chios (1822) shocked European opinion. Romantic poets like Lord Byron (who died in Greece in 1824) rallied support. Eventually, Britain, France, and Russia intervened, destroying the Ottoman-Egyptian fleet at the Battle of Navarino (1827). Greece gained independence in 1829 -- the first Christian nation to break free of Ottoman rule. The precedent was set: the empire could be dismembered, piece by piece, along ethnic and religious lines.

1853-1856

The Crimean War: Religion as Pretext

The Crimean War (1853-1856) illustrated how religion remained a tool of great-power politics even in the age of nationalism. The ostensible cause was a dispute over who had the right to protect Christian holy places in Ottoman Palestine -- Catholic France or Orthodox Russia. Tsar Nicholas I demanded that the Ottoman Sultan recognize Russia as the protector of all Orthodox Christians in the empire -- a demand that would have given Russia enormous leverage over Ottoman affairs. The Ottomans refused, backed by Britain and France, who feared Russian expansion. The resulting war -- fought mainly in Crimea -- cost over 300,000 lives and exposed the Ottoman Empire's military weakness, even in victory (the Ottomans won, but only because Britain and France did most of the fighting).

The deeper significance: the conflict over holy places in Palestine foreshadowed the far more devastating conflicts of the 20th century. The same land, the same holy sites, the same intersection of religion, nationalism, and great-power politics.

Rise of Nationalism

The 19th century witnessed the rise of the most powerful political force of the modern era: nationalism. The idea that each "people" -- defined by shared language, ethnicity, culture, and history -- deserved its own sovereign state reshaped every empire, every minority, and every faith community on earth. For multi-ethnic, multi-religious empires like the Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and Russian, nationalism was a death sentence. For peoples without states -- Italians, Germans, Greeks, Serbs, and eventually Jews and Arabs -- it was a rallying cry.

Section at a Glance

  • Nationalism replaces religion as the primary source of identity: people become "French" or "Greek" or "Serbian" rather than simply Catholic, Orthodox, or Muslim
  • The Ottoman millet system collapses as Greece (1829), Serbia (1830), Romania (1878), and Bulgaria (1878) each break away as independent nation-states
  • The Arab Nahda (cultural renaissance) emerges in Beirut and Cairo, reviving Arabic literature and planting seeds of Arab national consciousness that will reshape the 20th-century Middle East
  • Religion and nationalism sometimes align (Greek Orthodoxy + Greek nationhood) and sometimes conflict (secular Arab nationalism tries to unite Christians and Muslims under one identity)

Nationalism: The New Religion

Before nationalism, most people identified primarily through religion, dynasty, or locality. A peasant in 1700 was a Catholic from Provence, or an Orthodox from Wallachia, or a Jew from Vilna -- not a "Frenchman" or "Romanian" or "Lithuanian" in the modern sense. Nationalism changed this. It proposed that the fundamental unit of human belonging was the nation -- a group defined by shared language, culture, and (usually imagined) common ancestry. The implications were revolutionary. If the nation was the natural unit, then multi-ethnic empires were unnatural. If each nation deserved self-determination, then the Ottomans ruling Arabs, the Habsburgs ruling Hungarians, and the Russians ruling Poles were all forms of oppression.

Religion and nationalism had a complicated relationship. Sometimes they aligned: Greek nationalism was inseparable from Greek Orthodoxy; Polish nationalism was deeply Catholic; Serbian nationalism was bound up with the Serbian Orthodox Church. But sometimes they conflicted: Arab nationalism sought to unite Arabic-speaking Christians and Muslims, subordinating religious identity to linguistic and cultural identity. Jewish nationalism (Zionism) was largely secular, driven by Jews who had given up on religion but not on Jewish peoplehood.

The Strains on Multi-Ethnic Empires

Ottoman Empire

Nationalism was fatal to the Ottoman system. The Ottoman Empire had governed its diverse populations through the millet system -- separate religious communities (Muslim, Greek Orthodox, Armenian, Jewish) each managing their own internal affairs under overall Ottoman sovereignty. Nationalism shattered this arrangement by proposing that Greeks, Serbs, Bulgarians, Romanians, and Arabs each constituted a separate nation deserving independence. One by one, they broke away: Greece (1829), Serbia (autonomous 1830, independent 1878), Romania (1878), Bulgaria (1878). Each secession weakened the empire and emboldened the next nationalist movement.

Austro-Hungarian Empire

The Habsburg Empire faced similar pressures. Hungarians, Czechs, Poles, Croats, Slovaks, Slovenes, Romanians, and others all developed national movements demanding autonomy or independence. The Compromise of 1867, which created the Dual Monarchy of Austria-Hungary, satisfied Hungarian nationalism but left the Slavic peoples frustrated. This instability would eventually provide the spark for World War I.

Russian Empire

Russia governed the largest Jewish population in the world, as well as Poles, Finns, Ukrainians, Georgians, and dozens of other nationalities. Russian policy oscillated between Russification (forcing minorities to adopt Russian language and Orthodox Christianity) and repression. The treatment of Jews was particularly harsh -- confined to the Pale of Settlement, subject to quotas, military conscription, and periodic pogroms.

The Arab Nahda: An Intellectual Renaissance

In the Arabic-speaking world, the 19th century saw an intellectual awakening known as the Nahda ("Renaissance" or "Awakening"), centered in Egypt, Lebanon, and Syria, running roughly from the 1830s to the 1880s. The Nahda was a cultural revolution: its thinkers modernized the Arabic language, founded newspapers and literary journals, translated European works into Arabic, and debated the future of Arab society.

Key figures included Rifa'a al-Tahtawi (1801-1873), an Egyptian scholar who spent five years in Paris and returned to advocate for modern education, women's rights, and constitutional government. Boutros al-Bustani (1819-1883), a Lebanese Christian, founded the first modern Arabic encyclopedia and one of the first Arabic-language newspapers, and championed a secular, multi-confessional Arab identity. These thinkers planted the seeds of Arab nationalism -- an identity based on the Arabic language and a shared cultural heritage rather than on Islam alone. This was crucial because Arab Christians played a disproportionately large role in the Nahda, and Arab nationalism, at least in its early form, was explicitly inclusive of Christians.

Christian missionaries from Europe also contributed to this awakening, establishing schools and hospitals across the Levant and introducing the printing press to the region. The American University of Beirut (founded 1866 as the Syrian Protestant College) and the Universite Saint-Joseph (Jesuit, founded 1875) became incubators of the Nahda's intellectual elite. But missionary activity also created tensions -- many Muslims resented the implicit assumption that Western education came paired with Western religion and Western political influence.

Seeds of the 20th Century

This era planted every single seed that would erupt in the 20th century's catastrophic conflicts. Nationalism taught peoples to define themselves against each other. The decline of the Ottoman Empire created a power vacuum in the Middle East that European empires rushed to fill. Jewish emancipation's failure drove the birth of Zionism. Arab nationalism's awakening created aspirations that would collide with Zionist aspirations in Palestine. The Wahhabi-Saudi alliance laid the groundwork for the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia and the global spread of Salafist Islam. And the great empires -- Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian -- were all weakened to the breaking point, needing only the spark of a world war to shatter entirely.

Antisemitism & the Birth of Zionism

The great promise of the Enlightenment was that reason would triumph over prejudice. For Europe's Jews, this meant that emancipation and education would finally end centuries of persecution. It didn't. Instead, the late 19th century saw antisemitism intensify, mutate, and ultimately produce the political movement that would reshape the Middle East forever: Zionism.

The New Antisemitism: From Religion to Race

Medieval antisemitism was primarily religious: Jews were hated because they rejected Christ, because of blood libel accusations, because they were associated with usury. In theory, a Jew who converted to Christianity was no longer a target (though in practice, converted Jews -- conversos -- were often still suspect, as in Inquisition-era Spain). The 19th century produced something new and far more dangerous: racial antisemitism. Influenced by pseudo-scientific theories of race, thinkers like Arthur de Gobineau and Houston Stewart Chamberlain argued that races had fixed, inherited characteristics. Jews were not merely adherents of a wrong religion -- they were members of an alien, inferior (or dangerously clever) race. A Jew could convert, change his name, speak perfect German, and fight in the Kaiser's army, and it made no difference: his blood was still Jewish.

In 1879, the German journalist Wilhelm Marr coined the term "antisemitism" (Antisemitismus) to give this hatred a scientific-sounding label. He founded the League of Antisemites, the first political organization dedicated specifically to combating Jewish influence. Political parties built on antisemitic platforms gained seats in the German and Austrian parliaments. Karl Lueger, the antisemitic mayor of Vienna (1897-1910), was a major influence on the young Adolf Hitler. Antisemitism was no longer a prejudice -- it was a political program.

What are Pogroms?

Pogroms (from the Russian word meaning "to wreak havoc") were organized, often state-sanctioned, violent attacks against Jewish communities. Typically involving mobs looting and burning Jewish homes and businesses, beating, raping, and killing Jewish residents -- often while police stood by or participated. Pogroms were a recurring feature of Jewish life in the Russian Empire from the 1880s onward, and they drove the greatest Jewish migration in history.

Russian Pogroms: Organized Violence

In the Russian Empire, antisemitism turned violent on a massive scale. In March 1881, Tsar Alexander II was assassinated by revolutionaries. Although only one of the conspirators was Jewish, rumors spread that "the Jews" had killed the Tsar. What followed was a wave of pogroms -- organized anti-Jewish violence -- that swept through southern Russia and Ukraine in 1881-1884. Jewish homes were looted and burned. Jews were beaten, raped, and murdered in the streets. Local police often stood by or actively participated. The government passed the notorious May Laws of 1882, further restricting where Jews could live and work.

A second, even more brutal wave of pogroms erupted in 1903-1906. The Kishinev pogrom of 1903 (in present-day Chisinau, Moldova) shocked the world: 49 Jews killed, hundreds injured, homes and businesses destroyed, women raped -- while police looked on for two days before intervening. The poet Hayim Nahman Bialik, who visited Kishinev afterward, wrote "In the City of Slaughter," one of the most devastating poems in Hebrew literature, excoriating not just the perpetrators but also the Jewish victims for their passivity.

The pogroms triggered the greatest Jewish migration in history. Between 1881 and 1914, approximately 2 million Jews fled the Russian Empire. The vast majority went to the United States, transforming American Jewish life. But a small, ideologically driven minority -- perhaps 35,000 in the First Aliyah (1882-1903) and another 40,000 in the Second Aliyah (1904-1914) -- went to Ottoman Palestine.

What is the Dreyfus Affair?

The Dreyfus Affair (1894-1906) was a political scandal in France in which Captain Alfred Dreyfus, a Jewish officer in the French Army, was falsely convicted of treason (spying for Germany). Despite clear evidence of his innocence and the guilt of the real traitor, the Army and the French establishment covered up the truth, driven largely by antisemitism. The affair split France in two and shattered the belief that Jewish assimilation into European society could overcome deep-rooted hatred -- directly inspiring the birth of political Zionism.

The Dreyfus Affair: Enlightenment's Broken Promise

Captain Alfred Dreyfus (1859-1935), whose wrongful conviction for treason exposed the depth of antisemitism in "enlightened" France.

If the Russian pogroms showed what happened when the Enlightenment never arrived, the Dreyfus Affair showed something more disturbing: antisemitism could thrive even where it had. In 1894, Captain Alfred Dreyfus, a Jewish officer on the French Army's General Staff, was accused of passing military secrets to Germany. He was court-martialed, convicted, and sentenced to life imprisonment on Devil's Island, a penal colony off the coast of South America. The evidence against him was thin, and it soon emerged that the real traitor was Major Ferdinand Walsin Esterhazy, a non-Jewish officer with gambling debts.

But the Army refused to admit its error. When Colonel Georges Picquart, the Army's new head of intelligence, discovered evidence pointing to Esterhazy, he was transferred to a remote posting and eventually arrested. Esterhazy was tried and acquitted in just three minutes. The Army forged additional evidence against Dreyfus to shore up the conviction. France split into two camps: Dreyfusards (liberals, republicans, intellectuals -- including the novelist Emile Zola, whose open letter "J'Accuse...!" exposed the cover-up) and anti-Dreyfusards (the Army, the Church, monarchists, and antisemites). Mobs in Paris and other cities shouted "Death to Jews!" The affair dragged on for over a decade before Dreyfus was finally exonerated in 1906.

The Dreyfus Affair mattered beyond France because it demonstrated that even in the country that had first emancipated its Jews, even for a man who had done everything "right" -- served his country, risen through the ranks, been thoroughly assimilated -- Jewishness was an indelible mark. If antisemitism could flourish in Paris, the capital of the Enlightenment, then the Enlightenment's promise to Jews was a lie.

The Dreyfus Affair inspired Emile Zola's open letter "J'Accuse...!", published on the front page of the newspaper L'Aurore on January 13, 1898. It sold 300,000 copies in a single day and is considered one of the most impactful newspaper articles ever written. The affair also directly inspired Theodor Herzl's turn to Zionism -- one scandal, two world-changing consequences.

What is Zionism?

Zionism is the political movement advocating for the establishment of a Jewish homeland -- and later, the continued existence of -- a Jewish state in the historic Land of Israel (known in Hebrew as Eretz Yisrael). While religious longing for Zion (Jerusalem) had existed for millennia in Jewish prayer and tradition, political Zionism emerged in the late 19th century as a secular, nationalist response to persistent antisemitism. Its core argument: since Jews would never be fully safe as minorities in other nations, they needed a sovereign state of their own.

Theodor Herzl and the Birth of Political Zionism

Theodor Herzl (1860-1904), the father of political Zionism, who concluded that Jews would never be safe as minorities in Europe.

Theodor Herzl was an unlikely revolutionary. Born in Budapest in 1860, raised in Vienna, he was a thoroughly assimilated, German-speaking Jewish journalist and playwright who had little connection to Jewish religious life or to the Hebrew language. He was, by his own description, a "European" who happened to be Jewish.

In 1891-1895, Herzl served as the Paris correspondent for the Viennese newspaper Neue Freie Presse. He covered the Dreyfus trial and witnessed firsthand the anti-Jewish mobs in the streets of Paris. The experience shattered his faith in assimilation. If the most civilized country in Europe could erupt in antisemitic fury over a trumped-up charge against one Jewish officer, then no amount of assimilation would ever make Jews safe. His conclusion was stark: Jews were not merely a religion; they were a people. And as a people, they needed a state of their own.

In 1896, Herzl published "Der Judenstaat" ("The Jewish State"), a slim book that became the founding document of political Zionism. His argument was coolly practical, not religious or mystical. Antisemitism was not a prejudice that could be educated away; it was a permanent feature of any society where Jews lived as a minority. The only solution was a sovereign Jewish state where Jews could live as the majority and control their own destiny. Herzl did not specify Palestine -- he considered Argentina and later East Africa (the "Uganda Scheme") as alternatives -- but the overwhelming weight of Jewish historical and emotional attachment to the Land of Israel made Palestine the eventual choice.

Herzl seriously considered Argentina and British East Africa (Uganda) as possible Jewish homelands before settling on Palestine. The "Uganda Scheme" was actually put to a vote at the Sixth Zionist Congress in 1903 and nearly passed -- it was defeated only because Eastern European delegates threatened to walk out. The Jewish state could have been in Africa.

The First Zionist Congress (Basel, 1897)

The First Zionist Congress in Basel, Switzerland (1897) -- 208 delegates from 17 countries.

In August 1897, Herzl convened the First Zionist Congress in Basel, Switzerland. Two hundred and eight delegates from 17 countries attended. The Congress adopted the Basel Program, which called for "establishing for the Jewish people a publicly and legally assured home in Palestine." It created the World Zionist Organization, with Herzl as president, and established institutions -- a bank (the Jewish Colonial Trust), a newspaper, and a diplomatic apparatus -- to make the dream operational.

After the Congress, Herzl wrote in his diary what became one of the most famous predictions in modern history: "At Basel, I founded the Jewish State. If I said this out loud today, I would be answered by universal laughter. In five years perhaps, and certainly in fifty, everyone will perceive it." He was almost exactly right. Israel was founded in 1948, 51 years later. Herzl himself did not live to see it -- he died in 1904, exhausted by his diplomatic efforts, at the age of 44.

What is the Diaspora?

Diaspora (from the Greek for "scattering") refers to the communities of Jews living outside the Land of Israel. After the Roman destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE and the Bar Kokhba revolt in 135 CE, the majority of Jews lived in dispersion -- across Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. The term carries a sense of exile and longing for return, which became central to Jewish identity and prayer for nearly two thousand years.

What is a Kibbutz?

A kibbutz (plural: kibbutzim) is a collective agricultural community in Israel where members share property, labor, and resources. Founded by socialist Zionist immigrants in the early 20th century, kibbutzim were idealistic experiments in communal living -- members ate together, raised children collectively, and made decisions democratically. They played a disproportionate role in building Israel's economy, military, and political leadership.

The Aliyot: Jewish Immigration to Palestine

Even before Herzl, Jews had begun returning to Palestine. The First Aliyah (1882-1903) brought approximately 35,000 Jews, mostly from Russia, fleeing the pogroms. They were idealistic but practically unprepared. Many settled in agricultural colonies funded by Baron Edmond de Rothschild, growing grapes and citrus. The early settlements struggled with malaria, poor soil, and lack of farming experience. Many settlers left. But a core remained, establishing communities like Rishon LeZion, Petah Tikva, and Zikhron Ya'akov.

The Second Aliyah (1904-1914) was different in character. Its approximately 40,000 immigrants were more ideologically driven -- many were socialists who saw the return to Palestine as not just a national project but a social revolution. They would build a new kind of Jewish society: egalitarian, agricultural, rooted in physical labor rather than the commerce and scholarship of the diaspora. They founded the kibbutz movement -- collective agricultural communities where all property was shared. They established the first Jewish self-defense organizations (the Hashomer). And they revived Hebrew as a spoken, everyday language, largely through the tireless efforts of Eliezer Ben-Yehuda (1858-1922), who compiled the first modern Hebrew dictionary and insisted on speaking only Hebrew, even at home. In 1909, they founded Tel Aviv -- initially a small suburb north of the ancient port city of Jaffa, destined to become a major city.

But Palestine was not empty. By 1914, Ottoman Palestine had approximately 600,000-700,000 Arab inhabitants -- Muslim and Christian -- who had lived there for generations. The Arab population noticed the increasing Jewish immigration and land purchases, and the first frictions appeared. In 1891, a group of Arab notables in Jerusalem petitioned the Ottoman government to prohibit Jewish immigration and land purchases. Local tensions between Arab farmers and Jewish settlers over land and labor simmered. These early frictions were small, but they were the first tremors of a conflict that would come to define the 20th century.
