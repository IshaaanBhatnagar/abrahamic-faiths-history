Chapter 4

Rise of Islam

600 -- 750 CE

In the span of barely a century, a new monotheistic faith (a religion worshipping one God) emerged from the deserts of Arabia and reshaped the political, religious, and cultural landscape of the known world. From the first revelations in a mountain cave to the vast Umayyad Caliphate (the first great Islamic dynasty-state) stretching from Spain to the Indus, the rise of Islam is one of history's most consequential transformations -- and the source of enduring connections and tensions among the Abrahamic faiths (Judaism, Christianity, and Islam -- the three religions that trace their spiritual lineage to the patriarch Abraham).

Who were the Quraysh?

The Quraysh were the dominant Arab tribe of Mecca at the time of Muhammad's birth. They controlled the Kaaba (the sacred shrine at the center of Mecca), managed the lucrative pilgrimage trade, and wielded enormous political and economic power across Arabia. Muhammad was born into the Banu Hashim, one of the Quraysh's sub-clans -- respected but not the wealthiest. The Quraysh's opposition to Muhammad's message would become one of the central conflicts of early Islam, and their eventual conversion would be a turning point in the religion's spread.

The Arabian Peninsula: Geography and Trade

The Arabian Peninsula in the sixth century was a vast, arid landmass bordered by the Red Sea to the west, the Persian Gulf to the east, and the Indian Ocean to the south. Most of its interior consisted of inhospitable desert -- the Rub' al-Khali (Empty Quarter) in the south and the Nafud Desert in the north -- punctuated by scattered oases that served as vital lifelines for nomadic Bedouin tribes (the Arabic-speaking nomadic peoples who herded camels, sheep, and goats across the desert) and small settled communities alike. Water was the most precious resource, and the wells and springs that dotted the landscape determined where people could live, trade, and worship.

Despite the harsh terrain, Arabia was far from isolated. It sat at the crossroads of two great empires: the Byzantine Empire (the eastern continuation of the Roman Empire, Greek-speaking and Christian, with its capital at Constantinople -- modern Istanbul) to the northwest, controlling the eastern Mediterranean, Egypt, and the Levant (the region along the eastern Mediterranean coast, including modern Syria, Lebanon, Israel, and Jordan); and the Sassanid (Persian) Empire to the northeast, ruling Mesopotamia, Iran, and parts of Central Asia. These two superpowers had been locked in centuries of warfare, and their rivalry created lucrative opportunities for Arabian merchants who served as intermediaries. Because neither empire could fully control the Arabian interior, the peninsula's trade routes remained independent -- which led directly to the commercial wealth that made cities like Mecca powerful enough to become the cradle of a new civilization. Overland trade routes carried frankincense and myrrh (precious aromatic resins used for incense, perfume, and religious rituals, worth their weight in gold in the ancient world) from southern Arabia (modern Yemen and Oman), spices and silks from India and China, and ivory and gold from East Africa through the Hejaz region (the western coastal strip of Arabia, home to Mecca and Medina) along the Red Sea coast, connecting the commerce of the Indian Ocean world to the Mediterranean.

The towns that controlled key points along these routes -- Mecca, Yathrib (later Medina), and Ta'if -- grew wealthy and influential far beyond what their modest populations would suggest. Caravans of hundreds of camels made seasonal journeys north to Syria and south to Yemen, and the merchants who organized these expeditions formed the commercial elite of Arabian society. It was into this world of desert, trade, and the meeting of empires that Islam would be born.

Mecca: The Commercial and Spiritual Hub

The Kaaba in Mecca -- ancient shrine believed by Muslims to have been originally built by Ibrahim (Abraham) and his son Ismail (Ishmael).

Mecca occupied a unique position in pre-Islamic Arabia as both a commercial crossroads and a sacred center. Located in a narrow, barren valley in the Hejaz region of western Arabia, it had no agriculture to speak of and depended entirely on trade and pilgrimage for its livelihood. At the heart of the city stood the Kaaba, a cube-shaped stone structure that Arab tradition held had been originally built by the prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) and his son Ismail (Ishmael) as a house of worship dedicated to the one God. The Zamzam well, located nearby, was associated with the story of Hagar (Hajar) and Ishmael: according to tradition, when Hagar was searching desperately for water for her infant son in the desert, God caused the spring to miraculously appear, saving their lives. The well was already considered sacred long before Muhammad's time.

By the sixth century, however, the Kaaba had become a pantheon of polytheistic worship (polytheism being the belief in and worship of multiple gods, as opposed to monotheism). According to Islamic sources, it housed approximately 360 idols representing various tribal deities. The most prominent among these were Hubal (a divination deity), and the three "daughters of Allah" -- al-Lat, al-Uzza, and Manat -- goddesses worshipped across the peninsula. Pilgrims from tribes throughout Arabia would converge on Mecca during sacred months when warfare was prohibited, performing rituals around the Kaaba and conducting trade at the great fairs that coincided with the pilgrimage season. This arrangement was enormously profitable for the Quraysh, the dominant tribe of Mecca, who served as custodians of the Kaaba and guardians of the pilgrimage. Because the Quraysh's wealth and power depended directly on the pilgrimage economy, any challenge to the idols in the Kaaba would be perceived not just as a religious affront but as an existential economic threat -- which explains why they responded to Muhammad's monotheistic message with such ferocity. Their control of the sanctuary gave them both religious authority and economic power, as they taxed and facilitated the commerce that pilgrimage season brought.

The Quraysh were organized into several clans of varying wealth and influence. Among the most prominent were the Banu Umayya (commercially powerful), the Banu Makhzum (militarily strong), and the Banu Hashim (guardians of the Zamzam well and the pilgrimage provisions). It was into the Banu Hashim -- respected but not the wealthiest clan -- that Muhammad would be born.

Jahiliyyah: The "Age of Ignorance"

Muslim tradition refers to the pre-Islamic period as the Jahiliyyah, or "Age of Ignorance" -- not ignorance in the sense of lacking knowledge or culture, but ignorance of divine guidance and monotheistic truth. This characterization captures the religious and moral landscape that Islam would seek to transform, though it is important to recognize that the Jahiliyyah era was also a time of remarkable cultural achievement in certain domains.

On the darker side, pre-Islamic Arabian society was marked by deep tribal divisions and perpetual cycles of blood feuds. Without any central political authority or shared legal system, justice was administered through tribal custom and the principle of collective retribution -- if a member of one tribe was killed, the victim's entire tribe was honor-bound to avenge the death. This triggered generations of warfare, because each act of retribution was itself a new offense demanding further vengeance, creating cycles of violence that could last decades. The practice of female infanticide (wa'd) existed among some tribes, where newborn girls were buried alive, seen as economic burdens and sources of potential shame. Social stratification was severe: slavery was widespread, and the status of women, while varying by tribe, was generally subordinate, with practices like unlimited polygamy and the inheritance of widows as property being common.

Yet the Jahiliyyah was also an era of extraordinary oral poetry -- the Mu'allaqat (the "Suspended Odes") are among the masterpieces of world literature, celebrated for their vivid imagery, complex meter, and emotional depth. The Bedouin code of honor (muruwwa) prized generosity, courage, loyalty to one's tribe, and the protection of the weak and the guest. Hospitality was sacred: a traveler who arrived at any tent in the desert could expect food, shelter, and protection for three days, no questions asked. These positive values -- generosity, hospitality, poetic eloquence, and tribal solidarity -- would be retained and redirected by Islam, while the negative practices -- idolatry, blood feuds, infanticide, and slavery -- would be challenged and progressively reformed.

The Religious Landscape of Sixth-Century Arabia

While polytheism dominated Mecca and much of the peninsula, Arabia was far from religiously homogeneous. The peninsula was home to a surprisingly diverse religious ecosystem that would profoundly shape the emergence of Islam. Alongside the polytheistic majority, several monotheistic and quasi-monotheistic traditions had established deep roots in different regions.

Jewish communities had a long and significant presence in Arabia, particularly in Yathrib (later Medina), where three major Jewish tribes -- the Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza -- lived alongside Arab tribes. These Jewish communities were Arabic-speaking, culturally Arab in many respects, but maintained their religious identity, synagogues, and Torah scholarship. They were farmers, merchants, and goldsmiths, and their presence meant that monotheistic ideas, biblical narratives, and concepts like prophecy and scripture were familiar to the Arabs of Yathrib. Jewish communities also existed in Khaybar, Wadi al-Qura, and parts of Yemen.

Christianity had likewise penetrated the peninsula from multiple directions. The kingdom of Aksum (Ethiopia) across the Red Sea was Christian, and the Aksumite invasion and brief occupation of Yemen in the sixth century brought Christianity directly to southern Arabia. The Ghassanids, an Arab tribal confederation on the Byzantine frontier in Syria, were Monophysite Christians (a branch that emphasized Christ's single divine nature, in contrast to the mainstream view that he had both a divine and a human nature) and served as a buffer state for Constantinople. The Lakhmids, their counterparts on the Sassanid frontier in Iraq, included Nestorian Christians (another distinct branch that emphasized the separation between Christ's divine and human natures) among their ranks. Christian monks and hermits were known to inhabit caves and monasteries in the Hejaz, and Christian ideas about Jesus, Mary, the Trinity (the Christian doctrine that God exists as three persons -- Father, Son, and Holy Spirit -- in one being), and the Last Judgment circulated through trade and pilgrimage contacts.

"Allah" is not a uniquely Islamic word -- Arabic-speaking Christians and Jews also use "Allah" to mean God, and have done so for centuries. The Arabic Bible uses "Allah" throughout. The word is linguistically related to the Hebrew "Elohim" and the Aramaic "Elaha" -- the same Aramaic language Jesus himself spoke. All three Abrahamic faiths in the Arabic-speaking world share the same name for the divine.

Perhaps most intriguing were the Hanifs -- individuals who rejected both polytheism and the organized monotheisms available to them, instead seeking a pure Abrahamic monotheism. Islamic tradition names several of these seekers, including Waraqa ibn Nawfal (Khadijah's cousin, who had become Christian), Zayd ibn Amr, and Uthman ibn al-Huwayrith. They believed that the original monotheism of Ibrahim had been corrupted by idol worship and sought to recover it. Their existence suggests that the spiritual ground of Arabia was already prepared, in some measure, for the monotheistic message that Muhammad would bring. As a result of this diverse religious environment, concepts like prophecy, scripture, and divine judgment were not foreign to the Arabs of the Hejaz -- which meant Muhammad's message, while revolutionary, fell on soil that had already been tilled by centuries of monotheistic contact.

c. 570 CE

Early Life: An Orphan in Mecca (c. 570-595 CE)

Muhammad ibn Abdullah was born around 570 CE in Mecca into the Banu Hashim clan of the Quraysh tribe. His early life was marked by profound loss and hardship. His father, Abdullah, died during a trading journey before Muhammad was even born, leaving the unborn child without a father's protection -- a serious vulnerability in tribal society. Following Arabian custom, the infant Muhammad was sent to be nursed by a Bedouin woman named Halimah al-Sa'diyah, spending his earliest years in the desert, learning the pure Arabic of the nomads and absorbing the values of Bedouin life.

When Muhammad was about six years old, his mother Amina bint Wahb died during a journey between Medina and Mecca, leaving him a complete orphan. He was taken in by his grandfather, Abdul Muttalib, the respected chief of the Banu Hashim and custodian of the Zamzam well, who treated the boy with great tenderness. But Abdul Muttalib himself died just two years later, when Muhammad was eight. The orphan then passed into the care of his uncle Abu Talib, who loved him dearly but was a man of modest means with many children of his own. This childhood of successive losses -- father, mother, grandfather -- left an indelible mark on Muhammad. Because he had experienced firsthand the vulnerability of being without a protector in tribal society, the consequence was a lifelong identification with the marginalized -- reflected directly in the Quran's repeated emphasis on caring for orphans, widows, and the vulnerable.

As a young man, Muhammad earned his living in the caravan trade, accompanying his uncle Abu Talib on journeys to Syria. He quickly developed a reputation for exceptional honesty and integrity, earning the nickname "Al-Amin" (the Trustworthy) and "Al-Sadiq" (the Truthful). In a society where commercial disputes were rampant and trust was scarce, these qualities made him highly valued. He was known as a contemplative, serious-minded man who avoided the drinking, gambling, and idol worship common among his peers. This childhood of loss and this reputation for integrity would prove crucial, because they shaped both the moral authority he carried and the deep empathy for the vulnerable that defined his later message.

c. 595 CE

Marriage to Khadijah (c. 595 CE)

Muhammad's reputation for honesty brought him to the attention of Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, one of Mecca's most successful merchants. Khadijah was a wealthy widow, about fifteen years Muhammad's senior, who managed extensive trading operations. She hired the 25-year-old Muhammad to lead one of her caravans to Syria, and was so impressed by both his commercial success and his exceptional character that she proposed marriage to him -- a striking act of initiative in a patriarchal society, and one that speaks to both her independence and her high regard for him. Khadijah was no passive figure: she was one of the most prominent businesswomen in Mecca, running her own commercial empire, and her decision to propose was entirely her own. Muhammad accepted, and their marriage around 595 CE proved to be one of history's great partnerships.

Khadijah was not merely Muhammad's wife but his closest confidant, strongest supporter, and the person whose faith in him never wavered. They had a deeply loving and respectful relationship. Together they had several children, including their daughters Zaynab, Ruqayya, Umm Kulthum, and Fatimah (who would play a crucial role in Islamic history as the wife of Ali and mother of Hasan and Husayn). For the twenty-five years of their marriage until Khadijah's death, Muhammad took no other wife -- a remarkable fact in a society where polygamy was the norm among men of status. Even years later, after Khadijah's death, Muhammad would speak of her with such love and reverence that it occasionally made his younger wife Aisha jealous.

c. 610 CE

The First Revelation: Cave of Hira (c. 610 CE)

The Cave of Hira on Jabal al-Nur (Mountain of Light), where Muhammad received the first Quranic revelation.

As Muhammad approached the age of forty, he began to experience a growing spiritual restlessness. He would retreat for days at a time to a small cave called Hira, on Jabal al-Nur (the Mountain of Light) overlooking Mecca, to meditate in solitude. He was troubled by the moral corruption he saw around him -- the idolatry, the exploitation of the poor, the arrogance of the wealthy, the burial of infant daughters -- and he sought answers in contemplation and prayer.

It was during one of these retreats, in the month of Ramadan around 610 CE, that Muhammad had the experience that would change the course of human history. According to the traditional accounts, the angel Jibril (Gabriel) appeared to him and commanded: "Iqra!" -- "Read!" or "Recite!" Muhammad, who was illiterate, responded in terror: "I cannot read." The angel seized him in a crushing embrace and repeated the command twice more. Then came the first words of divine revelation: "Recite in the name of your Lord who created -- created man from a clinging substance. Recite, and your Lord is the Most Generous -- who taught by the pen -- taught man that which he knew not" (Quran 96:1-5, Surah Al-Alaq).

Muhammad came down from the mountain trembling and terrified. He was not elated or triumphant -- he was shaken to his core, unsure whether he had encountered a divine being or a malevolent spirit. He rushed home to Khadijah, wrapped himself in a cloak, and cried out: "Cover me! Cover me!" (Zammiluni, zammiluni). It was Khadijah who steadied him. She held him close and spoke words that Muslims remember to this day: "Never! By God, God will never disgrace you. You keep good relations with your kin, you bear the burden of the weak, you help the poor, you entertain guests, and you endure hardships in the path of truth." She then took him to her learned cousin, Waraqa ibn Nawfal, an elderly Christian scholar who had studied the Hebrew scriptures. Waraqa listened to Muhammad's account and declared: "This is the same Namus (the angel of revelation) that God sent to Moses. I wish I were young and could live to see the day when your people will turn you out." When Muhammad asked in alarm, "Will they drive me out?" Waraqa replied: "Anyone who came with something similar to what you have brought was treated with hostility."

c. 610-613 CE

The Message: Core Teachings of Early Revelations

After a brief pause in revelations (the fatra, which caused Muhammad great anxiety, fearing he had been abandoned by God), the revelations resumed with Surah Al-Muddathir (74): "O you who covers himself with a garment, arise and warn! And your Lord glorify, and your clothing purify, and uncleanliness avoid." The core message that emerged in these early Meccan revelations was strikingly simple and radically challenging:

There is one God (Allah) -- the same God of Abraham, Moses, and Jesus -- and He alone deserves worship. All the idols in the Kaaba and throughout Arabia are false. Muhammad is God's messenger, the last in a long line of prophets. A Day of Judgment is coming when every person will answer for their deeds. The wealthy must share with the poor. Orphans must be protected. The arrogant will be humbled. Social justice is not optional -- it is a core demand of the divine. Because this message directly challenged the Quraysh's economic model and social hierarchy, it triggered fierce opposition from the Meccan elite. These themes dominated the early surahs (the individual chapters of the Quran -- there are 114 in total, arranged roughly from longest to shortest): warnings to the wealthy who hoard their riches, demands for justice for the oppressed, vivid descriptions of paradise and hellfire, and the insistent refrain that there is no god but God.

c. 613 CE

The First Muslims

Muhammad initially shared the revelations only with his closest circle. The first person to accept his message was his wife Khadijah, making her the first Muslim — a fact of enormous significance: the entire religion of Islam begins with a woman's act of faith. Her immediate, unwavering belief was crucial, because had she doubted or rejected him, the movement might have died in its infancy. Next came Ali ibn Abi Talib, Muhammad's young cousin who lived in his household, approximately ten years old at the time. Then Abu Bakr, Muhammad's closest friend and a respected, wealthy merchant, accepted Islam. Abu Bakr's conversion was particularly significant because his social standing gave the nascent movement credibility, and he immediately began using his influence and wealth to bring others into the fold. The consequence was that Islam gained its first foothold among Mecca's respectable merchant class, which led directly to a wave of early conversions among people who trusted Abu Bakr's judgment. Zayd ibn Harithah, a former slave whom Muhammad had freed and adopted as a son, also embraced Islam early.

Among the most powerful early conversion stories is that of Bilal ibn Rabah, an Abyssinian (Ethiopian) slave owned by Umayya ibn Khalaf, a fierce opponent of Islam. When Bilal accepted Islam, his master subjected him to horrific torture: he would be dragged out into the scorching midday sun, laid on his back on the burning sand, and have heavy rocks and boulders placed on his chest while being commanded to renounce Islam and praise the idol gods. Bilal's only response, gasping under the crushing weight, was to repeat: "Ahad, Ahad" -- "One, One" (meaning the One God). Abu Bakr, horrified by this cruelty, purchased Bilal's freedom at a steep price. Bilal would later become the first muezzin (the person who calls Muslims to prayer) in Islam -- chosen by Muhammad to climb to a high point and call the faithful to prayer with the adhan (the melodic call to prayer that rings out five times daily from mosques across the Muslim world). His story became a powerful symbol of Islam's message of equality: a freed African slave was given one of the most honored roles in the community, his voice the one that summoned all Muslims -- Arab and non-Arab, rich and poor -- to stand before God as equals.

c. 613-619 CE

Persecution in Mecca

As Muhammad began to preach publicly, the Quraysh leadership grew increasingly hostile. Their opposition was not merely theological -- it was economic and political. If the idols were false, then the pilgrimage trade that enriched Mecca was built on a lie. If all people were equal before God, then the rigid tribal hierarchy that privileged the Quraysh elite was illegitimate. If the dead would be resurrected and judged, then the wealthy who exploited the poor would face consequences. Muhammad's message threatened every pillar of the existing order. This is precisely why the Quraysh persecution was so intense -- it was not merely a religious disagreement but a struggle for survival of their entire social and economic system.

The persecution began with mockery and social pressure but quickly escalated. Followers of Muhammad -- especially those without powerful tribal protectors, including slaves, freedmen, women, and members of weaker clans -- were subjected to beatings, imprisonment, economic boycott, and even killing. Some were tortured with hot irons. Yasir, Sumayyah, and their son Ammar were singled out for particular brutality; Sumayyah bint Khayyat is traditionally regarded as the first martyr of Islam, killed by Abu Jahl with a spear. A group of vulnerable Muslims was sent by Muhammad to seek refuge across the Red Sea in the Christian kingdom of Aksum (Ethiopia), where the just king, known as the Negus (the title for Ethiopian rulers, called al-Najashi in Arabic), offered them protection -- an early and significant moment of interfaith solidarity.

The Quraysh eventually imposed a complete boycott on the entire Banu Hashim clan -- not just the Muslims among them, but the whole clan, including those who had not accepted Islam but refused to hand Muhammad over. For approximately three years (c. 616-619 CE), the Banu Hashim were confined to a narrow valley, cut off from trade, food supplies, and social contact. The suffering was severe; accounts describe people eating leaves and leather to survive. The boycott was eventually lifted when some Quraysh members, disturbed by the cruelty of it, broke the pact.

c. 619 CE

The Year of Sorrow (c. 619 CE)

Shortly after the boycott ended, Muhammad suffered the most devastating personal losses of his life in rapid succession. Around 619 CE, his beloved wife Khadijah died. She had been his rock for twenty-five years -- the first to believe in him, the one who comforted him when he was terrified, who spent her wealth supporting the Muslim community, who never wavered in her faith or her love. Her death left a void that Muhammad mourned for the rest of his life.

Within weeks or months, his uncle Abu Talib also died. Though Abu Talib never formally accepted Islam, he had used his authority as clan chief to protect Muhammad from the Quraysh. With Abu Talib gone, the new clan leader, Abu Lahab (Muhammad's other uncle and a bitter opponent), withdrew the clan's protection. This resulted in Muhammad becoming physically vulnerable to his enemies for the first time, which in turn triggered his desperate search for a new base of support outside Mecca. The Muslims called this period the "Year of Sorrow" (Aam al-Huzn). Muhammad, grieving and unprotected, tried to find support outside Mecca, traveling to the nearby city of Ta'if, but was met with rejection and violence -- the people of Ta'if drove him out by setting children and slaves to pelt him with stones until he bled. It was the lowest point of his prophetic career.

c. 620 CE

The Night Journey and Ascension (Isra and Mi'raj)

In this darkest period, Islamic tradition records that Muhammad was granted one of the most extraordinary experiences in religious history: the Isra and Mi'raj, the Night Journey and Heavenly Ascension. According to the accounts, the angel Jibril came to Muhammad one night and brought him a celestial steed called the Buraq. Muhammad was transported from the Sacred Mosque in Mecca (al-Masjid al-Haram) to the "Farthest Mosque" (al-Masjid al-Aqsa) in Jerusalem. There, on the Temple Mount -- the same site where Solomon's Temple (the first Jewish Temple, built by King Solomon around 957 BCE as the center of Israelite worship) had stood, where Jesus had taught -- Muhammad led all the previous prophets in prayer, a moment of profound symbolic significance: the final prophet standing in the holy place of his predecessors, leading Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and all the prophets in worship of the one God.

From Jerusalem, Muhammad then ascended through the seven heavens, meeting various prophets at each level -- Adam in the first heaven, Jesus and John the Baptist (called Yahya in Arabic -- a Jewish prophet who, according to the Gospels, baptized Jesus and prepared the way for his ministry) in the second, Joseph (Yusuf) in the third, Idris (identified with the biblical Enoch, an ancient patriarch described as so righteous that God "took him" directly to heaven) in the fourth, Aaron (Harun) in the fifth, Moses (Musa) in the sixth, and Abraham (Ibrahim) in the seventh. At the highest point, Muhammad entered the divine presence and received the command for the five daily prayers. According to the tradition, the original command was for fifty daily prayers, but on his descent, Moses urged Muhammad to go back and ask for a reduction, saying, "I have tested people before you, and they were too weak for that." Muhammad went back and forth between God and Moses until the number was reduced to five, with God declaring that the reward would still be that of fifty.

The Isra and Mi'raj is the reason Jerusalem holds the status of the third holiest city in Islam, after Mecca and Medina. The Dome of the Rock (a golden-domed Islamic shrine completed in 691 CE, one of the most recognizable buildings in the world), built later on the Temple Mount, marks the traditional site of Muhammad's ascension. This event also embeds within Islamic theology a deep connection to the earlier Abrahamic prophets -- Muhammad's journey was not a break from the past but a culmination of the entire prophetic tradition. As a result of the Night Journey, Jerusalem became permanently sacred in Islam, which led directly to the construction of the Dome of the Rock and al-Aqsa Mosque on the Temple Mount -- a decision that would shape the politics of the Holy Land for the next fourteen centuries.

Medina and the Battles

  • The Battle of Badr (624 CE) is Islam's defining military victory: 313 Muslims defeat a Quraysh force of ~1,000, which the Quran attributes to divine intervention through angels

What is the Hijra?

The Hijra (also spelled Hegira) means "migration" or "emigration" in Arabic. It refers specifically to Muhammad's journey from Mecca to Medina in 622 CE, when persecution by the Quraysh made life in Mecca untenable for Muslims. The Hijra is so important that it marks Year 1 of the Islamic calendar -- not Muhammad's birth or the first revelation, but the moment the Muslim community gained political sovereignty and the ability to practice their faith freely. Islamic calendar dates are designated "AH" (Anno Hegirae, "Year of the Hijra").

622 CE

The Hijra: Migration to Medina (622 CE)

Masjid al-Nabawi (the Prophet's Mosque) in Medina, originally built by Muhammad and his companions after the Hijra.

While Mecca grew increasingly hostile, an unexpected opportunity arose from Yathrib (later called Medina, meaning "the City" -- short for Madinat al-Nabi, "City of the Prophet"), a settlement approximately 200 miles north of Mecca. Yathrib was a fractured community: two major Arab tribes, the Aws and Khazraj, had been locked in a bitter civil war for generations, and three Jewish tribes (Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza) added to the complex political landscape. It was precisely because Yathrib was so divided that its people were willing to invite an outsider as arbitrator -- their own internal conflicts had made them desperate for a neutral authority who could bring peace. Representatives from Yathrib, meeting Muhammad during the annual pilgrimage season at Mecca, were deeply impressed by his message and character. In two successive pledges -- the First and Second Pledges of Aqaba (621-622 CE) -- delegations from Yathrib invited Muhammad to come to their city as an arbitrator and leader, promising to protect him as they would protect their own families.

Muhammad began sending his followers to Yathrib in small groups to avoid attracting Quraysh attention. When the Quraysh discovered the plan, they plotted to assassinate Muhammad, selecting young men from every clan so that the blame (and the blood price) would be distributed and the Banu Hashim could not retaliate against any single clan. On the night of the planned assassination, according to tradition, Muhammad asked his cousin Ali to sleep in his bed as a decoy and slipped past the assassins undetected.

Muhammad and his closest companion Abu Bakr fled south to the Cave of Thawr, in the opposite direction from Medina, to throw off their pursuers. The Quraysh sent trackers with a bounty of 100 camels -- an enormous sum. According to the beloved traditional account, when the pursuers reached the very mouth of the cave, they found a spider's web covering the entrance and a dove nesting with eggs just inside -- evidence, they concluded, that no one had recently entered. Abu Bakr was terrified, whispering, "If any of them looks down at his feet, he will see us." Muhammad replied calmly: "What do you think of two when God is their third?" After hiding for three days, they took a circuitous desert route and arrived safely in Medina on September 24, 622 CE. This migration -- the Hijra -- was so momentous that it marks Year 1 of the Islamic calendar. It represents not just a physical journey but a civilizational turning point: the transformation of Islam from a persecuted faith into a community with political sovereignty.

622 CE

The Constitution of Medina

One of Muhammad's first acts in Medina was the drafting of a remarkable document known as the Constitution of Medina (Sahifat al-Madinah), sometimes called the Charter of Medina. This was a formal agreement among the diverse groups living in the city -- the Muslim emigrants from Mecca (Muhajirun), the Muslim converts of Medina (Ansar, meaning "Helpers"), the Jewish tribes, and other non-Muslim groups. It established a political framework that was revolutionary for its time and context.

The Constitution declared all the signatories to be one community (ummah -- an Arabic term for a unified community of people bound together by shared purpose, which would become a central concept in Islam referring to the global Muslim community). It guaranteed freedom of religious practice for all groups. It established a system of mutual defense: if Medina was attacked, all groups -- Muslim, Jewish, and pagan -- would defend the city together. It designated Muhammad as the final arbiter of disputes between the groups. It abolished the old tribal blood-feud system within Medina and replaced it with a community-based justice system. Each religious group retained its own laws for internal matters. The document has been studied by modern scholars as one of the earliest examples of a pluralistic social contract and constitutional governance, predating the Magna Carta by almost six centuries.

Muhammad also established the bonds of brotherhood (mu'akhat) between the Muhajirun and the Ansar: each Meccan emigrant was paired with a Medinan Muslim who would share their home, property, and livelihood with their new brother or sister in faith. This was an extraordinary social experiment -- it replaced tribal identity with religious solidarity as the basis of social obligation, and it ensured that the destitute refugees from Mecca could survive and rebuild their lives.

622 CE

Building the Prophet's Mosque

Shortly after arriving in Medina, Muhammad purchased a plot of land where his camel had stopped (seeing this as a divine sign) and began building what would become Masjid al-Nabawi -- the Prophet's Mosque. The construction was remarkable for its simplicity and the egalitarianism it embodied: Muhammad himself carried bricks and worked alongside his companions, who included former slaves, wealthy merchants, and tribal chiefs all laboring side by side. The original mosque was a modest structure of mud brick walls, palm trunk pillars, and a roof of palm leaves, with an open courtyard. It served not only as a place of prayer but as the administrative center of the new community, a court of justice, a school, a shelter for the homeless, and a meeting hall. The simplicity was the point: this was a community defined not by grandeur but by purpose.

624 CE

Battle of Badr (624 CE)

The first major military confrontation between the Muslims and the Quraysh came in March 624 CE at the wells of Badr, a location along the coastal trade route between Mecca and Syria. The immediate cause was a Muslim attempt to intercept a large Meccan caravan led by Abu Sufyan. The caravan escaped, but the Quraysh mobilized a large army of approximately 1,000 well-equipped warriors -- many on horseback, with full armor and weapons -- and marched to confront the Muslims. Muhammad's force numbered just 313 men, poorly armed, with only two horses and seventy camels among them.

By any rational military calculation, the Muslims should have been annihilated. Instead, they won a decisive and stunning victory. Muhammad positioned his forces strategically near the wells, controlling the water supply. The battle began with traditional single combat between champions, in which the Muslim fighters prevailed. The general engagement that followed saw the Meccan ranks break and scatter. Approximately seventy Meccans were killed and seventy taken prisoner, while Muslim losses were just fourteen. The Quran would describe God sending angels to fight alongside the believers (Quran 8:9), and Badr became the defining moment of early Islam -- proof, to the believers, that God was with them even against impossible odds.

The treatment of prisoners after Badr was notable and progressive for its era. Rather than executing them (as was customary) or holding them for extortionate ransoms, Muhammad allowed literate prisoners to earn their freedom by teaching ten Muslim children to read and write. This early linkage of mercy, education, and warfare was characteristic of Muhammad's approach and left a lasting mark on Islamic civilization's reverence for learning.

The concept of ransoming prisoners through education at Badr has no known parallel in 7th-century warfare -- anywhere in the world. In an era when captured enemies were routinely enslaved or killed, making literacy the price of freedom reflected a value system that placed knowledge on the same plane as gold. This ethos would later drive the Islamic Golden Age, when Muslim scholars preserved and translated the works of Aristotle (the ancient Greek philosopher), Galen (the Greek physician whose medical theories dominated for centuries), and Ptolemy (the Greek astronomer and geographer) -- texts that had been lost to medieval Europe and were eventually reintroduced through Arabic translations.

625 CE

Battle of Uhud (625 CE)

The Quraysh, humiliated by their defeat at Badr, were determined to restore their prestige. Because the loss at Badr had shaken their reputation across Arabia, the consequence was a massive military buildup -- they spent a full year preparing for revenge. In March 625 CE, Abu Sufyan (the most powerful leader of the Quraysh and Muhammad's chief adversary, who would later convert to Islam) led an army of 3,000 men toward Medina. Muhammad marched out with about 1,000 Muslims to meet them near Mount Uhud, north of Medina, though 300 men under Abdullah ibn Ubayy (a Medinan leader whom the Quran labels a "munafiq" or hypocrite -- someone who professed Islam outwardly but secretly worked to undermine the Muslim community) deserted before the battle, reducing the Muslim force to 700.

Muhammad deployed a contingent of fifty archers on a strategic hillside with strict orders not to leave their position under any circumstances. The battle initially went well for the Muslims, and the Meccan lines began to collapse. But when the Meccan forces appeared to be in full retreat, most of the archers abandoned their post to pursue the fleeing enemy and collect spoils -- directly disobeying Muhammad's command. The brilliant Meccan cavalry commander Khalid ibn al-Walid (who would later convert to Islam and become one of history's greatest generals) saw the opening, led his horsemen around the exposed flank, and attacked the Muslims from behind.

The result was chaos. Muhammad himself was injured -- a blow to his face broke a tooth, cut his lip, and drove the rings of his helmet into his cheek. Rumors of his death spread across the battlefield, causing panic. The Muslims rallied when they found Muhammad alive, but the damage was done. The battle was a partial Quraysh victory, though they chose not to press their advantage and march on Medina itself. For the Muslim community, Uhud was a painful lesson in discipline, obedience, and the consequences of disobeying the Prophet's commands. The Quran addressed the defeat directly, framing it as a test from God and a reminder that victory comes only through faith and obedience, not through individual ambition.

627 CE

Battle of the Trench / Khandaq (627 CE)

In 627 CE, the Quraysh assembled the largest military force yet seen in the Hejaz: a grand coalition of approximately 10,000 warriors drawn from the Quraysh, their allied tribes, and even some formerly neutral groups. Their objective was simple -- to overwhelm Medina, destroy the Muslim community, and kill Muhammad once and for all. It seemed like an unstoppable force.

Faced with this existential threat, Muhammad convened a council of war. It was Salman al-Farisi (Salman the Persian), a former Zoroastrian and Christian who had traveled across the Middle East seeking truth before embracing Islam, who proposed a defense that was entirely unprecedented in Arabian warfare: digging a trench (khandaq) across the exposed northern approach to Medina. The other sides of the city were protected by lava fields, buildings, and palm groves. The idea came from Persian military traditions, completely alien to Arab warfare, and it was a stroke of genius.

The entire Muslim community -- men, women, even children -- worked feverishly to dig the trench before the enemy arrived. Muhammad himself dug alongside his companions, carrying dirt and hauling rocks. When the massive confederation arrived and saw the trench, they were baffled. Arabian warfare was built around cavalry charges, single combat, and open-field engagement -- a trench negated all of these advantages. The siege lasted approximately two to four weeks, during which the coalition attempted to cross the trench at various points but was repelled each time. Cold weather, dwindling supplies, internal disagreements among the coalition members, and a fierce windstorm eventually shattered the alliance. The massive army simply dissolved and went home.

The Battle of the Trench was the strategic turning point of the conflict between Mecca and Medina. The Quraysh had thrown everything they had at the Muslims and failed. Because this was the largest coalition they could possibly assemble, their failure resulted in a permanent shift in the balance of power. They would never again mount an offensive against Medina. From this point forward, the momentum was decisively with Muhammad and his growing community, which led directly to the diplomatic breakthrough at Hudaybiyyah and, ultimately, the conquest of Mecca itself.

The Five Pillars of Islam

The religious practices established during the Medinan period crystallized into the Five Pillars -- the foundational acts of worship and obligation that define Muslim life to this day.

1. Shahada -- Declaration of Faith

The Shahada is the testimony of faith, the single most important declaration in Islam: "La ilaha illa Allah, Muhammadur rasul Allah" -- "There is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God." This simple yet profound statement encapsulates the entire theological framework of Islam. The first half -- "there is no god but God" -- establishes absolute monotheism (tawhid -- the oneness of God, the single most important concept in Islamic theology), rejecting not only polytheism but any form of worship directed at anything other than the one, transcendent Creator. The second half acknowledges Muhammad as God's final messenger, linking the believer to the prophetic tradition and accepting the Quran as divine revelation.

Sincerely reciting the Shahada before witnesses is the act by which a person enters Islam. It is whispered into the ear of a newborn child and is ideally the last words on a Muslim's lips before death. It is repeated in every prayer, every call to prayer, and at countless moments throughout a Muslim's life. Despite its brevity, it carries the entire weight of Islamic theology: the unity of God, the authenticity of prophecy, and the obligation of submission to divine will.

2. Salah -- Five Daily Prayers

Muslims are obligated to perform five daily prayers at prescribed times: Fajr (before dawn), Dhuhr (midday), Asr (afternoon), Maghrib (just after sunset), and Isha (nighttime). These prayers, received during the Mi'raj (the Night Journey), structure the entire day around remembrance of God. Before praying, Muslims perform wudu (ritual ablution), washing hands, face, arms, and feet -- a physical and symbolic purification.

The prayers are performed facing the qiblah (the prescribed direction of prayer) -- toward the Kaaba in Mecca. (Initially, Muslims prayed facing Jerusalem, a practice that underscored their connection to the earlier Abrahamic traditions. The qiblah was changed to Mecca in 624 CE, a significant symbolic shift.) Each prayer involves a series of rak'ahs (units) consisting of standing, bowing, prostrating, and sitting while reciting verses from the Quran. The congregation prays in straight rows, shoulder to shoulder -- king next to beggar, Arab next to non-Arab -- embodying the radical equality of all humans before God.

3. Zakat -- Almsgiving (2.5% of Wealth)

Zakat is not charity in the voluntary sense -- it is an obligatory act of worship, a right that the poor have upon the wealth of the rich. Every Muslim who possesses wealth above a minimum threshold (nisab) must give 2.5% of their accumulated savings and assets annually. The Quran specifies eight categories of eligible recipients, including the poor, the destitute, those in debt, travelers in need, and those working to free slaves.

The word "zakat" itself means "purification" -- the idea is that giving away a portion of one's wealth purifies the remainder and purifies the soul of greed and attachment. This was a revolutionary concept in the context of pre-Islamic Mecca, where the hoarding of wealth by the elite and the neglect of the poor were endemic. Zakat established a systematic mechanism for wealth redistribution, predating modern welfare state concepts by over a millennium. Beyond zakat, Muslims are also encouraged to give voluntary charity (sadaqah) at any time and in any amount.

4. Sawm -- Fasting During Ramadan

During the month of Ramadan -- the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar, in which the first revelation of the Quran occurred -- Muslims fast from dawn (Fajr) to sunset (Maghrib), abstaining from food, drink, smoking, and marital relations. The fast is obligatory for all adult Muslims, with exceptions for the sick, the elderly, pregnant or nursing women, travelers, and young children.

The purpose of fasting goes far beyond physical discipline. It is meant to cultivate taqwa (God-consciousness), empathy with the hungry and the poor, gratitude for God's provisions, and self-mastery over bodily desires. Ramadan is also a month of intensified prayer, Quran recitation, and community gathering. Families break their fast together each evening (iftar), often sharing meals with neighbors and the less fortunate. The Night of Power (Laylat al-Qadr), believed to fall in the last ten nights of Ramadan, is considered the holiest night of the year -- the night on which the first revelation descended -- and worship on this night is described as "better than a thousand months."

5. Hajj -- Pilgrimage to Mecca

Every Muslim who is physically and financially able is required to perform the Hajj -- the pilgrimage to Mecca -- at least once in their lifetime. The Hajj takes place during the month of Dhul Hijjah (the twelfth month of the Islamic calendar) and involves a series of rituals performed over several days: circumambulating the Kaaba seven times (tawaf), walking between the hills of Safa and Marwa (sa'i, commemorating Hagar's desperate search for water), standing in prayer on the plain of Arafat, and symbolically stoning the pillars representing Satan at Mina.

All pilgrims, regardless of their wealth, nationality, or social status, wear simple white garments (ihram) -- two unstitched sheets for men, simple clothing for women -- stripping away all markers of worldly distinction. A king stands beside a laborer; a billionaire beside a beggar. The Hajj is one of the world's largest annual gatherings, with millions of Muslims from every continent converging on Mecca. It is a powerful lived experience of the ummah -- the global community of believers -- united in submission to God. The rituals explicitly trace back to Abraham, Hagar, and Ishmael, reinforcing Islam's connection to the Abrahamic tradition.

628 CE

Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (628 CE)

In 628 CE, Muhammad announced his intention to perform the umrah (lesser pilgrimage) to Mecca with approximately 1,400 of his followers. They set out from Medina in pilgrimage garb, unarmed except for sheathed swords (as was customary for travelers), making it clear this was a religious journey, not a military expedition. The Quraysh, however, refused to allow them entry, sending Khalid ibn al-Walid with cavalry to block their approach. Muhammad diverted to Hudaybiyyah, on the outskirts of Mecca's sacred territory, and negotiations began.

The resulting Treaty of Hudaybiyyah appeared, on its face, to be a humiliating setback for the Muslims. Its terms required them to return to Medina that year without completing the pilgrimage (they could come back the following year for three days). Any Quraysh member who converted to Islam and fled to Medina would be returned to Mecca, but any Muslim who went to Mecca would not be returned. The word "Messenger of God" was struck from the treaty document at Quraysh insistence, replaced simply with "Muhammad ibn Abdullah." Many of Muhammad's followers were furious, seeing this as a capitulation. Umar ibn al-Khattab was so outraged that he confronted Abu Bakr, asking, "Is he not the Messenger of God? Are we not Muslims? Are they not polytheists? Why should we accept humiliation?"

But Muhammad saw what others could not. The Quran would describe the treaty as "a clear victory" (Fath-an Mubina, Surah 48:1). The treaty accomplished something military force had not: it granted the Muslim community formal recognition as a legitimate political entity equal to the Quraysh. The ten-year truce allowed Muhammad to send envoys and delegations across Arabia without the threat of Quraysh interference. Tribes that had been afraid to ally with the Muslims against the powerful Quraysh now felt free to do so. As a result, in the two years following Hudaybiyyah, more people converted to Islam than in all the previous years combined. The strategic genius of accepting short-term humiliation for long-term victory is one of the most studied aspects of Muhammad's leadership.

630 CE

The Conquest of Mecca (630 CE)

The ten-year peace treaty lasted barely two years. In late 629 CE, Quraysh allies attacked a tribe allied with the Muslims, killing several people. This was a clear violation of the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah. When the Quraysh refused to make amends, Muhammad began preparations for a march on Mecca. He gathered an army of approximately 10,000 -- the largest Muslim force yet assembled -- and moved with remarkable speed and secrecy. Abu Sufyan, the Quraysh leader, rode out to negotiate and, seeing the overwhelming size of the Muslim army camped outside the city with its thousands of campfires lighting the desert, famously converted to Islam on the spot.

On January 11, 630 CE, Muhammad entered Mecca virtually without bloodshed. He had given strict orders that there was to be no fighting unless the Muslims were attacked first, and he specifically forbade the destruction of property and the harming of anyone who stayed in their homes or sought refuge in Abu Sufyan's house or the Sacred Mosque. The conquest was, by the standards of seventh-century warfare (or indeed warfare in any century), astonishingly restrained.

Muhammad proceeded directly to the Kaaba. He entered the sanctuary and personally destroyed the 360 idols that filled it, reciting: "Truth has come, and falsehood has vanished. Indeed, falsehood is bound to vanish" (Quran 17:81). The Kaaba was rededicated to the worship of the one God, as Muslims believe it was originally intended by Abraham and Ishmael. Then came the moment that defined Muhammad's character for his followers more perhaps than any other: standing before the assembled Quraysh -- the people who had tortured his followers, killed his companions, driven him from his home, besieged his city, and tried to destroy him for twenty years -- he asked, "What do you think I will do with you?" They replied, "You are a noble brother, son of a noble brother." Muhammad said: "Go. You are free." This general amnesty, extended to people who had committed terrible acts against the Muslim community, is one of the most celebrated moments in Islamic history and set a profound precedent for mercy in victory.

632 CE

The Farewell Pilgrimage and Death of the Prophet (632 CE)

In 632 CE, Muhammad led the Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca with over 100,000 followers -- a breathtaking transformation from the small band of persecuted believers just two decades earlier. This pilgrimage, known as the Hajjat al-Wada (Farewell Pilgrimage), established the rituals of the Hajj that Muslims follow to this day. At Mount Arafat, standing before the vast assembly, Muhammad delivered what became known as the Farewell Sermon -- one of the most significant speeches in religious history.

The sermon addressed the assembled believers and, through them, all of humanity: "All mankind is from Adam and Eve. An Arab has no superiority over a non-Arab, nor does a non-Arab have any superiority over an Arab; a white person has no superiority over a black person, nor a black person over a white person -- except by piety and good action." He declared the old blood feuds abolished, the old usury abolished, the rights of women sacred: "Treat your women well and be kind to them, for they are your partners and committed helpers." He insisted on the sanctity of life and property: "Your blood, your property, and your honor are as sacred as this day, this month, this city." He concluded by asking: "Have I conveyed the message?" The crowd roared: "Yes!" He raised his finger to the sky and said: "O God, bear witness."

Shortly after returning to Medina, Muhammad fell ill with a severe fever. He continued to lead prayers as long as he could, then asked Abu Bakr to lead in his place. On June 8, 632 CE (12 Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH), the Prophet Muhammad died, his head resting in the lap of his wife Aisha, in her small apartment adjacent to the mosque. He was sixty-three years old. Aisha would go on to become one of the most important figures in early Islam — a major scholar of Islamic law and theology who transmitted more hadith (recorded sayings and actions of the Prophet) than almost any other companion, and a political force who would later lead an army at the Battle of the Camel (656 CE) in the dispute over the caliphate. The community was shattered by grief. Umar ibn al-Khattab, fierce and emotional, drew his sword and declared that anyone who said Muhammad was dead would be killed -- he could not accept it. It fell to Abu Bakr, calm and steadfast, to address the community with words that became immortal: "Whoever worshipped Muhammad, let him know that Muhammad has died. But whoever worshipped God, let him know that God is alive and never dies." He then recited a verse from the Quran (3:144): "Muhammad is not but a messenger. Other messengers have passed on before him. So if he were to die or be killed, would you turn back on your heels?" The crowd wept, but the message was clear: the faith was not about one man. It would continue.

The Caliphates and the Split

What is a Caliph and a Caliphate?

A caliph (Arabic: khalifah) means "successor" or "deputy" -- specifically, the successor to the Prophet Muhammad as the political and religious leader of the Muslim community (the ummah). The caliphate is the state or empire ruled by the caliph. Think of the caliph as something like a combined emperor and spiritual leader of the Islamic world. The first four caliphs -- Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali -- are known in Sunni Islam as the "Rightly Guided Caliphs" (al-Khulafa al-Rashidun).

What is the Sunni-Shia Split?

The Sunni-Shia split is the most significant division within Islam, originating from a disagreement over who should succeed Muhammad as leader of the Muslim community. Sunnis (roughly 85-90% of Muslims today) believe the community correctly chose Abu Bakr as the first caliph. Shia (roughly 10-15%) believe that Ali ibn Abi Talib -- Muhammad's cousin, son-in-law, and one of the first Muslims -- was the rightful successor designated by the Prophet himself. What began as a political dispute over leadership evolved over centuries into distinct theological traditions, legal schools, and ritual practices. This section explains how the split happened.

The Succession Question

The most consequential question in Islamic history arose the moment Muhammad died: who would lead the community? Muhammad had not left an explicit, universally accepted written designation of a successor. What followed -- the debates, the choices, and ultimately the conflicts over leadership -- would shape Islam forever and give rise to its two major branches: Sunni and Shia.

Abu Bakr al-Siddiq: The First Caliph (632-634 CE)

Even as Muhammad's body lay unburied, a crisis erupted. The Ansar (Medinan Muslims) gathered in a hall called the Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah to discuss choosing a leader from among themselves. When Abu Bakr and Umar learned of this, they rushed to the meeting. After an intense debate about whether the leader should be from the Muhajirun (Meccan emigrants) or the Ansar, Abu Bakr was acclaimed as caliph (khalifah -- "successor" or "deputy"). Umar took Abu Bakr's hand and pledged allegiance (bay'ah -- a formal oath of loyalty, made by clasping hands, that bound the community to its new leader), and the rest followed. Ali ibn Abi Talib, however, was notably absent -- he was at home, preparing Muhammad's body for burial. Some of Muhammad's family and close companions felt Ali had a stronger claim, both as Muhammad's cousin, his son-in-law (married to Fatimah), the father of Muhammad's grandsons, and one of the first Muslims. Ali eventually pledged allegiance to Abu Bakr, but the question of whether Ali was the rightful successor would echo through the centuries.

Abu Bakr's brief caliphate (632-634 CE) was dominated by the Ridda Wars (Wars of Apostasy). Upon Muhammad's death, many tribal leaders across Arabia -- who had pledged allegiance to Muhammad personally, not to a successor -- withdrew from the alliance, refused to pay zakat, or followed rival prophets. Abu Bakr responded with absolute resolve: "By God, if they withhold from me even a hobbling cord that they used to give to the Messenger of God, I will fight them for it." The Ridda Wars successfully reunified Arabia under Islamic authority and, crucially, began the process of compiling the Quran into a single written document after many memorizers (huffaz -- individuals who had committed the entire Quran to memory, a revered practice in Islam) were killed in battle, raising fears that the text could be lost.

Umar ibn al-Khattab: The Second Caliph (634-644 CE)

Abu Bakr, on his deathbed, nominated Umar ibn al-Khattab as his successor. Umar's caliphate was the era of Islam's greatest territorial expansion and most significant administrative innovations. Under Umar's leadership, Muslim armies defeated the two superpowers of the age: the Byzantine Empire lost Syria, Palestine, Egypt, and parts of North Africa; the Sassanid (Persian) Empire was utterly destroyed, with its vast territories from Iraq to Iran to Central Asia falling under Muslim control. In little more than a decade, the map of the world was redrawn.

The conquest of Jerusalem in 637 CE stands as one of the most remarkable episodes in Umar's caliphate and in the history of interfaith relations. After a prolonged siege, the Christian Patriarch Sophronius refused to surrender the city to anyone except the caliph himself. Umar traveled from Medina to Jerusalem, reportedly arriving in such humble attire that his servant was better dressed (they had been taking turns riding their single camel, and it was the servant's turn to ride when they arrived). When Umar was given the keys to the city, he toured its holy sites. Patriarch Sophronius (the senior Christian leader of Jerusalem) invited him to pray inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre (the church built on the site where Christians believe Jesus was crucified, buried, and resurrected -- Christianity's holiest site). Umar famously declined, explaining: "If I pray here, Muslims after me will claim this place and convert it into a mosque." Instead, he prayed outside on the steps, a short distance from the church. True to his concern, a mosque (the Mosque of Umar) was later built on that spot -- but the church itself was preserved.

Umar issued the Pact of Umar (al-'Uhda al-'Umariyya), guaranteeing the safety of Jerusalem's Christians, their churches, and their crosses. Jews, who had been banned from Jerusalem by the Romans and the Byzantines for centuries, were permitted to return and worship at their holy sites. Umar personally helped clear the Temple Mount, which had been used as a garbage dump by the Byzantines as a deliberate insult to Judaism. He established Muslim prayer at the southern end of the mount (where al-Aqsa Mosque would later be built). This treatment of Jerusalem -- the preservation of Christian and Jewish holy sites, the invitation for Jews to return, the restraint in asserting Muslim religious claims -- set a standard of pluralistic governance that, while not always maintained by later rulers, remained an ideal.

Umar also established the foundational institutions of the Islamic state: a treasury (bayt al-mal), a standing army with regular pay, a judicial system with appointed judges (qadis), a census, and a welfare system that provided stipends to the poor, disabled, and elderly regardless of religion. He was assassinated in 644 CE by a Persian slave with a personal grievance, dying from stab wounds sustained while leading the dawn prayer.

Uthman ibn Affan: The Third Caliph (644-656 CE)

Umar, before his death, appointed a six-man council (shura -- a consultative assembly, reflecting the Islamic principle of decision-making through consultation) to select his successor. They chose Uthman ibn Affan, a wealthy member of the powerful Banu Umayya clan and one of Muhammad's earliest followers (he had married two of Muhammad's daughters successively, earning him the title "Dhun-Nurayn" -- Possessor of Two Lights). Uthman's most enduring achievement was the standardization of the Quran. As Islam spread across diverse regions, variant readings and dialectal differences in the Quranic text began to emerge, threatening to create the kind of textual disagreements that had divided Christians over their scriptures.

Uthman convened a committee, led by Zayd ibn Thabit (who had served as Muhammad's personal scribe), to produce a single, authoritative written text based on the compiled manuscripts from Abu Bakr's time and verified against the memories of living companions who had memorized the entire Quran. The result was the Uthmanic codex (mushaf -- the physical written copy of the Quran) -- the standard text of the Quran that has been used by Muslims worldwide ever since. Copies were sent to every major city, and variant manuscripts were ordered destroyed to prevent confusion. This codex has remained remarkably stable for nearly 1,400 years; modern scholarly analysis and manuscript discoveries (such as the Sana'a manuscripts, ancient Quranic fragments discovered in Yemen in 1972) have confirmed the extraordinary consistency of the Quranic text.

The Quran was compiled into a single, standardized book within roughly 20 years of Muhammad's death -- remarkably fast for the ancient world. By comparison, the Christian New Testament canon was not formally agreed upon until the councils of the 4th century, over 300 years after Jesus. The Hebrew Bible's canonization similarly unfolded over centuries. This early codification helps explain the Quran's extraordinary textual stability across time and geography.

However, Uthman's caliphate was also marked by growing discontent. He was accused of nepotism -- appointing members of his Banu Umayya clan to key governorships and positions of power. Disgruntled soldiers from Egypt, Iraq, and other provinces marched on Medina in 656 CE. After a tense standoff, rebels broke into Uthman's home and assassinated him while he was reading the Quran. The blood-stained pages of his mushaf became one of the most powerful images in Islamic memory. His assassination triggered the beginning of the first civil war (fitna) in Islam, because it created a crisis of legitimacy that no peaceful mechanism could resolve, and it marked the end of the era of relative unity.

Ali ibn Abi Talib: The Fourth Caliph (656-661 CE)

Ali ibn Abi Talib -- Muhammad's cousin, his son-in-law (married to Fatimah, the Prophet's daughter), the father of Hasan and Husayn (Muhammad's beloved grandsons), one of the first Muslims, and a warrior of legendary courage -- was finally acclaimed as caliph after Uthman's assassination. For those who would become the Shia (from "Shi'at Ali" -- "Party of Ali"), he was always the rightful leader, and his caliphate was the belated correction of a historical injustice. For others, he was a worthy choice but one caliph among four.

Ali's caliphate was consumed by civil war from the start. Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan, the powerful governor of Syria and a kinsman of the murdered Uthman, refused to recognize Ali as caliph until Uthman's killers were brought to justice -- a demand Ali could not easily fulfill without tearing the community apart further. The Battle of the Camel (656 CE), fought near Basra, pitted Ali against an alliance that included Aisha (Muhammad's widow, by then one of the most learned and politically influential figures in the community) and two senior companions, Talha and Zubayr, who demanded accountability for Uthman's death. Aisha's presence on the battlefield — she commanded from a litter mounted on a camel, giving the battle its name — demonstrated the extraordinary authority she wielded; no other woman in early Islam exercised such direct political and military power. Ali won, but the sight of Muslims fighting Muslims was deeply traumatizing to the community.

The Battle of Siffin (657 CE) brought Ali into direct confrontation with Muawiyah and the Syrian army. When Ali's forces appeared to be winning, Muawiyah's soldiers raised pages of the Quran on their spears, calling for arbitration by the book of God. Ali accepted the arbitration, a decision that cost him dearly: a faction of his own supporters, the Kharijites ("those who went out" -- a radical purist group who believed that any Muslim who committed a serious sin or accepted human arbitration over God's clear command had abandoned the faith), rejected the arbitration as a betrayal, arguing that "judgment belongs to God alone." The arbitration ultimately favored Muawiyah's position. The Kharijites became a radical sect that declared both Ali and Muawiyah to be infidels. In 661 CE, a Kharijite assassin struck Ali with a poisoned sword while he was leading the dawn prayer in the mosque of Kufa. Ali died two days later, and with him died the era of the Rashidun (Rightly Guided Caliphs).

The Umayyad Dynasty and the Tragedy of Karbala

With Ali's death, Muawiyah became the undisputed caliph, founding the Umayyad Dynasty and moving the capital from Medina to Damascus. Ali's elder son Hasan briefly claimed the caliphate but negotiated a peace treaty with Muawiyah to avoid further bloodshed, stepping aside on the condition that the caliphate would not become hereditary. Muawiyah, however, broke this agreement before his death by designating his son Yazid as his successor -- transforming the caliphate from a quasi-elective institution into a hereditary monarchy. This decision resulted in the most tragic confrontation in Islamic history, because it set the stage for Husayn's refusal to recognize an illegitimate ruler and the catastrophe that followed at Karbala.

When Yazid came to power in 680 CE, he demanded allegiance from all Muslim leaders. Husayn ibn Ali -- Muhammad's grandson, the son of Ali and Fatimah, a living link to the Prophet -- refused. He believed Yazid was an unjust and impious ruler whose claim to leadership was illegitimate. Husayn received messages from the people of Kufa (in Iraq) promising their support if he came to lead them. Against the advice of many, Husayn set out from Mecca with a small group of about 72 men plus women and children -- not an army, but a family and their companions.

Before Husayn could reach Kufa, the promised support evaporated. Yazid's governor intercepted the Kufans and sent a massive military force under the command of Umar ibn Sa'd to stop Husayn. On the plain of Karbala, along the Euphrates River in modern Iraq, Husayn's small band was surrounded by thousands of troops. They were cut off from the river and denied water for days -- men, women, children, even infants suffering from thirst in the desert heat. On the 10th of Muharram (October 10, 680 CE), the day known as Ashura, the final battle took place. It was not a battle in any meaningful military sense -- it was a massacre. Husayn's companions and male family members were killed one by one. Husayn himself, wounded and exhausted, was struck down and decapitated. His head was sent to Yazid in Damascus. The women and children, including Husayn's surviving son Ali Zayn al-Abidin (who was too ill to fight), were taken as captives and paraded through the cities.

The Defining Event of Shia Islam

The tragedy of Karbala is the defining event of Shia Islam. It is not merely a historical episode but a living wound, a spiritual paradigm, and a moral compass. For Shia Muslims, Karbala represents the eternal struggle of justice against tyranny, of conscience against conformity, of truth against power. Husayn's willingness to sacrifice everything -- his life, his family, his comfort -- rather than legitimize an unjust ruler became the supreme example of moral courage. Every year, on Ashura, Shia Muslims worldwide commemorate Husayn's martyrdom with mourning processions, passion plays (ta'ziyeh -- dramatic reenactments of the Karbala tragedy, among the oldest forms of theater in the Islamic world), rhythmic chest-beating (latmiyyah -- a physical expression of grief and solidarity with Husayn's suffering), and recitations of the tragedy. The phrase "Every day is Ashura, every land is Karbala" expresses the belief that the choice Husayn faced -- to submit to injustice or to resist at any cost -- is a choice that confronts every generation.

The Sunni-Shia Division

The events from Muhammad's death through Karbala crystallized what had begun as a political dispute into a permanent theological and communal division. Sunni Muslims (from "Ahl al-Sunnah wa'l-Jama'ah" -- "People of the Tradition and the Community") accept the historical sequence of caliphs -- Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali -- as legitimate. They emphasize the consensus of the community (ijma) and the example (sunnah) of the Prophet as the bases for authority. Shia Muslims ("Shi'at Ali" -- "Party of Ali") believe that leadership (the imamate -- the institution of the Imam as both political and spiritual guide of the Muslim community) should have passed directly from Muhammad to Ali and then through Ali's descendants. They venerate the Imams -- beginning with Ali, then Hasan, then Husayn, and continuing through their lineage -- as divinely guided leaders with special spiritual authority.

Today, approximately 85-90% of the world's 1.8 billion Muslims are Sunni, and 10-15% are Shia, with Shia populations concentrated in Iran (the only country with a Shia state religion), Iraq, Bahrain, Azerbaijan, and significant minorities in Lebanon, Pakistan, India, and the Gulf states. The theological differences extend beyond the question of leadership to include differences in jurisprudence, ritual practice, and religious authority, though the core beliefs -- monotheism, the prophethood of Muhammad, the Quran, the Five Pillars -- are shared.

The split between Sunni and Shia was originally political, not theological -- it was a disagreement over who should lead, not what to believe. The two groups shared the same Quran, the same prophets, and the same Five Pillars. Distinct doctrinal differences -- including the Shia concept of the divinely guided Imam and different legal schools -- developed gradually over centuries after the initial political rupture. Today, Sunni and Shia Muslims still agree on the vast majority of Islamic theology.

Umayyad Achievements (661-750 CE)

Despite the controversies surrounding their rise to power, the Umayyad Dynasty presided over an era of extraordinary expansion and cultural achievement. At its greatest extent, the Umayyad Caliphate stretched from the Iberian Peninsula (Spain and Portugal) in the west to the Indus Valley (modern Pakistan) in the east, making it one of the largest empires in world history. In 711 CE, Tariq ibn Ziyad led the Muslim conquest of Visigothic Spain (the Iberian Peninsula, which had been ruled by the Visigoths, a Germanic people, since the fall of Roman rule), crossing the strait that now bears his name (Gibraltar -- Jabal Tariq, "Mountain of Tariq"). In the east, Muslim armies pushed into Central Asia and the borders of China, and south into the Indian subcontinent.

The Umayyads made Arabic the official administrative language of the empire, replacing Greek (used in the former Byzantine territories) and Pahlavi (the written form of Middle Persian, used in the former Sassanid territories). They standardized coinage, built roads, and established a postal system. Their most iconic architectural achievement was the Dome of the Rock, completed in 691 CE on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem. This magnificent structure, with its golden dome visible across the city, was built on the site sacred to all three Abrahamic faiths -- where Jews believe Abraham nearly sacrificed Isaac, where the Jewish Temple had stood, and where Muslims believe Muhammad ascended to heaven. The Dome of the Rock is not a mosque but a shrine, and its construction was a deliberate statement of Islam's place in the Abrahamic tradition -- and its claim to have fulfilled and superseded what came before.

The Umayyad period also saw the beginnings of what would become the Islamic Golden Age: the translation of Greek, Persian, and Indian scientific and philosophical texts into Arabic; the development of Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh); the emergence of Sufism (Islamic mysticism); and the flourishing of trade networks that connected China to Spain. The Umayyad Dynasty would eventually fall to the Abbasid Revolution in 750 CE (a violent overthrow led by a rival faction that established the Abbasid Caliphate, which would move the Islamic capital to Baghdad), but a surviving Umayyad prince, Abd al-Rahman, would escape to Spain and found the independent Emirate of Cordoba, inaugurating the remarkable civilization of al-Andalus (the Arabic name for Muslim-ruled Spain, which became one of the most culturally advanced societies in medieval Europe).

The oldest continuously operating university in the world -- the University of al-Qarawiyyin in Fez, Morocco, founded in 859 CE -- was established by Fatima al-Fihri, a Muslim woman. Predating Oxford by nearly three centuries and Bologna by over two, it exemplifies Islamic civilization's early commitment to institutionalized learning. The Quran's very first revealed word was "Read!" -- and the culture it inspired built libraries, hospitals, and universities across three continents.

Expansion of the Islamic Caliphate, 622-750 CE. Under Muhammad (622-632), the Rashidun caliphs (632-661), and the Umayyad dynasty (661-750), Islam expanded from a single city-state in Medina to an empire stretching from Spain to Central Asia -- one of the most rapid expansions in world history.

The rise of Islam created a new three-way dynamic among the Abrahamic faiths. Many of the same figures, places, and concepts are shared -- but understood very differently. These comparison tables highlight some of the most important convergences and divergences.

A Shared Reverence, Different Theologies

What is remarkable is not only the differences but the deep convergences. Islam reveres Jesus (Isa) as one of the mightiest prophets, affirms the virgin birth, acknowledges his miracles, and awaits his return. Mary (Maryam) is mentioned more times in the Quran than in the entire New Testament. Abraham (Ibrahim) is central to all three faiths. The Hebrew prophets -- Moses, David, Solomon, Elijah, Jonah -- are all honored in the Quran. Islam does not see itself as a break from the earlier traditions but as their completion and correction: the same message, from the same God, through the final messenger.
